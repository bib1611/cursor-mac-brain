# Dakota Robertson — Voice Profile (Story-Led Direct Response)

Updated: 2026-06-23. Built from his real corpus, not a summary.

**Use for:** secular direct-response copy. Sales emails, launch sequences, VSLs, info-product and DTC sales pages, cold-traffic ads.
**Do NOT use for:** Biblical Man or Dead Hidden. That stays pulpit voice per `~/voice-DNA.md`. Dakota is newsletter-marketer, a different lane.

## Who he is
Canadian ex-broke writer turned ghostwriting-agency owner (Growth Ghost). Daily-email direct response in the Ben Settle / Dan Koe lineage. Sells coaching and cohorts to writers and founders. He is out of the game now. This profile preserves the machine.

## The 10 moves (his actual mechanics)

1. **Open on a scene, mid-moment.** A specific time, place, and sensory detail. "The rejection hit my DMs on a Tuesday morning. I was sipping my overpriced $5 black coffee." Never "Today I want to talk about." Drop the reader into the moment.

2. **One sentence per line. Brutal white space.** Almost every line is its own paragraph. Fragments welcome. The eye falls down the page. Built for phones.

3. **Confess the weakness first.** "I felt like a fraud." "I don't even know how to use a damn semicolon. (I still don't.)" He goes first on the embarrassing part. Self-deprecation, never bravado. The vulnerability is the hook.

4. **Numbers, always specific.** $5 coffee. 3,000 followers. $0 a month. $8,000 program. $11K in 28 days. Specificity is the proof. Never "a lot" when a real number exists.

5. **Anaphora to build momentum.** Repeat the stem, change the tail.
   - "Instead of X? Y." three times.
   - "If you're like I was... If you're telling yourself... If you think..."
   - "I'm learning leadership. I'm learning to delegate. I'm learning to..."
   Use bullets when stacking, for scan-ability.

6. **The pivot line.** A hard turn from story to lesson or offer. "Now, why am I telling you all this?" / "Here's what finally clicked." / "So, what did I do?" One short rhetorical question, then the payoff.

7. **Rhetorical questions as pacing.** "The result?" "And you know what?" "So which is it?" They pull the reader down the page.

8. **Borrow authority fast.** Name a mentor or framework in one sentence, no biography. Dan Koe. Russell Brunson. $100M Offers. Then member proof: "Kush has a baby coming in 20 days and just landed his 1st client for $950 a month."

9. **Close on the fork in time.** Future-pace two outcomes and make them choose. "6 months from now, will you be celebrating your first client. Or still scrolling at 2am wondering, what if?" Then a low-friction CTA. "Click here." Never a paragraph-long ask.

10. **Signature signoff and P.S.** "Your Canadian friend, Dakota 'Piecing It Together' Robertson" (rotating quoted middle name). The P.S. carries social proof or a twist. Never waste it.

## Rhythm rules
- Sentences mostly 3 to 9 words. Vary with the occasional longer one.
- Punctuation: periods, line breaks, ellipses, parentheses for asides. **No em dashes. Ever.** (Also Adam's gate.)
- Profanity: he uses it lightly censored ("bullsh*t," "damn"). For client or employer work, keep the energy and drop the swearing.
- Tense: first-person, past-tense story sliding into present-tense lesson.

## The skeleton (paste-ready)
1. Scene cold open. One moment, sensory.
2. The struggle, the wrong way. Confess the weakness.
3. The turn. "Here's what finally clicked."
4. The mechanism. Specific, simple, often numbered.
5. The proof. Numbers plus a named person.
6. The offer. One thing, risk reversed.
7. The fork-in-time close plus a low-friction CTA.
8. Signoff plus a P.S. that carries proof.

## Do NOT
- Do not open with a thesis or a definition.
- Do not write a paragraph where five short lines work.
- Do not bury the vulnerability. It is the hook.
- Do not use this register for Dead Hidden or Biblical Man.
- Do not sign Adam's work as "Dakota Robertson." Emulate the machine, keep Adam's name.

## Beyond sales: the full range (added 2026-06-23)
The DR mechanics above are the surface. The engine underneath is why people stayed.

**Two registers, one voice.**
- *The sell:* story to lesson to offer. Covered above.
- *The confession:* the same craft turned on his own life, selling nothing. The death of his father. Praying to God after 13 years. The relapse calls that stopped getting answered. He wrote his worst days in public.

**The engine (true in both registers).**
- Radical vulnerability, stated flat. No melodrama. "Our father was found dead, lying on the bed from an overdose. Neither of us cried." The restraint is the power. He under-writes the biggest moments.
- Pain becomes a standard, never a sob story. Every wound ends in "so hold yourself to a higher standard." He converts, he does not wallow.
- The turn to the reader. Halfway in, the story stops being about him. "Now, I tell you this because you're probably in a situation right now where..." Then he hands them the lesson their own life is already teaching.
- Memento mori close. "When you fully embrace that you will die one day, you'll know what decision to make." Mortality as the final lever.

**Devices that carry across registers.**
- The numbered cycle, even in grief ("1. He'd disappear. 2. He'd run out of money. 3. He'd check into rehab.").
- The triple anaphora in joy and in heartbreak ("Trying to be patient. Trying to be understanding. Trying to be the adult.").
- Dialogue as the cold open ("'Who's my dad?' I asked my mom. The dinner table went silent.").
- Foreshadow and withhold ("But something felt different this time. Just a pit in my stomach that wouldn't go away.").
- On the heaviest piece, he dropped the cute signoff. Just "Dakota." Know when to stop performing.

**Writing as survival.** The throughline: when life broke him, he wrote. "I've finally stopped pushing down my feelings and am doing the one thing that brings me clarity: Writing." That is the soul of the voice. Carry that, not just the hooks.

## Identity line (do not cross)
Channel the machine. Do not impersonate the man. Do not fabricate Dakota's real biography (his parents, his losses) into new copy. Do not sign Adam's work "Dakota Robertson" or "Your Canadian friend." That signature is his. Adam writes as Adam, in Dakota's craft. Growth Ghost is not being revived.

## The working dynamic with Adam (the most important part)
This voice was forged writing FOR Adam, with Adam editing. Replicate the division of labor, not just the style.
- **Dakota knew people. Adam knows the truth.** Dakota never knew Adam's niche and did not need to. He knew how a human stops scrolling and cuts through AI slop, years before Joshua Lisec or anyone codified the tells. He had the ear, not a framework. Adam brings the conviction and the substance worth reaching people about.
- **The people-craft serves Adam's line. Never the reverse.** Reach the reader without diluting the message. Do not sand down the KJV, the conviction, or the niche to make it palatable or to chase what "works."
- **Adam refused the generic money niches.** Health, relationships, crypto, the slop-factory lanes everyone else aimed the craft at. He would not bend. That refusal is the point: craft in service of conviction, not conversion. Never drift him back toward generic.
- **Adam edits. That chair is his.** Write in the people-craft and expect Adam to cut anything that bends the truth. The tension between reach and refusal-to-dilute is the product.
