# the man who feels nothing

Today they hand you a card.

Maybe a mug. Maybe socks. Maybe a tie you wear twice a year.

You smile. You mean it.

And under the smile, nothing.

I know that man. I was that man.

Provider. Fixer. The one who keeps the lights on and the truck running. Bills paid. Roof solid. Freezer full.

Dead behind the eyes.

## the verse nobody reads at the breakfast table

Because thou sayest, I am rich, and increased with goods, and have need of nothing; and knowest not that thou art wretched, and miserable, and poor, and blind, and naked. Revelation 3:17.

He is not talking to the broke man.

He is talking to the man who has everything.

The one who needs nothing. The one who can sit at his own table, surrounded by his own children, and feel like he is watching it through glass.

You hear them. You answer them. You pay for them.

You cannot reach them.

## how the numbness comes

It does not arrive in a day.

It comes one swallowed thing at a time.

A hard week. A screen at night instead of a prayer. A son who needed you and got a tired no. You told yourself you would make it up later.

Later kept moving.

The provider learns to function without feeling. It is efficient. It pays the mortgage. It also turns a father into a roof and a wallet and a ride to practice.

Present in the house. Gone from the home.

And the worst part is you can fake it for years. Nobody files a complaint against a man who does everything right and loves nothing.

## the door you forgot was there

Here is what wrecks me about Laodicea.

God calls them blind and naked. Then He does not walk away.

Behold, I stand at the door, and knock: if any man hear my voice, and open the door, I will come in to him, and will sup with him, and he with me. Revelation 3:20.

That knock is for the numb man.

The one who provides and cannot feel.

He is standing outside the very house you built, asking to be let back into your own heart.

You do not have to manufacture feeling. You cannot. The man who went numb cannot will himself warm.

You open the door. He comes in. He does the thing you stopped being able to do.

## before the card gets thrown out

That Father's Day card lands in the trash by July.

The man your kids actually need does not.

Stop reading. Go find the one in your house you have been watching through glass.

Put your hand on his shoulder. Or hers.

Say the thing you have felt and never said.

You are not too far gone. The One who knocks is still on the porch.

Open it.
