# you were in the room the whole time

You didn't leave.

That's the lie you tell yourself at night. You provided. You stayed. You came home.

Every single day.

But your son can't remember the last time you looked at him. Not glanced. Looked.

There's a difference and your boy knows it.

I drove a garbage truck for years. Then a train. I came through the door tired, and I called it love. The bills were paid. Nobody went hungry. I thought that was the whole job.

It wasn't.

A man can be home and gone at the same time. Body in the chair. Soul somewhere else. The phone. The work that follows you through the door. The screen that eats the hours you'll never get back.

The kids grow up next to a ghost who pays the rent.

My grandson Blake came back into the house this spring. NICU survivor. Fifty pounds of nothing once, now loud and alive in my hallway at six in the morning. And I caught myself one day reaching for the phone instead of the boy.

Sat there. Hand on the glass. He was right in front of me.

That's the thing nobody warns you about. The absence doesn't look like absence. It looks like a man at the table. It looks like a father at the game, watching the screen instead of the field.

You're there. And you're not.

The Bible doesn't say teach your children when it's convenient. It says, "thou shalt teach them diligently unto thy children, and shalt talk of them when thou sittest in thine house, and when thou walkest by the way, and when thou liest down, and when thou risest up."

Sitting. Walking. Lying down. Rising up.

That's not a sermon slot. That's the whole day. That's the small hours that don't feel holy while you're living them.

I used to think the war was out there. The job. The money. The grind.

The war is in the living room. And most of us are losing it without a fight, because we never showed up to swing.

Here's what I know now that cost me too much to learn.

The years don't announce themselves. There's no horn that blows. One day the house is loud. The next it's quiet, and you're standing in a room that used to hold a child, holding a phone, wondering where the time went.

It didn't go anywhere. You traded it. A scroll at a time.

"Lo, children are an heritage of the Lord." An heritage. Not an interruption.

You can fix this tonight. You don't need a program. You don't need a better man than the one you already are.

You need to put the glass down and look up.

Look at the kid in front of you. Ask him a question and wait for the whole answer. Sit on the floor. Be bored with him. Let the work wait one hour. It waited this long.

Presence is the gift. Not the mug. Not the tie. You.

Turn the heart of the father to the children. That's the prayer. That's the command. Do it before the room goes quiet.

You were in the room the whole time.

Be in it.
