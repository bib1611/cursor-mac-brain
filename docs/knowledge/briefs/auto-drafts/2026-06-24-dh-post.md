# the thing in the walls

Something lived in my house before I was born.

I never saw it. I felt it.

It moved through the men in my family like a draft through old wood. You couldn't point at it. But every room it touched got colder.

A reader told me this week his mother was beaten with extension cords. He said violence and drink were "the most reliable things in the household." Reliable. Sit with that word. The terror showed up on schedule. Like a tenant who pays rent.

That is what the old book is talking about.

"Visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children, and upon the children's children, unto the third and to the fourth generation."

Read it slow. It doesn't say the punishment. It says the iniquity. The bent itself travels. The appetite. The rage you didn't earn. The hunger that wakes up in you and wears your grandfather's face.

You think you chose it. You didn't.

It was waiting in the walls when they brought you home from the hospital.

Here is what they won't tell you in the bright rooms with the coffee bar. Some things are not psychology. Some things are not "patterns." Some things are spirits that learned your family's address and never left.

You can medicate a pattern.

You cannot medicate a thing that wants your children.

It is patient. It watched your father fail. It is watching you now. And it has already picked out which of your kids looks most like the door it came through.

That should scare you. Fear is the beginning of wisdom here.

Because the same book that named the curse named the end of it.

The thing in the walls answers to a Name. One Name. It does not negotiate. It does not respect your effort or your therapy or your white-knuckle Januarys. It bows to blood that is not yours.

"The blood of Jesus Christ his Son cleanseth us from all sin."

That is not a poem. That is an eviction notice.

You don't reason with what's in the walls. You don't manage it. You drag it into the light and you put the blood on the doorpost and you tell it the lease is up.

Out loud. By name. Tonight.

The men in my line never did that. They drank instead. They went quiet instead. They handed it down because nobody told them it could be refused.

I'm telling you. It can be refused.

The draft can stop with you.

But you have to know what you're fighting. Not a habit. Not a mood. A thing. Old. Hungry. Standing at the foot of your child's bed while you scroll.

Get up.

You are the wall it has to get through now.
