# The Sentence the Serpent Whispered Before You Were Born

You were not the first ear it reached.

It has been in your bloodline since the garden.

A sentence.

Not an apple. Not a snake on a branch. A sentence.

"Yea, hath God said."

Four words. Six thousand years. Every kingdom of man built on top of them.

## What Actually Fell in Eden

Read it slow.

Genesis 3:1.

"Now the serpent was more subtil than any beast of the field which the LORD God had made. And he said unto the woman, Yea, hath God said, Ye shall not eat of every tree of the garden?"

He did not strike.

He did not bite.

He did not coil.

He spoke.

The first weapon ever used against a human being was a question. The first wound ever inflicted on the human race was a doubt. The whole project of hell since that afternoon has been the same project. Question the word. Erode the trust. Make the daughter of Eve hungry for the fruit by making her unsure of the voice that named the tree.

The serpent is not in the garden anymore.

The sentence still is.

## The Whisper in Every Translation Committee Room

You have heard the same four words a thousand times in your life and never recognized them.

"The earliest manuscripts do not contain this verse."

Yea, hath God said.

"This passage was likely added by a later scribe."

Yea, hath God said.

"The Greek actually means something different than what your King James Bible says."

Yea, hath God said.

"That word in Hebrew is debated by scholars."

Yea, hath God said.

Same serpent. Same garden. Same four words.

Different fig leaf.

Every footnote in every modern Bible that reads "earliest and most reliable manuscripts" is the serpent leaning over your shoulder asking you the question he asked Eve.

You have read it your whole life and called it scholarship.

It is not scholarship.

It is the first sentence ever spoken against the word of God, dressed up in tweed.

## The Whisper in the Pulpit

You have heard it in church too.

"What this really means in the original."

Yea, hath God said.

"The cultural context tells us."

Yea, hath God said.

"This was written for that audience, not ours."

Yea, hath God said.

The man at the front did not mean it the way the serpent did. Maybe.

But the sentence does the same work whether the mouth that speaks it is righteous or not.

It moves the authority off the page and into a credential.

It puts a man in between you and the book.

It introduces a layer of doubt where God put a layer of obedience.

The serpent does not care who delivers the line.

He only cares that the line gets delivered.

## The Whisper in Your Own Chest

You have spoken it to yourself.

"Did God really say that thing about my desires."

Yea, hath God said.

"Did God really mean it about divorce."

Yea, hath God said.

"Did God really mean it about my money, my mouth, my marriage, my men."

Yea, hath God said.

You have rehearsed the question so many times it sounds like your own voice now.

It is not your voice.

It never was.

You inherited it. Like a heirloom blade your grandfather sharpened and your father swung and you have been swinging at your own throat with for forty years.

The serpent does not need to enter the room anymore.

He left the sentence in the wallpaper.

## What Eve Did and What You Are Doing

Eve answered the sentence with a recital that already had error in it.

"We may eat of the fruit of the trees of the garden. But of the fruit of the tree which is in the midst of the garden, God hath said, Ye shall not eat of it, neither shall ye touch it, lest ye die."

God did not say "neither shall ye touch it."

She added that.

Once the doubt landed, the word started to slip in her mouth. She tightened it where God had left it loose. She added a clause God never spoke. She started defending a Bible she was already misquoting.

That is you right now.

You are defending a Bible you have already let the serpent edit.

You are tightening verses you were never told to tighten. You are loosening verses you were told to obey. You are adding "in this context" and "with grace" and "for that culture" and you have not noticed the recital is already corrupted by the time it leaves your teeth.

The serpent did not have to make you sin.

He just had to make you slightly unsure of the sentence.

The rest was inertia.

## What the Lord Did in the Wilderness

Matthew 4.

The second Adam in the wilderness.

The serpent comes again.

Same playbook. Same four words underneath every offer.

"If thou be the Son of God."

Yea, hath God said you were His Son. Are you sure.

Jesus does not debate.

He does not explain.

He does not parse the Greek.

He answers with three words.

"It is written."

Then a verse.

"It is written."

Then a verse.

"It is written."

Then a verse.

Three lies. Three verses. One sword. End of conversation.

That is the only way the sentence ever dies.

You answer the doubt with a verse you have not allowed to be edited.

## The Bible You Inherit Is the Bible You Will Fight With

If your Bible has footnotes that question the verses, your sword has cracks in it before you swing.

If your Bible has brackets around the long ending of Mark, the woman in John 8, the rejoicing of the angels in Luke 15, you are walking into a serpent fight with a sword that has been pre-bent.

If your Bible reads "the earliest and most reliable manuscripts do not contain" anywhere on its pages, the serpent put that footnote there. Whether the translator knew it or not.

Pick up a Bible the serpent did not get to edit.

Mine is 1611.

You can argue the date. You cannot argue what the doubt costs you when the wilderness shows up.

## The Sentence Buried in Your Bloodline

Eve heard it once.

Cain heard it.

Saul heard it.

David heard it.

Solomon heard it.

Peter heard it on the porch by the fire.

You have heard it every time you opened a book that asked you whether God really said.

The sentence does not die when you ignore it.

It dies when you answer it.

It is written.

That is the sword.

That has always been the sword.

## The Last Thing

There is a sentence the serpent has been whispering to your line since Eden.

You have repeated it. Your father repeated it. Your church repeated it. Your translators repeated it in tweed. You have learned to ask the question without noticing you are asking it.

Tonight, find one verse the serpent has talked you out of.

One.

Read it the way it was written.

Say it out loud.

Then stand on it.

He has been after that one verse for six thousand years.

Give it back to the King.
