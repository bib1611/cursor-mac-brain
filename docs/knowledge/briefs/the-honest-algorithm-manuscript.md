# THE HONEST ALGORITHM
### How a Christian writer reached the lost on a platform built to corrupt them, without selling out.

By Adam Johnson

---

## Preface

This book is for the Christian writer who has true things to say and watches them die in the feed.

You have seen it. You spend an hour on a post that costs you something to write, you put it up, and it sits there with seven likes from your aunt and your old youth pastor. Meanwhile a man with no Bible and a ring light posts a video of himself crying about manhood and gets a hundred thousand views. You start to wonder if reach belongs only to people willing to fake it. You start to wonder if maybe the algorithm itself is rigged against the kind of thing you want to say.

The wondering is reasonable. The conclusion is wrong.

The machine is not rigged against truth. It is rigged against soft truth, hedged truth, truth that opens with throat-clearing and ends with a soft pivot back to nothing. It rewards posts that make people stop, react, and reply, and conviction does that better than charm. You do not need to lie to win the feed. You need to stop apologizing for what you believe and learn how the machine actually weighs a sentence.

That is what this book teaches.

What this book is not is a manual for tricks. There is no growth hack in here. There is no thirty-day formula. There is no engagement-bait template you will be ashamed of in six months when a fifteen-year-old in your church quotes it back to you. The discipline I will hand you is harder than tricks and worth more. It is the discipline of writing sharper truth, structured for a machine that rewards sharpness, with a conscience clean enough to sleep on.

Everything in here is one of three things. It is publicly verifiable, lifted from open-source code or open record. It is my own result, named in first person, with the numbers as they happened. Or it is my own synthesis of what I have learned writing under my own name for the past two years across two publications and one X account, watching what works and what costs me.

I will not teach you to manipulate a reader. The audience this book serves would never forgive me for it, and neither would the Lord.

I will teach you to be heard.

One housekeeping note before we begin. I write in the King James. You may not. The principles in this book do not require you to switch translations to use them. The verses I quote are quoted in the King James because that is the Bible open on my desk. Quote them however your own conviction leads you. The mechanism in this book serves the writer who loves Scripture, whichever translation she reaches for first.

Open your Bible. Open your drafts folder. Let us begin.

## Chapter 1 — The Machine, Plainly

You have been told the X algorithm is a black box.

It is not. The code is sitting on the open internet, published by the company that runs it, available to anyone who can read past the comments. I have read it. You can read it. The reason most Christian writers cannot tell you how a post gets ranked is not because the information is hidden. It is because they decided early on that the machine was either too complicated to understand or too dirty to look at, and they have been guessing ever since.

Stop guessing. Here is the machine, plainly.

Every post on the platform passes through a single ranking model that sits between you and the reader. The model has one job. It looks at a post, it looks at a viewer, and it predicts, for that specific pair, the probability that the viewer will do each of about a dozen things. The list of things is short and worth knowing by heart. The viewer might like the post. She might reply to it. She might repost it without comment. She might quote it with her own commentary. She might click a link in it. She might tap to expand the conversation. She might dwell on it for several seconds without doing anything visible. She might follow you because of it. She might mute you, block you, or report you.

Each of those predicted actions has a weight attached to it. The weights are not equal. Replies are weighted highest among the positive signals. Reposts and quotes are weighted next, with quotes slightly higher because they generate more conversation. Likes come after. Profile clicks and follows are valuable but rarer. Dwell time matters and is harder to game.

On the negative side, the weights are brutal. A predicted block costs you. A predicted report costs you more. A predicted "not interested" costs you. The model takes every predicted probability, multiplies it by the matching weight, sums the lot, and produces one number for the viewer-post pair. The number is the score. The highest scores rise to the top of the feed.

That is the entire mechanism. Everything else is detail.

Three pieces of that detail change how you write.

The first piece. Each post is scored against the viewer, not against other posts. There is no single leaderboard. The same post you wrote at six in the morning gets a different score for your mother, your barber, and a stranger who follows three accounts that follow you. The machine is not asking "is this post good." It is asking "will this specific person engage with this specific post." That is why the same sentence that flopped on Tuesday can hit on Saturday with a different audience composition in the room.

The second piece. The feed pulls roughly half its posts from accounts the viewer already follows and roughly half from accounts she does not. That second half is where strangers find you. It is also where most Christian writers leave money on the table. They write only for the people who already love them and wonder why no new readers ever show up. The out-of-network half of the feed is the door through which a stranger walks into your work for the first time, and the only thing that gets you through that door is a post the machine has decided is worth showing someone who has never heard of you. Write for her. Write for the woman who has never read a word you have written and has eight seconds to decide whether to read another.

The third piece. A post has a half-life of about six hours. The first hour is the loud hour. If a post is going to do something, it will start doing it inside the first sixty minutes. After six hours, the score decays sharply, and after twelve, the post is mostly dead. There are exceptions, and we will get to them in the thread chapter, but the rule holds. This is why posting at four in the morning when your audience is asleep is a waste of a good sentence.

There are two more numbers worth knowing.

The first is Tweepcred. Tweepcred is a per-account reputation score. The machine keeps a running estimate of how much the platform should trust your account, and it uses that estimate as a filter and a multiplier. Above a Tweepcred of about sixty-five, your posts are eligible for the out-of-network half of the feed. Below it, the algorithm treats you as a suspected spammer or low-quality account and quietly limits how far your posts can travel, no matter how good they are. Tweepcred goes up with consistent engagement, age of account, follower-to-following ratios in healthy ranges, and the absence of reports and blocks. It goes down when people block you, mute you, or report you. This is the math under what writers call "shadowban." It is not a switch a person flipped. It is a number the machine maintains, and it answers a single question the machine cares about: should we trust this account.

The second number is the link penalty. Posts with a link in the body of the post are scored lower than posts without. The platform openly says so. The reasoning is simple. A link sends the viewer off the platform, which the platform does not want, and link-laden posts have historically correlated with spam. There is a clean workaround that does not cheat. Write the post without the link. Reply to your own post with the link. The viewer who wants the link clicks through. The machine scores your original post on its own merits and gives the reply to the people who already engaged with the post. You lose nothing. You gain reach.

There are smaller details. Hashtags above a couple per post penalize you. All-caps screaming penalizes you. Posting a duplicate of someone else's post word for word penalizes you. None of those should matter to a writer with anything real to say.

So here is the machine, summarized in a paragraph. A single model scores every post against every potential viewer. It rewards replies most, then quotes, then reposts, then likes, then clicks and dwell. It punishes blocks, mutes, and reports the hardest. Half your reach comes from people who already follow you and half from people who do not, gated by your Tweepcred. Posts decay over six hours. Links in the body cost you. The fix for the link is one tap.

I want you to sit with this for a moment, because the implication is the whole game.

The implication is that you cannot trick the machine into giving you reach. There is no caption you can copy that bypasses the score. The score is built from what real human beings do when they see your sentence. The only honest way to get reach is to write a sentence that makes a real human being do one of the high-weighted things. Reply. Quote. Repost. Like. Stay. Follow.

Conviction does this. Apology does not.

Specificity does this. Vague piety does not.

A sentence that makes a stranger feel something true and uncomfortable in the first eight seconds does this. A sentence that opens with "I just want to share a thought" does not.

The machine is not your enemy. The machine is a mirror. It shows you, in cold numbers, what your writing actually does to a stranger. If your posts die, it is not because the platform hates you. It is because the woman who scrolled past you did not feel anything worth pausing for. That is a writing problem, and writing problems have solutions.

Take this with you tomorrow morning. Open the app. Look at your last ten posts. Ask one question of each. Did this post give a stranger a reason to reply, quote, or repost it. If the answer is no, you know what failed. It was not the algorithm. It was the first line.

The rest of the book is how to fix the first line, and every line after it, without dirtying your hands.

## Chapter 2 — Why Confrontation Beats Comfort

The two heaviest weights in the entire ranking model are replies and quotes.

Hold that fact in front of you for the rest of this chapter, because everything else hangs on it. The machine is not measuring whether your post made a reader nod. It is measuring whether your post made her type. The post that gets replies and quotes is the post that wins the feed. The post that gets a quiet little like and a continued scroll loses.

There is exactly one kind of writing that reliably makes a stranger stop and type. It is the writing that makes her feel something is at stake. She agrees and wants to back you. She disagrees and wants to correct you. She recognizes herself and wants to confess. She sees someone she knows in the line and wants to send it to them. All four are responses to conviction. None of them are responses to warmth.

I learned this on my own account, in public, with receipts.

In early June I posted about the man who ended his unborn son that week because the boy had Down syndrome. The headlines called him brave. Thousands of strangers in the comments called him brave. I sat with the story for a day, opened a draft, and wrote one sentence. "A man ended his unborn son this week because the boy had Down syndrome. Thousands of strangers called him brave." Then I named the parallel that nobody was naming. There is nothing new under the sun. Rome left its weak infants on the hill to die. The empire that called that brave is the empire whose bones are in a museum.

That post did 2,607 likes. 31,703 impressions. 426 reposts. 66 replies. Inside one day.

The comparable warm post on the same topic, written the way most Christian writers write about life, sank into single digits and nobody saw it. The numbers exist on my account. Anyone who wants to look can look.

Same writer. Same theology. Same week. Same audience. The only thing that changed was the first line.

I want to be careful here, because the wrong reading of that case study is dangerous, and the dangerous reading is the one the internet usually picks up. The wrong reading is "be meaner." The wrong reading is that picking a fight gets reach, so pick more fights. The wrong reading turns a writer with conviction into a writer with a chip on his shoulder, and a discernment audience can smell the difference inside one paragraph.

The right reading is harder and better. The right reading is that warmth, as most Christian writers practice it, is not warmth at all. It is fear in a polite costume. It is the writer hedging her own point so that she does not have to defend it. The reader, scrolling past, knows. The reader has been around Christian writing her whole life. She has heard a thousand sermons that began with three minutes of throat-clearing before any actual claim. She is not stopping for the throat-clearing. She is waiting for the claim. If you give her the claim in the first sentence, she will stop. If you bury it under five sentences of preamble, she will not.

Confrontation, properly understood, is not aggression. It is the refusal to bury the lede.

That is the discipline.

Here is the test I run on a post before I publish it. Read the first sentence aloud. Does it make a claim that a thinking believer would either fight or champion. If yes, the post has a chance. If no, the post is dead before it lands. I do not care how beautiful the third paragraph is. The third paragraph never gets read if the first sentence sounds like the introduction to a homily.

Now, the other side of the discipline. There is a line, and the machine itself draws it.

Remember Tweepcred. Remember that blocks and reports are weighted hard against you. A post that rallies your tribe to reply and quote you while also producing a manageable number of disagreements is the post that wins. A post that produces a flood of blocks and reports tanks your account, even if it goes viral in the moment. There are accounts that have learned to write deliberately enraging content because the rage produces replies, and those accounts get a short ride before their Tweepcred craters and the algorithm quietly buries them. Do not be one of them.

The way to confront without falling off the cliff is to tie the edge of every sharp post to a defensible biblical point. The reader who agrees should be agreeing not with your tone but with your text. The reader who disagrees should be forced to argue with the verse, not with you. That is the difference between a confrontational Christian writer and an angry contrarian. The contrarian fights for the fight. The Christian writer fights because there is a man or a woman in his readership whose soul is on the line, and a polite post will let her walk straight past the warning she needed.

Think of it this way. Your warm post is a sign on a country road that says "drive carefully." Your sharp post is the same sign that says "bridge out ahead." Both are true. Both are the same warning. Only one will make her hit the brake.

A passage to keep in front of you. Paul wrote to the Galatians, "Am I therefore become your enemy, because I tell you the truth?" That sentence is in the New Testament because Paul, the most pastoral man who ever wrote a letter, had to defend himself against the charge that telling the truth made him unloving. It did not. The charge was wrong then and it is wrong now. The Christian writer who confronts in love is not the enemy of his reader. He is sometimes the only friend in her feed who is not lying to her.

The tribe you write for is hungry for that friend. Find them by telling the truth in a sentence sharp enough to wake them up. Then earn their trust by making sure every sharp sentence pays off.

The takeaway is small enough to write on a card and tape inside your phone case. The two heaviest weights in the algorithm are replies and quotes. Warmth produces neither. Conviction produces both. Sharpen the first line of the next post you write until a thinking believer would either fight it or champion it, and tie the edge to a verse you can defend. Then publish, and watch the machine do exactly what the machine is built to do.

## Chapter 3 — The Five Hooks That Stop the Scroll

You scroll past nine posts a minute. So does your reader. So does the woman who needs to hear what you have to say but does not yet know your name.

The first line of your post is the only thing in the world that has the authority to stop her thumb. Everything you wrote underneath the first line, every careful sentence and every well-chosen verse and every callback to a beloved old hymn, lives or dies on the first eight or ten words. This is not a complaint about the modern attention span. It is a description of the medium you chose. The medium is the feed, and the feed is a river, and a river does not stop because you waved at it. It stops because something on the bank caught its eye.

This chapter teaches you five things that catch the eye, on the human and on the machine at the same time.

These are not gimmicks. They are patterns I have watched run a hundred times across faith and discernment accounts, including my own, including writers I read every day. Each one opens a different door in the reader's mind, and each one requires a different kind of post behind the door. Learn all five. Then use the one the truth demands.

Three principles run underneath every hook that works, and they are worth saying once before we name the patterns.

The first principle is specificity. Vague is invisible. "The church has lost its way" is invisible. "Last Sunday, three people in our church wept during a sermon about Sodom, and not one of them was the pastor" is not. Numbers, names, places, days, exact phrases the reader can picture. The brain processes the specific as real and the general as noise.

The second principle is tension. Two ideas that should not sit next to each other, sitting next to each other in the same sentence. A grandmother and a switchblade. A pastor and a poker chip. A Bible verse and a billion-dollar industry. The mind cannot rest until it figures out how the two are connected, and figuring out how they are connected requires reading the next sentence.

The third principle is honesty. The hook must be true. It must pay off. The post that follows must deliver exactly what the first line promised, and not a millimeter less. I will come back to this hard at the end of the chapter, because it is the spine of the whole book.

Hold those three. Now meet the five.

### Hook One: The Paradigm Flip

The reader assumed A. You open with not-A. The assumption itself snaps awake and demands to know why it was wrong.

Why it works. On the human, the flip exploits the brain's pattern engine. It hands her a contradiction she did not see coming, and the contradiction is uncomfortable to leave hanging, so she reads to resolve it. On the machine, the flip generates replies and quotes at high rates, because half the readers want to defend the assumption and half want to celebrate that someone else also questioned it. Both halves type.

The rule. The flip must be true and you must be able to prove it in the post. Flipping a paradigm you cannot defend in three paragraphs makes you a clickbait artist. Flipping one you can defend in three paragraphs makes you a teacher. The discernment reader is unusually fast at sensing the difference, and she is unusually slow to forgive a writer she catches bluffing.

Examples.

"The thousand years in Revelation 20 is mentioned six times in six verses, and most pulpits explain it away in one sentence."

"Most of what your pastor preaches on Sunday morning he never preaches on Tuesday night."

"Noah was not a children's story. It is the most adult chapter in the Old Testament, and the church handed it to four-year-olds with stuffed giraffes."

Template. "Most believers think A. The Bible says B. Here is why it matters this week."

### Hook Two: The Objective Authority

A number, a date, a name, or a verse, dropped flat on the table at the top of the post.

Why it works. On the human, an objective marker is harder to dismiss than an opinion. The reader cannot argue with 1611 the way she can argue with "I think." Authority shortens the distance between her doubt and your point. On the machine, posts that open with a concrete fact tend to produce higher dwell time, because the reader's eyes catch on the specific and slow down, and dwell time is a positive signal the model rewards.

The rule. The authority must be real and verifiable. Inventing a statistic to make a true point is the fastest way to destroy a discernment audience. The reader who would have been your strongest defender becomes your loudest critic the moment she finds the number is fake, and she will find it. She owns books. She has a concordance. She knows people. Get the number right, or generalize the claim and say "many" instead of "eighty-three percent."

Examples.

"In the year 1611, forty-seven men finished a translation that is still in print four hundred years later."

"Genesis chapter five lists ten men in order, Adam through Noah, and the meanings of their names, read straight down, spell the gospel."

"Jesus, in His own name, counts to eight hundred eighty-eight in the Greek letters Matthew used."

Template. "Specific number or date or verse. Counterintuitive implication. Reason the reader needs to know it now."

### Hook Three: The Mirror

The reader sees herself in the first sentence. She thinks, that is me. She has to keep reading because you are describing her interior life better than she could describe it to her husband.

Why it works. On the human, recognition is one of the deepest pulls in writing. The reader who feels seen by a stranger will give that stranger her attention until he stops being accurate. On the machine, mirror posts generate two of the highest-weight signals at once. They generate replies, because the reader wants to confess, and they generate reposts, because she wants the people in her life to read it too. The mirror is the engine behind the post in my own account that I described in chapter two. The Down syndrome father post worked because the first line held up a mirror to a culture's casual cruelty that thousands of believers had been quietly grieving and not naming.

The rule. The mirror must be earned. If you have not lived the thing, you cannot describe it without sounding like a man writing a women's magazine ad. The mirror works when the reader senses that the writer has stood where she is standing. The moment it sounds like research, it stops working. Write mirrors from your own scars or do not write them at all.

Examples.

"You opened your Bible this morning, read three verses, and felt nothing, and now you are wondering if something is wrong with you."

"You have been praying for seventeen years for a marriage that has not changed, and last night you almost stopped."

"You teach your children to love Jesus and you cannot remember the last time you did."

Template. "You did this specific thing. You felt this specific feeling you have not told anyone. Here is the honest pivot that says you are not alone and there is a way through it."

### Hook Four: The Velvet Rope

A small, true sentence that says who the post is for and, by implication, who it is not for.

Why it works. On the human, scarcity is a built-in trigger of attention. If something is not for everyone, the reader wants to know whether it is for her. On the machine, the velvet rope quietly improves your engagement quality. Readers who self-select into the post are more likely to dwell, reply, and follow. Readers who self-select out scroll on without blocking or muting, because the post explicitly told them it was not for them, and they took it as honest rather than offensive. You protect your Tweepcred and concentrate your engagement at the same time.

The rule. Do not lie about who the post is for. The temptation is to flatter the reader into thinking she is one of the chosen few. The reality is harder and better. Write for the actual person you are trying to reach, and let the rest scroll on without resentment.

Examples.

"This post is not for the man who is comfortable with his Bible reading. It is for the one who has not opened it in three weeks and cannot say why."

"For the woman who teaches Sunday school and is starting to wonder whether she believes any of it anymore."

"For the man who leads the family devotion on Sunday and has not opened his own Bible alone since Thursday."

Template. "This is not for the comfortable. This is for the specific reader who is hurting in a specific way. Here is what she needs to hear."

### Hook Five: The Action-Talk Gap

You name the gap between what people publicly say and what they actually do. The reader recognizes the gap because she has seen it in her own life, and the recognition is a small jolt of honesty that pulls her in.

Why it works. On the human, the gap-hook activates the conscience without you having to deliver a sermon to do it. The reader does the convicting work in her own head before she reaches your second paragraph. On the machine, gap-hooks produce some of the highest reply rates of any pattern, because the reader has an immediate opinion about the gap and wants to share it. They also produce quotes, because the gap is a frame the reader can apply to her own audience.

The rule. Use this one on yourself before you use it on anyone else. If your gap-call sounds like a man pointing at other men, the post reads as superior. If it sounds like a writer who has wrestled with the same gap in his own life and is reporting from inside it, the post reads as honest. The first version makes enemies. The second one makes disciples.

Examples.

"We say we believe the Bible is true and we read it less than we read our phones."

"Half the men in your church would die for their families and have not prayed with their wives this month."

"The church spent forty years calling Hollywood evil and forty years quoting it from the pulpit."

Template. "We say the thing we publicly claim. We do the thing we actually do. Here is the honest reckoning that comes next."

### The Honesty Gate

Now we come to the spine, which I told you we would come back to.

Every hook in this chapter works. Every hook in this chapter can also be used to lie, and the lie will get the click. That is the temptation. You write a paradigm flip that flips a paradigm you cannot defend. You quote an objective authority that you fudged by ten percent. You write a mirror that flatters the reader instead of telling her the truth. You hang a velvet rope around a post that does not earn it. You point at an action-talk gap to make yourself look better than the people you are pointing at.

All five hooks will go viral with a lie inside them. None of them will keep a Christian reader.

A discernment audience is a different animal than the rest of the internet. The discernment reader is trained to look for the seam. She has been lied to by pastors, by news anchors, by Bible teachers with platforms, by half the men in her own family. She came to your post in the first place because she is hunting for someone who will not lie to her. The moment she finds the seam in your hook, she does not just unfollow. She remembers your name, and she warns her friends.

The honesty gate is this. The post must pay off the hook's promise. Every word of it.

If your hook says "the verse you quote to defend tithing does not say what you think it says," then the post that follows had better contain the verse, the original context, and a defensible reading. Not a vague gesture toward "well, the early church gave generously." A reading, with work behind it.

If your hook says "you opened your Bible this morning and felt nothing," then the post that follows had better address that feeling honestly. Not with platitudes. With the kind of pastoral care a person who has felt the same thing can offer.

If your hook says a number, the number must be right. If your hook says a date, the date must be right. If your hook says the church got something wrong, you must show your work in the verses, not in your feelings.

Overclaim wins the click. Overclaim loses the trust. And a discernment audience never forgives.

Write hooks that are sharper than your readers expect and softer than your enemies accuse. Then back them up.

Tomorrow morning, before you open the app, sit down with a pen for ten minutes and write three first lines for the post you want to publish. One paradigm flip. One mirror. One velvet rope. Read them aloud. Pick the one the truth demands and use it. Throw the other two away. Do this every day for thirty days and the shape of your account will change. Do it for ninety and the kind of reader who follows you will change.

That change is the whole point. You are not building reach. You are building a room full of people who came in because you told them the truth, and who stay because you keep telling them the truth. The hooks are the door. The honesty is the room.

## Chapter 4 — Thread Architecture

A single post stops the scroll for a second and a half. A thread, built right, stops it for four minutes.

That difference is the whole reason threads exist on this platform. The algorithm scores every post in the thread on its own, but the human reader is making one decision over and over again as she goes down the chain. Stay or scroll. Stay or scroll. Every line is a small re-decision. A thread that holds her through ten of those decisions has done something a single post almost never does. It has earned dwell time measured in minutes instead of seconds, it has produced a sequence of small commitments, and at the end of it she is in your follower count instead of someone else's.

This chapter is how to build that thread.

There is a shape to threads that hold, and the shape is older than the platform. It is the shape of a sermon, with the noise sanded off. Hook. Twist. Bridge. Body. Close. You can call the parts whatever you want. The order does not move.

The hook is the diagnosis. One line, sharp, in the same family as the five hooks in the last chapter. It names the disease the rest of the thread will treat. If the diagnosis is wrong, the patient gets up and walks out before the treatment starts.

The twist is the staccato symptom list. Three to five short lines, fragments, one per line, with white space between them. Each line names a symptom of the disease the hook just diagnosed. The twist serves two purposes at once. On the reader, it builds recognition. She nods at the first symptom, leans in at the second, and by the third she is committed because the writer is describing her own life with frightening accuracy. On the machine, the staccato structure produces dwell time. Each line is a small visual unit her eye stops on, and dwell time is a positive signal in the rank.

The bridge is the promise. One line, sometimes two. "Here are five steps. Save this. One of eight." The bridge tells the reader what is coming and what she is supposed to do with the thread when it is over. The save instruction matters, because bookmarks are a quieter but real signal in the ranking model, and threads worth saving get saved.

The body is the work. Numbered, scannable, one idea per number. Each numbered post in the thread should be able to stand alone if a stranger sees only that one. The reader who follows the thread top to bottom gets the full argument. The reader who lands on number four out of context still gets a complete sentence with a complete point. That redundancy is not a bug. It is how the algorithm samples your thread into the feeds of strangers. Any post in your thread might be the one that gets shown to her first.

The close is the ask. Not a sale. A small, specific next step. Follow for more like this. Read the long version on Substack. Reply with the verse that hit you hardest. The close does one job, and you should be honest about which job. If you ask for everything, you get nothing.

That is the architecture. Now the details that make it hold.

The optimal length of a thread is seven to ten parts. Shorter than seven and the format does not earn its keep. The reader could have read the same content in one long post and saved the taps. Longer than ten and the drop-off accelerates past the point where the math works. You can write a thirty-part thread, and people will tell you the engagement on parts twenty-eight through thirty is depressing. They are right. The reader gets tired. The machine gets bored. Keep it tight.

Sentence rhythm matters more in a thread than in a single post, because the reader is making the stay-or-scroll decision over and over. Staccato fragments build dwell and scroll momentum. Long, balanced sentences slow the reader down at the wrong moments. The teaching prose in the body can vary, but the hook and the twist should be short and punchy. One line. Period. Next line.

Now the strategic question. What do you want the thread to do?

There are two main weapons a thread can be, and you choose by what you want at the end. A reply-magnet thread is one that asks questions, names confrontations, and invites the reader to argue or confess. The reply-magnet thread produces high reply weight, high follower conversion, and good in-network reach. A quote-magnet thread is one built of aphorisms and identity lines that the reader wants to put her name on by quoting. The quote-magnet thread produces high repost and quote weight, which carries it further into out-of-network feeds. Both work. They produce different kinds of threads and different kinds of growth.

Decide before you write. A thread that tries to do both ends up doing neither.

Worked example. Suppose you want to write a thread for a discernment audience about the numbers in the Bible. Here is how the architecture lays down.

The hook is one line, paradigm-flip shape. "Your reading plan taught you to skip the numbers God commanded you to count."

The twist is four short lines.

"You read about a hundred and fifty-three fish and feel nothing."

"You read that a man lived nine hundred sixty-nine years and turn the page."

"You were told the numbers belong to conspiracy videos and crystal shops."

"That instinct is not reverence. It is a habit."

The bridge is one line. "Five numbers your reading plan walks past. Save this. One of eight."

The body is six numbered posts. Each one is one number with one verse and one sentence about why it matters. Genesis five, where the ages add up to the year of the flood. The hundred fifty-three fish in John twenty-one. The four hundred thirty years in Galatians three. The twelve and twelve in Mark five. The eight hundred eighty-eight that Jesus counts to in His own name. The six hundred sixty-six that Revelation tells you to count.

The close is one line. "If you want the method, not just the receipts, the guide is in the next post." Then a self-reply with the link, never in the body.

That thread, in the architecture above, will outperform a single fifteen-hundred-character post on the same topic almost every time. The reason is not magic. The reason is that the architecture gives the reader six separate reasons to nod, three separate places to reply, and one specific thing to do when she is done.

The takeaway. Before you write your next thread, write the architecture on a sheet of paper. Hook. Twist. Bridge. Body. Close. Fill in each one with the specific words. Read the chain aloud. If any link in the chain does not earn its place, cut it and shorten the thread. A clean six-part thread beats a bloated nine-part thread every time.

## Chapter 5 — The Substack Engine

X is the river. Substack is the well.

I want you to hold both metaphors in your head, because they describe different jobs, and confusing the jobs is the single biggest strategic error Christian writers make on these two platforms. The river moves fast. It carries strangers past your door at a thousand miles an hour. You catch a few and let the rest go. The well is where you take the ones you caught and let them drink slowly. The river is for reach. The well is for trust, depth, money, and the long relationship.

Most writers I read who are struggling are trying to use the river like a well and the well like a river. They post long, careful, well-cited essays on X and watch them die. Then they post short, breezy, half-finished thoughts on Substack and wonder why no one upgrades to paid. The platforms are not interchangeable. They have different physics, and you write differently for each one.

The Substack engine I am going to give you is the loop I run on my own account and have watched scale from a few hundred subscribers to a paid base that pays my mortgage. It is not complicated. It is just disciplined.

The loop has four steps.

Step one. Write the article on Substack. Long enough to deliver real value. Specific enough that no one else on the platform could have written it. Honest enough that you would defend every line in person. The article is the asset. Everything else in the loop serves the article.

Step two. Post a short summary on X. Two or three lines that capture the spine of the article, written with one of the hook archetypes from chapter three. The summary is not the article. The summary is the trailer. It does one job, which is to make the reader want to click through. Include the Substack link, but here is the discipline. Write the post without the link. Reply to your own post with the link. Same rule as in chapter one. The machine penalizes links in the body. The self-reply gives the curious reader the click she wants and gives the machine the clean post it needs.

Step three. Earn a quote. The single fastest way to extend the life of a Substack post on X is to get another creator in your niche to quote-repost it with their own commentary. A quote-repost generates a fresh ranking event in the algorithm, with a fresh out-of-network audience, and your original post gets a second life inside their commentary. You do not earn quotes by asking for them. You earn quotes by writing things other creators want their own audiences to see, and by being the kind of writer who quotes other people generously yourself. Reciprocity is real. Spend a week quote-reposting the writers you most respect, with honest commentary, and watch what happens to your own work over the following month.

Step four. Repackage. The article you wrote in step one can become at least five more pieces of content. A thread on X that summarizes the seven main points. Three Notes on Substack pulled from individual paragraphs. A Reel-shaped vertical video where you read the strongest paragraph aloud. An email to your list. A reply to a relevant news story when one breaks. The article is not a one-time event. It is a quarry, and you mine it for weeks.

Here are the numbers from my own loop, because numbers are more honest than principles.

I posted an article on Substack that drew roughly thirty thousand impressions on X across the original summary post and a thread. The article itself converted, in those first few days, about one hundred subscribers and five paid. A conversion rate in the low single digits is normal and healthy on a well-written article delivered to a cold audience.

Then one creator I respect quote-reposted the article with two lines of commentary. His audience was adjacent to mine but not overlapping. Inside the next week, that single quote-repost drove another one hundred seventy subscribers and three more paid. One quote. From one creator. More than doubled the lifetime conversion of the post.

This is why earning quotes is not optional. It is the only thing that breaks the article out of the orbit of your existing audience and into the orbit of someone else's, on a platform built to reward exactly that motion.

Two more pieces of strategy before the takeaway.

Do not "move" to Substack. The number of writers I have watched announce that they are leaving X to focus on Substack is depressing. Substack does not have a free reach engine the way X does. Substack's recommendation system is real but small. The writer who walks away from X and tries to grow on Substack alone is walking away from the river to fish in a bucket. Stay on X. Use the river. The river is where the strangers live.

Import your email list. If you have a list anywhere, Mailchimp, ConvertKit, Beehiiv, Gmail contacts you have been writing to for years, import it into Substack on day one. The platform makes this easy and most writers never do it. Every name on that list is a relationship you have already earned, and putting them inside the Substack ecosystem means they will see your work, recommend it to friends, and convert to paid at higher rates than strangers ever will.

Spend ten to fifteen minutes a day reading and commenting on Substack posts in your niche. Not on the giant accounts. On the writers two or three rungs above you on the ladder. Thoughtful comments under those writers do three things at once. They build relationships with creators who can quote you later. They put your name in front of the kind of reader who would convert if she found you. And they sharpen your own thinking by forcing you to engage with arguments other than your own.

The takeaway. Pick one Substack article on your calendar this week. Write it long, specific, and honest. Summarize it in a sharp X post with the link in the self-reply. Send a thoughtful comment under three writers you respect that same morning. Repackage the article into a thread by Friday. Do this loop for ninety days and your list will not be the same list it is today.

## Chapter 6 — Selling Without Selling Out

There is a lie a lot of Christian writers carry around about selling.

The lie says that asking for money is somehow worldly, that the spiritual move is to give everything away for free and trust the Lord to provide. The lie sounds humble. It is not. It is a kind of vanity, because the writer who refuses to charge for his work is usually charging in a different currency, like attention or status or the secret pride of being the man who never sold out. The Apostle Paul made tents. He also took offerings. He also wrote, "Even so hath the Lord ordained that they which preach the gospel should live of the gospel." There is no New Testament case for the writer who refuses to be compensated for the work that feeds his family.

The question is not whether to sell. The question is how to sell without becoming the kind of writer who sells.

This chapter is the answer.

Start with the architecture. You need an offer ladder, low to high, with each rung an easy next step from the rung below it. The cold reader who just discovered you should be able to spend seven dollars without thinking hard. The warm reader who has been reading you for six months should be able to spend nineteen dollars on a focused guide. The committed reader who has bought twice already should be able to spend thirty-nine dollars on a bundle. The reader who has decided you are her teacher should be able to spend several hundred on the deep archive.

The numbers are illustrative. The principle is not. Every rung should feel like one small step further into the relationship, not a leap. If the cheapest thing you sell is two hundred dollars, the cold reader has no on-ramp. If the most expensive thing you sell is nineteen dollars, the committed reader has nowhere to go.

I will show you the ladder I run, because real numbers beat principles.

The bottom rung is a seven-dollar sampler. It is a small PDF that solves one specific problem and gives the reader a taste of how I teach. It is the first yes. The second rung is a nineteen-dollar focused guide on a specific topic, written for the reader who liked the sampler and wants the full method. The third rung is a thirty-nine-dollar bundle of three or four guides at a discount off the itemized price. The bundle catches the reader who would have spent forty dollars but does not want to spend two hundred eighty-five. The top rung is the full archive at two hundred eighty-five dollars, for the reader who has decided I am her teacher and wants everything I have written, organized.

Each rung serves a different reader. Each rung pays for itself. None of them feels like a trap.

Now the offer that does not feel like an offer.

The discipline is simple to state and hard to practice. You pair real value with the ask. You do not ask before you have delivered.

In practice this means most of what you post is free. Most of what you write on Substack is free. Most of your X posts give the reader something to take away without buying anything. Then, in a specific post that ends a teaching arc, you make the offer. The offer is not a separate thing pasted on top of the value. It is the next logical step for the reader who just got the value.

Here is the shape. You write a long post or thread that teaches one specific method in depth, with examples, with verses, with everything you have. At the end, in one paragraph, you say something like, "If you want the rest of the method, with the full worked examples, the guide is here." Link in the self-reply. The reader who got value from the free piece has a clear next step. The reader who did not get value scrolls on without resentment.

This is what I mean by selling without selling out. You are not tricking the reader into buying. You are making it easy for the people you already helped to go deeper. The transaction is honest because the relationship is honest.

A few more disciplines that keep the ladder clean.

Paywall after you have delivered, not before. The Substack post that teases the answer and then hides it behind a paywall is a different kind of post than the one that delivers the full free article and then offers a paid deep-dive at the end. The first one feels like a hostage situation. The second one feels like a hand reaching down to pull the reader up another rung. I write the second kind almost exclusively. The reader notices, and over time her trust compounds.

Never beg. There is a category of Christian writer post that is essentially "please subscribe to keep this work going." There are times when a direct appeal is right, especially around a launch or a fundraiser, but the default mode of a healthy account is not a tip jar with a Bible verse taped to it. Trust your work. If the work is good, the buyers will come without being asked twice.

Pair the ask with a cause when you can. Not in a manipulative way. In an honest way. If part of every sale of a particular guide funds a real cause you care about, say so plainly, once, in the offer. The reader who would have bought anyway is glad. The reader on the fence often crosses it. The reader who was never going to buy is not annoyed, because you only said it once.

Links in the self-reply, never in the post body. The same machine rule from chapter one applies to every offer post. The body of the post does the teaching. The reply has the link.

Case study, my own. I shipped a guide called How to Count Your Bible. I priced it at nineteen dollars. I built a clean landing page on my own site, with the copy in the same voice as the Substack and the X posts, so the page felt like an extension of the writing instead of a separate marketing layer. I sent one email to my list, owning a mistake the system had made the day before. I posted one long X thread that taught the method in summary and pointed to the page. I posted one Substack article that ran the free taste fully and pitched the guide as the deeper method, with the offer placed before the paywall, not behind it.

Inside the first afternoon, eleven sales. Two hundred nine dollars on the guide alone. Sales kept coming through the week as the thread and the Substack post compounded. No discount codes. No countdown timers. No artificial scarcity. Just a well-architected ladder, three honest channels, and the discipline to ask once.

A discernment audience will buy from you forever if you sell the way an honest pastor would. They will leave you the day you start selling like the men they used to follow before they found you. The choice is yours, every time you write an offer post.

The takeaway. Sketch your offer ladder on a single sheet of paper this week. Bottom rung at seven dollars. Middle rung at nineteen. Bundle rung at thirty-nine or so. Top rung at whatever the deep archive is worth. If you are missing a rung, write it before you write another long teaching post. The reader you converted last week deserves a clear next step. Give it to her.

## Chapter 7 — Writing Human in an AI World

The reader can tell.

I have been studying this for two years across hundreds of Christian accounts, and I want to tell you plainly what I have found. A discernment audience can tell the difference between writing that came from a human conscience and writing that came from a machine. Not always inside one sentence. Almost always inside one paragraph. Reliably inside one post. The reader cannot always explain how she knows. She just knows. The writing has a sheen to it. It is too even. The transitions are too neat. The verbs are right but somehow not earned. She closes the tab and does not come back.

The temptation in front of every writer right now is to let the machine smooth out the rough places in his prose. The temptation is reasonable. The output is fluent. The work is faster. The reader, you tell yourself, will not notice.

She notices.

This chapter is how to use these tools without losing the reader who is the whole reason you write.

Start with the diagnosis. There are tells that mark machine-generated text, and once you learn them, you cannot unsee them. I want you to learn them, because the editing pass you will run on every piece of writing that touches a language model has to remove all of them. Every one.

The first tell is the smooth transition. Machine writing connects every paragraph with a tidy connective phrase. "Building on this." "With that in mind." "Importantly." A human writer in conviction mode does not transition. He stops the previous thought, breathes, and starts the next one. The reader feels the breath. The machine smooths the breath out.

The second tell is the parade of triplets. Three adjectives. Three examples. Three rhetorical questions. The triplet is a comforting structure for a model, because it sounds balanced. The human writer occasionally writes triplets, but not on every page. The reader who sees three triplets in three paragraphs feels something is wrong even if she cannot name it.

The third tell is the abstract noun ending. The machine loves to end paragraphs on words like clarity, integrity, alignment, intentionality. They sound profound and commit to nothing. A human writer in conviction mode ends paragraphs on concrete nouns. A door. A name. A verse. The reader's mind has something to hold.

The fourth tell is the absence of a body. The machine has never been hungry, scared, embarrassed, or tired. It cannot describe a feeling from the inside. When it tries, it writes around the feeling at one or two degrees of remove. The human writer who actually lived the feeling writes from inside it. The reader can feel the difference in her own chest.

The fifth tell is the vocabulary plateau. The machine has a midrange vocabulary it stays inside almost all the time. It rarely reaches for the unusual word, and it rarely chooses the shockingly simple word when the simple word is better. Human writing has peaks and valleys in word choice. Machine writing has a long, flat ridgeline.

The sixth tell is the AI-tell words I have been telling you to avoid throughout this book. Delve. Unpack. Navigate. Landscape. Foster. Leverage. Robust. Seamless. Comprehensive. Ecosystem. Phrases like "in today's world" and "let's be clear." These words are not bad words on their own. They are words a machine reaches for because its training data is saturated with them. A human writer who avoids them sounds like a human writer.

Six tells. Strip all of them.

Here is the editing pass. Use it on every piece of writing that touched a language model on the way to your hand, including pieces you wrote yourself and then asked the model to "polish."

Pass one. Read the piece aloud, slowly. Mark every sentence that does not sound like you would say it across a kitchen table. Cut or rewrite each one.

Pass two. Hunt the AI-tell words. Search the document for each of the six listed above plus any others on your personal list. Replace every one with a word you actually use.

Pass three. Find the abstract endings. Every paragraph that ends on an abstract noun, rewrite the last sentence so it ends on a concrete one. A door. A name. A verse.

Pass four. Find the triplets. Cut at least one third of them. If you wrote three adjectives, keep the best one. If you wrote three examples, keep the strongest two. If you wrote three rhetorical questions, keep one and answer it.

Pass five. Find the smooth transitions. Delete most of them. Let the paragraphs sit next to each other without a bridge phrase. The reader will follow. The break is a feature.

Pass six. Add a wound. Somewhere in the piece, a sentence that costs you something to write. A failure you have not told anyone. A doubt you sat with. A line that the machine could not have written because the machine has not lived it. One per piece minimum.

Six passes. Twenty minutes on a piece you would have published in five.

Worth every minute.

Now the affirmative use. The model is not nothing. The model is a tool, and a tool used well by a writer who already has something to say is faster than the writer alone. The right way to use the model is to think of it as a junior editor who never tires, never gets bored, and has no taste. You bring the conviction. You bring the verses. You bring the lived experience. You ask the model to draft alternative phrasings, to find weak transitions, to flag where your argument loses its thread, to summarize what you wrote so you can check whether your point landed. Then you put every word it suggested through the six-pass filter above.

If you do this, the model will save you hours every week, and your reader will never know the model touched the piece. If you do not do this, the model will save you the same hours, and your reader will leave inside three months.

The Apostle Paul dictated most of his letters to a scribe. The Lord let Solomon collect proverbs from many sources. The use of help is not the sin. The sin is letting the help speak in your name when you have not done the work to make the words yours.

The takeaway. Print the six tells on a single sheet of paper. Tape it to the wall next to your writing space. Run every piece of writing that you publish in your name through the six passes before you publish. If the piece will not survive the passes without becoming weaker, the piece was not yours to begin with. Throw it out and write the human one.

## Chapter 8 — The Daily Rule of Life

Reach is not the goal.

I want to say that at the top of the last chapter because everything in this book has been pointed at reach, and the man who finishes a book about reach without remembering why he was reaching for it will become a worse Christian writer, not a better one. The goal is faithfulness with reach. The goal is to be heard saying the true thing by the woman who needed to hear it. Reach is a means. Faithfulness is the end.

That said, the means matter, because the man without a sustainable daily practice does not stay on the platform long enough to be faithful at scale. He burns out, he gets discouraged, he disappears, and the reader who would have been changed by his fortieth post never gets to hear it because he stopped at his twentieth.

This chapter is the practice that sustains the work.

The structure I will give you is mine. Adjust the numbers to your life. The shape should hold.

Two to three original posts a day on X. The strongest one in the morning, in the loud hour for your audience. For most American faith accounts, that is somewhere between six and nine in the morning local time. The morning post is the one you spent thirty minutes on the night before, polished, hook-checked, ready to publish at the right hour. The afternoon and evening posts can be lighter, but they should still be on-message. Three posts a day, every day, builds the consistency the algorithm rewards and the reader expects.

Twenty-five to fifty thoughtful in-niche replies. This is the work most Christian writers refuse to do, and it is the work that separates the accounts that grow from the accounts that plateau. Spend an hour a day, in two thirty-minute blocks, replying to other writers in your space with substantive comments. Not "amen." Not a flame emoji. Real sentences that engage with the post and add to the conversation. This produces three things at once. It builds relationships with creators who will eventually quote and recommend you. It puts your name in front of audiences you do not yet have. And it sharpens your own thinking by forcing you to engage with arguments other than your own.

One thread a week. The thread is the long-form anchor that demonstrates depth. The single posts establish range and rhythm. The thread proves you can hold a thought for ten parts and bring it home. Pick the day your audience is most likely to read at length. For most accounts, that is Saturday or Sunday afternoon. Write the thread on Wednesday or Thursday, sit on it for a day, edit it Friday morning, publish at the chosen hour.

Real relationships, not metrics relationships. Find five to ten writers in your space whose work you genuinely respect, and become a genuine friend to their work. Read their posts before you reply. Quote them when they write something that moves you. Send them a direct message when they publish something that changed your week. Do this for a year and you will have a small circle of writers who will recommend your work into their audiences when they have something true to say about it. That circle is worth more than ten thousand cold followers.

A Substack article every week or every other week. Pick a cadence and hold it. The reader who knows that you publish on Tuesdays at seven in the morning will open the email. The reader who never knows when you will publish stops opening the email. Cadence is its own form of trust.

An email to the list at least monthly. The email is not the Substack post. The email is a personal note to the people who have given you their inbox. Write it short. Write it warm. Write it like you are writing to a friend, because you are.

That is the practice. Two or three posts a day. Twenty-five to fifty replies. One thread a week. Five to ten real relationships. A Substack article on a held cadence. A monthly email. It fits into about two hours a day for most writers who have other lives, jobs, and families.

A few more disciplines that protect the practice from becoming a grind.

A weekly sabbath from the app. One full day a week with no posting, no scrolling, no replying. The Lord rested on the seventh day and the world did not stop. Neither will your reach. The day off does more for the quality of the six days on than any extra post you could have made on the seventh.

A monthly silence. Once a month, take three or four days off the platform entirely. Write only for your Substack and your offline life. Come back with fresh eyes. The accounts that grow over years are the accounts whose writers have learned to step away without panicking.

A yearly retreat from metrics. Once a year, take a full month and do not look at a single number. Not impressions, not followers, not paid subs, not anything. Write for the reader you imagine, not for the dashboard. You will rediscover what you actually believe about what you are doing, and you will write better the rest of the year for having done it.

Honest accountability. Have one or two people in your life who will tell you, plainly, when your writing is starting to drift. The drift can take many forms. Performance creeping in where conviction used to be. Anger becoming the default tone. The same point being made too many times because it once went viral. Vanity in the byline. A good accountability partner sees the drift before you do and tells you while there is still time to course-correct.

A Bible open at your desk. Every morning before you write, before the app, before the inbox. Open the Book. Read a chapter. Pray over the day. The writing that is downstream of that habit reads differently than the writing that is not, and the reader can tell. She came to your account hungry for someone whose work was rooted in the Book. Be rooted in the Book.

A passage to close with. Paul, writing to Timothy in his last letter, said, "I have fought a good fight, I have finished my course, I have kept the faith." Three verbs, in past tense, at the end of a life. Fought. Finished. Kept. Not viral. Not famous. Not influential. Faithful.

That is the standard for the Christian writer with a platform. Not the size of the account. The faithfulness of the man at the end of it.

Build the practice that lets you make that sentence true about your own work, fifty years from now, in front of the only Reader who is going to grade your account on the day that counts.

The takeaway. Write the practice on a sheet of paper this week. Two or three posts a day. Twenty-five to fifty replies. One thread. A Substack article on a held cadence. An email a month. Five real relationships. A weekly sabbath. A Bible open at your desk before any of it. Tape it next to the AI tells from the last chapter. Then live the practice for ninety days without changing it, and see what the Lord does with a writer who refuses to quit.
