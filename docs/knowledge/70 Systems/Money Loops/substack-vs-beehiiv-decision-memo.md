# Substack vs beehiiv — Decision Memo (Biblical Man)

## Verdict

Stay on Substack for The Biblical Man right now. The bankable win from leaving is small (~$5,200 to $6,400/yr net after the beehiiv platform fee) and it buys you a 6 to 8 week deliverability warmup and the loss of free, compounding discovery at the exact moment your paid acquisition has already cratered 86% in four months (145 to 20/mo). You do not rip out a discovery engine while the engine you would replace it with costs $1.63+ per subscriber. If the itch to move is real, test it on Dead Hidden first, not the $82K publication.

## The economics

Two facts kill most of the upside before you start.

First, the "$90/mo" number is wrong for your list size. At 25K+ subscribers beehiiv's Scale plan runs ~$149/mo (~$1,788/yr) and Max (removes branding, allows the direct sponsor storefront) runs ~$249/mo (~$2,988/yr). The $96 figure is Scale at 10K subs or Max's 1K base price. You are a 25K list. Budget $1,788 to $2,988/yr, not $1,080.

Second, the real subscription math:
- Substack takes ~10%, roughly **$8,200/yr** on $82K ARR.
- beehiiv takes 0% of subscriptions but charges the platform fee.
- Net saving on the cut alone: **$8,200 minus $1,788 = ~$6,400/yr** on Scale. On Max: **~$5,200/yr**.

So before a single ad, leaving puts ~$5,200 to $6,400/yr in your pocket. That is the entire guaranteed prize. It is one mid-tier post's worth of conversions, not a transformation.

Now the break-even on the thing Adam actually wants (sponsors). Staying costs you the $8,200 cut but keeps free discovery. Leaving on Scale nets +$6,400 before ads. The question is not "does beehiiv make ad money" but "does beehiiv ad money plus the saved cut beat what staying-plus-Substack-native-sponsorships earns." Substack does NOT forbid sponsors. That premise is false. You can run sponsored content on Substack today, and Substack is in a sponsorship beta taking no cut during the pilot. So the honest break-even is:

**beehiiv ad revenue must exceed the value of discovery you lose, because the cut savings are roughly cancelled by the lost free acquisition.**

Realistic beehiiv ad revenue at 25K, faith niche (a low-CPM category), weekly send, single slot, partial fill: **~$150 to $500/mo, so ~$1,800 to $6,000/yr.** Note beehiiv keeps the spread on ad-network revenue. The "0% take" applies only to subscriptions, not ads. So the ad number is already net to you and the ceiling is soft because faith advertiser inventory is thin.

Stack it: leaving (Scale) = ~$6,400 saved + ~$1,800 to $6,000 ads = **~$8,200 to $12,400/yr gross benefit.** Against that you spend the platform fee (already netted), eat a 6 to 8 week deliverability dip, and lose ~25% of your paid acquisition channel. If lost discovery costs you even 30 to 50 paid subs/yr you would have gotten free, at ~$50/yr each that is $1,500 to $2,500/yr gone, plus the compounding you never see. The ad upside does not clearly clear that bar. It is a coin flip dressed as a strategy.

## The real risk

Two risks, and the second is the one that should stop you.

**Migration risk to the 1,242 active paid / ~$82K ARR.** This is more survivable than it looks. beehiiv copies the Stripe card data, so subscribers do NOT re-subscribe and do NOT re-enter cards. Grandfathered pricing holds. The catastrophic "everyone re-subscribes" scenario does not apply. The actual at-risk slice is narrow but specific: your **22 ios_app paid subs** (Apple-billed, no Stripe card to copy, likely must be re-acquired), your **13 gift + 11 comp** (hand-grant by hand), and any dead/expired cards. That is roughly 40 to 50 subs needing manual handling, not 1,242. Do-able. The bigger operational landmine is your **159 paid subscribers with email turned off** (~13% of paid) and the fact that an "everyone" send only reaches ~73% of the list. Bring a dirty 25K to a cold domain and you spam-folder yourself for weeks. The migration is a managed risk. It is not the reason to stay.

**Growth-flywheel risk. This is the reason to stay.** Your paid acquisition went 148 to 145 to 82 to 40 to 25 to **20/mo**. Free signups fell from ~2,067 in Feb to ~536 in Jun. You are in a steep, active decline. Substack's network drives ~25% of paid conversions platform-wide and 25 to 30%+ of all subscriptions through its app and Notes feed. beehiiv has no consumer app, no feed, no Notes, no organic discovery at all. You replace free, passive, compounding reach with Boosts at ~$1.63+/sub plus a 20% fee and a 10x-CPA funding requirement. Leaving Substack while acquisition is collapsing removes the one channel still partially offsetting the collapse. Best case it does nothing. Worst case it converts a declining-but-free curve into a faster decline you now have to pay to slow. You do not amputate the leg that is still bearing weight.

## What the data says to do

Run both and test. Dead Hidden first. Not Biblical Man.

Dead Hidden is the test bed: ~$14.5K ARR, 164 paid. If the migration spam-foldered every send for two months and you lost 10% of paid, the exposure is ~$1,450, not ~$8,200. You learn the exact same things (real faith-niche ad fill rate, real CPM, real deliverability warmup curve, real Stripe-copy attrition, whether Apple-billed subs migrate at all) on a publication where a bad month is a rounding error. Biblical Man is the prize and the income floor for a five-kid household. You do not run the experiment on the prize.

Concrete sequence:
1. Stand up beehiiv on the free tier. Run a read-only content import (touches nothing on Substack).
2. Migrate **Dead Hidden's** free list (cleaned, dead addresses dropped) and warm a custom domain.
3. After 30 days live on Dead Hidden, read the real dashboard: actual CPM, actual ad fill for a Christian-men audience, actual open rate post-warmup, actual paid attrition.
4. Only if Dead Hidden's numbers beat the model do you even consider Biblical Man. Pre-work for that day: attach a custom domain on Substack NOW ($50, locks auto-redirects) so you do not nuke backlinks and SEO at cutover.

And the cheap win that needs no migration at all: turn on Substack native sponsors on Biblical Man this month. It tests the entire "can I make sponsor money with this audience" question with zero platform risk. If faith sponsors will not pay you on Substack, they will not pay you on beehiiv either, and you just saved yourself an $82K gamble.

## Open questions for Adam

1. **Is the throttle real, or is it the cadence?** The data says fatigue, not throttling: 6 "everyone" sends in 48 hours crashed your open rate 44% to 11%. That is you burning the list, not Substack capping it. Before blaming the platform, answer honestly: did the reach drop because Substack suppressed you, or because you mailed everyone six times in two days during a June fatigue stretch?

2. **What is YOUR actual Substack subscriber-source split?** The 25% network figure is a platform average. Pull Stats to subscriber sources. If most of your growth comes from your 52.9K X following and direct, leaving is survivable. If 20%+ comes from Notes, Recommendations, and the app, leaving guts a quarter of your acquisition mid-decline. This single number decides the whole question and only your dashboard has it.

3. **Why is acquisition actually collapsing?** Did you slow publishing? Change the offer? Lose Notes reach? Platform migration does not fix a content or cadence problem. If the falloff is self-inflicted, beehiiv inherits the same falling curve with worse discovery.

4. **Will faith brands actually buy?** Christian-men is a low-CPM, thin-inventory niche. Do you have, or can you name, three sponsors who would pay you directly today? If yes, the direct storefront (Max plan) is the real case and you do not need the ad network at all. If no, the ad upside is hypothetical and the move rests entirely on a ~$6,400/yr cut saving that does not justify the disruption.
