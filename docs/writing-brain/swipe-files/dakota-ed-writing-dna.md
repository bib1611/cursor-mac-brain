## ED LATIMORE — the hard verdict, no-bullshit DNA

**One-line summary:** He writes like a man telling you something he already learned the hard way. Verdict first, scar as proof, cuss as the period, the qualifier deliberately left off so you argue back. AI rounds every edge smooth. Ed leaves the edges on, because the edge IS the voice.

### Sentence mechanics
- **Front-load the verdict in the first 3-6 words, then defend it backward.** "He should have taken her into the men's bathroom, full stop." Never build up to a conclusion. State it, then earn it.
- **Baseline 7-12 words. Drop to one-line fragments for the hit.** "You suffer now. / You benefit later." Isolate the key line on its own with white space around it.
- **Start cold, mid-stride, like you've already been talking.** "You gotta understand something if you ever wanna be something: No one cares about you." No throat-clearing clause.
- **Fragments land at the END of a thought, not the start.** "Build relationships. Protect your reputation." (whole tweet)

### The anti-slop tells (what Ed NEVER does)
- **No hedge stacks. One honest aside at most, in his own slang.** "Obviously, this is not medical advice and just my experience (do your own research, please), but..."
- **No throat-clearing intro.** Replaces "it's worth noting" with "Here's the part no one teaches you," "Let's be real for a second."
- **No decorative rule-of-three.** When he lists three, each carries different weight or one breaks the pattern.
- **Breaks grammar on purpose because clean grammar reads as machine.** His own words: "Sometimes, you should have a run-on sentence, split the infinitive, or end a sentence with a preposition. It just sounds better." This is the Rosetta Stone — rough grammar IS the human tell.

### Word DNA
- Plain Anglo-Saxon verbs: get, take, break, hit, wear down, sap, scrub out. Never facilitate, optimize, leverage, navigate.
- Name the specific object: "a bic pen and some chore boy," "a fifth of Mad Dog AND Thunderbird," "a D-battery." Never "items," never "things."
- (For Adam, the cuss converts to the blunt KJV-adjacent hit: the cuss-position becomes the hard short verdict at the end of the beat. "Your faith is not weak. It is dead.")

### The earned-authority move
Specific personal event → blunt universal rule. Never assert abstractly when a scar will carry it. "It took me four tries to pass calculus... A good teacher is someone who struggled." And the signature: **lower your own status to raise your authority.** "I'm just a guy who used to get punched in the head for a living." (Adam already has this: "Just me, a Bible, and a laptop.")

### The reply-engine DNA (the argue-bait at sentence level)
State an absolute + remove every qualifier + frame disagreement as a failure of nerve or intelligence. The missing hedge is the hook people grab to reply.
- "It's not fair, but one of the most reliable signs that you're no good is that you're single."
- "You can not solve a courage problem with more intelligence."
- The "pick your poison, except it isn't" reversal: build the opponent's logic, then collapse it.

### WRITE LIKE ED — 10 rules
1. Front-load the verdict in the first 6 words. Defend it backward.
2. Cut every hedge but one honest aside.
3. Start cold. First sentence is already the argument.
4. Break a grammar rule on purpose when it sounds better.
5. Name the specific object, never the category.
6. Land the hard short line at the end of the beat, as the punch.
7. Prove the claim with a scar, then state the rule.
8. Lower your own status to raise your authority.
9. Isolate the key line on its own paragraph.
10. State the absolute, delete the qualifier, frame disagreement as cowardice. The missing hedge is the hook.

## DAKOTA ROBERTSON — the texting-your-best-friend DNA

**Master test (his, verbatim):** "Read the first paragraph out loud. Ask yourself: 'Would I say this to my best friend in person?' If the answer is no, rewrite it like you're talking to a friend who calls bullsh*t when they hear it."

### Sentence mechanics
- **One sentence = one paragraph. White space IS the pacing.** "So I'd delete it. / Start over. / Delete again."
- **Default 4-9 words. Fragment cascades that build, then a hard stop.** "Boring. Generic. Forgettable."
- **One-word landings for emphasis.** "Nope." / "Crickets." / "Nothing."
- **The colon-then-drop.** Setup line ending in a colon, payoff isolated below it.
- Periods over commas, line breaks over paragraphs, fragments over full sentences. (Identical to Adam's PART 0 — this is why the fit is clean.)

### The cold-open DNA
Shape: [mundane physical action] + [hyper-specific sensory/dollar/time detail] + [a small wrongness that creates a question].
- "The rejection hit my DMs on a Tuesday morning. / I was sipping my overpriced $5 black coffee at my favorite cafe."
- "I watched my friend get robbed a few days ago. / All because I had to take a piss." (dramatic line + crude deflating second line; the whiplash is the hook)
- Verb is concrete and bodily (sipping, watching, sitting, shaking). Detail is absurdly specific. Second line deflates or deepens, never explains.

### The anti-slop tells (what Dakota NEVER does)
- **No corporate transitions or hedges.** Where AI writes a smooth bridge, he writes "Nope." or "Lemme explain..."
- **The deflating parenthetical aside** undercuts his own line: "(I still don't.)" "(I promise I didn't smoke crack before I wrote this)."
- **Direct conversational "you" address** — answers the reader's unspoken objection in their own voice: "Now, you're probably wondering, 'WTF does this even mean?'"
- **Answers his own rhetorical question with one word:** "Was it profitable? / Hell no."
- His own enemy-naming: "The education system made our writing drier than a nun at an orgy." "Removing all personality so it sounds professional (aka: neutered)."

### Word DNA
- **The dumber you write, the smarter you are.** "Don't say 'Utilize.' Say 'Use.'" Say the smart thing in the dumbest accurate words.
- **Self-deprecation as a constant** (the single most repeated move, and the reason it can't read as AI): "cause I'm a fatty," "I felt like an imposter," "I was being a pussy coward." (For Adam this is already native: "didn't want to open the Book this morning. Tired. Numb.")
- Talks TO the reader, warm and intimate: "Lemme break it down for ya." "my friend."

### The curiosity-loop DNA
- **The parenthetical read-til-the-end bribe near the top:** "(if you wanna make a guaranteed $10,000/month from ghostwriting in the next 6 months, read until the end)." (Adam version points at the SKU or the turn, never a fake promise.)
- **Mid-post subhead in the reader's own voice:** "'Dakota, Why Did You Just Tell Me a Story About Almost Fighting a Crackhead?'"
- **Never end a section on a closed thought.** End on "But..." or a one-line drop that yanks them down the page. "And all was fine and dandy for the first 10 minutes. / But then, it happened..."

### The confession-to-lesson move
Confess something embarrassing/low-status/painful in a concrete scene, then pivot on ONE clean bridge line into a transferable principle aimed at the reader. **Specificity = Credibility. The more specific your failure, the more universal it becomes.** Bridge lines: "Now, why am I telling you this?" / "That's the lesson I wanna pass on to you." (This IS Adam's PART 0 emotional architecture — confession, vulnerability, authority — with a sharper hook on the front.)

### WRITE LIKE DAKOTA — 10 rules
1. One sentence per paragraph. White space does the pacing.
2. Open in a scene, not a thesis. Mundane action + hyper-specific detail.
3. Write like you're texting your best friend an unhinged story.
4. Use the dumbest accurate word. Kill big words and "utilize."
5. Undercut yourself with a parenthetical aside.
6. Self-deprecate on purpose. Be the failure before anyone else can.
7. Answer the reader's unspoken objection in their voice, then a one-word reply.
8. Plant a curiosity loop early and bribe them to keep reading.
9. Turn an embarrassing confession into a lesson with one clean bridge line.
10. Never end a section on a closed thought. End on "But..." or a one-line drop.
