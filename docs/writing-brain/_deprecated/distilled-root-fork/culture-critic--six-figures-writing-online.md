# Culture Critic — Six Figures Writing Online From Zero

## Source
- File: `transcripts/CultureCritc.txt`
- Teacher: Culture Critic (1.5M, soon 1.6M followers on X; 100,000 email subscribers), interviewed by AOP / Dino.
- Topic: turning a free X audience into a paid newsletter business; thread writing; the low-ticket ladder.
- Type: transcript.

## Core thesis
You make six figures writing online by building authority with massive free content on X, then converting your most engaged readers into a paid newsletter, then offering the small paying core higher-ticket things. Never write anything that cannot go viral, and pick topics that already proved they go viral in other corners of the internet.

## Key frameworks / rules

- **Two jobs on X, every day, no matter the business.** Job one: build authority and give away free, useful, interesting content (thousands of words on the timeline). Job two: convert that into one-to-one relationships with real people. Reach is worthless without the personal connection.

- **Never write anything that cannot go viral.** Do not spend five to eight hours researching a thread that has no path to a broad audience. If you scroll the page, nothing should be there without mainstream-interest potential. The pool of people who treat X this seriously is tiny, so you are competing against almost no one.

- **Make a big topic out of a niche topic.** Position any subject so a maximally broad audience cares. Not "Vespasian rebuilt this building" (niche). Instead "ancient Rome had a technology nobody knows about" (everyone). The teacher's flagged-gold line: turn a niche topic into a big topic so everyone cares.

- **Validated content, two roads, same destination.** Either (a) wrap around what is trending right now (Zuby/Dino method, real-time), or (b) mine your own proven winners and the internet's proven winners and give more of what already worked (Culture Critic method). Both are "validated content" because both borrow ideas the populace already rewarded.

- **Research five times more than you write.** Before writing on a topic, search X, search past Reddit posts, read the TOP comments, pull Google and historical content. The hooks and images that worked are already there. Do not copy them flat; give your own spin. "When I post something, I know it is going to go viral because it has gone viral 100 times before in various corners of the internet."

- **Hook first, thread second.** Pick a topic, then wait until a hook strikes you. Do not select a topic off the list until a viral hook arrives that day. Often he has no idea what the thread body is until the hook exists. (He builds X posts and emails the same way Adam should: subject line / first line first, fill the blank after.)

- **The endless-idea file.** Keep one very long running Word doc or Apple Notes list. Drop every stray thought, every article take, every one-liner. Every morning, scroll the list and pick the topic with the broadest-care potential that you can also hook that day. Eighteen months of this means you never run out.

- **Writer's-block protocol.** If nothing original comes, open your own archive of past posts. Take a different angle on a thread that already performed, reading its retweets and comments to find the new angle. Or shelve the stuck topic entirely and pick a different one. "You know something is good if it is really easy to write."

- **Tailor-made images are the highest-ROI signal.** A self-made image (five minutes in Canva) tells the timeline the thread is high-effort before a word is read. It is the single best signal that clicking is worth it. Early-platform advantage: doing basic image editing on X now is like making Mr. Beast thumbnails on YouTube in 2012. Custom memes far outperform canned or borrowed images.

- **Two kinds of value, give one away, sell the other.** "Entertainment / entertaining value" (interesting, makes the world make sense, the Rome-and-music thread) you give away forever. "Build value" (a tool a reader can use to make 10k or walk out the door) you do not give away free. Lead content should skew toward the entertaining kind. Coaches must be careful: give away the what, sell the implementation, or readers take it and never hire you.

- **The long ladder. Build from the bottom, not the top.** Do not chase the 10k high-ticket sale first. Start with something that gets people in the door cheap ($2 to $4 sub, $10 newsletter). It barely makes money, but a $4 buyer is far more likely to buy again than any free follower. "Easier to keep a customer than acquire a new one." Postpone the reward, ladder it up.

- **The paying core is tiny and that is the point.** Out of 1.5M followers, only a few hundred most-engaged people ever buy. Finding the most engaged is the whole game. You do not need a huge base; a 2,000-follower account already has someone who would hire it today.

- **Never refuse a call. Personally onboard every buyer.** He sends a thank-you note to every single person who pays $10 and invites them to reach out. In one month that produced over 100 conversations with warm people ready to buy any future coaching or service. The number-one lesson: bring the most engaged readers somewhere personal, then pitch directly.

- **Spaces are an underused engine.** Run a Space at least once a week. It pulls warm subs (he picked up 30 to 40 subs from one Space), lets readers hear your real voice (deeper connection, better for selling the "build value" you withhold in text), and every Space transcript becomes an email, content ideas, and product ideas.

- **Pick the established platform, not the new one.** When choosing where to host or sell (Substack vs beehiiv, Gumroad vs newer tools), serious creators pay slightly higher fees for the platform with a track record. A new platform can fold, get sketchy, or shut you down, and your subscription product and readers' access die with it. Peace of mind beats one or two saved percent. You still own your email list on Substack.

- **Persuasive writing is the load-bearing skill.** If you cannot write, you will struggle to grow. Going from good to great with a coach is attainable; going from zero is brutal. Writing persuasively (and speaking) is one of the highest-ROI skills that exists.

## Hook or thread patterns

- **The three-part cliffhanger hook (his default thread opener).**
  1. Line 1 sets the stage with something people already care about and will retweet on its own (a retweetable, scene-setting statement / common trope).
  2. Line 2 is the "cheesy" curiosity clip that opens the loop ("It all started when Pythagoras discovered something mind-blowing about reality").
  3. Line 3 introduces the twist that drives the explanation home.
  - Example from the call: "Past societies produced so much beauty because they knew that math and beauty were deeply connected. It all started when Pythagoras discovered something mind-blowing about reality. The universe is not made of matter, but music." (2.2M views.)

- **Two-things-you-care-about + hidden depth.** Bolt two familiar high-interest subjects together, then promise the parallel runs deeper than the reader knows. "America was founded to be the true successor of ancient Rome... but you don't know how deep the parallels run." (39M views after a big-account repost.) Pattern: thing you care about, the explanation you assume, then "here is why it's wrong / here is the twist."
  - KJV-discernment brand-adapted: "America and ancient Israel made the same covenant. Most pastors never tell you how the story ended. (then the twist on judgment)" Or two-things bolt: "The King James Bible and the printing press arrived in the same century. The reason that timing matters will change how you read Revelation."
  - KJV three-part cliffhanger adapted: "Past churches produced men who feared God because they preached the whole counsel. It started when one translation got quietly swapped out. What your pastor reads from is not the book your grandfather was saved by."

- **The deep-link call to action (newsletter conversion).** Drop the email link very deep at the bottom of a thread, every single day. Even though the link gets a tiny fraction of the top-post views, it converted around 10 percent of those who reached it into the list. Then retweet the thread (or pin it) so profile visitors get a second shot at the link. Exact wording used: "If you enjoy threads like this, I go deeper in my free newsletter. Do not miss tomorrow's email." (Note the urgency: implies an active daily newsletter.)

## Brand application
- Build the email list in parallel with X now. X is rented ground. One algorithm tweak and the game is crushed. The list is owned. Move the most convicted readers off the timeline into something Adam owns, every day.
- Run the deep-link CTA at the bottom of every X thread, then retweet it. Adam's wording, his voice: "If this cut you, I bleed more in the free letter. Tomorrow's hits harder. Don't miss it." Keep the Substack link in the self-reply / deep slot, never the post body (matches PART 2).
- Stop writing anything with no viral path. Before a thread, mine where the topic already exploded: X search, Reddit top comments, YouTube. The discernment angles that already went viral (KJV vs modern versions, dispensational truth, dead pulpits) are proven; give Adam's wounded spin, never a copy.
- Make a big topic out of a niche one. Not "the textual variant in 1 John 5:7." Instead "they deleted a verse about the Trinity from your Bible and nobody noticed." Same fact, broad care.
- Lead with the entertaining/convicting value free, withhold the build value for the paid Vault and for Spaces. In Spaces Adam's voice carries the deeper teaching, which sells harder than text.
- Run a weekly Space. Hear readers, harvest every transcript into a Follow Me email and three Notes, pitch the Vault twice mid-Space ("stop what you are doing right now"), not just at the end.
- Personally answer every buyer. A thank-you note to every $7, $19, $39, or Vault buyer. That warms the few hundred who will ever buy the high-ticket rung.
- Keep selling on Substack and Stripe, the established rails, for peace of mind. Do not move the store to a new platform that can fold.
