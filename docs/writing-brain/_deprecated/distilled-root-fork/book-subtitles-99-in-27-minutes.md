## Core thesis
People buy books for three reasons only: Functional, Emotional, Financial. The title's job is curiosity and hook. The subtitle's job is the value promise that answers "why should I care?" and closes the deal. Write the subtitle first, not the title.

## Key frameworks / rules
- The three buying reasons. Functional (help me do something or solve a problem). Emotional (help me feel something or become someone). Financial (help me save or make money). Most products hit one or more.
- Reader question per reason. Functional: "What will I be able to do after reading this?" Emotional: "How will I feel different after reading this?" Financial: "What's the ROI of my time and money?"
- Title vs subtitle split jobs. Title creates curiosity, can be mysterious, does not explain everything, works like a newspaper headline. Subtitle gets specific, promises transformation, speaks to the reader's need.
- The 99 process. One: make a spreadsheet with three columns, Functional / Emotional / Financial. Two: brainstorm answers per column (what will they learn, what will they be able to do, what problem does this solve, what transformation does this enable). Three: write every answer as a second-person command. Four: mix and match across columns. Five: test top 10 to 15 on real readers.
- Why 99 and why a timer. Your first idea is rarely your best. Volume breaks past the obvious into gems. Set a 27-minute timer and write without editing or judging. Let the list sit overnight, then pick.
- Second-person commands are the unit. Frame every option as a direct active command so the reader can picture the outcome. "Lose 30 pounds in 90 days." "Build a business that runs without you." "Find peace in the midst of chaos."
- Mix-and-match combos. Functional + Emotional: "Master public speaking and conquer your fear." Functional + Financial: "Build mobile apps and earn $10K/month." All three: "Transform your body, reclaim your confidence, and save thousands on healthcare."
- Clarity over cleverness. The subtitle is not the place for wordplay or mystery. If a reader does not instantly understand the offer, rewrite.
- Specificity sells. Weak: "Improve your health." Strong: "Reverse type 2 diabetes through intermittent fasting." More specific (while still true) means it lands harder with the target reader.
- Power-word buckets. Time-based: "In 30 days," "Step-by-step," "Quick," "Simple." Transformation: "Transform," "Master," "Discover," "Unlock." Results: "Proven," "Practical," "Complete," "Essential."
- Length and the 3-to-5-second test. Subtitles run longer than titles, 10 to 15 words is common, but every word earns its place. A reader should grasp it in 3 to 5 seconds.
- Four pitfalls to kill. Vague promises ("change your life"). Insider jargon the target reader will not know. Multiple muddled ideas (one clear promise beats three). False promises that oversell what the book delivers.
- The test questions to ask readers. "What do you think this book is about?" "Would you want to read this?" "What do you expect to get from it?" Their answers reveal if the subtitle is clear, compelling, and accurate.
- Subtitle is an act of service. Frame: a weak subtitle keeps a good book from the readers who need it. The subtitle helps the right reader find the right book at the right time.

## Hook or thread patterns
The "subtitle as second-person command" pattern doubles as a tweet hook and a product-page promise. Brand-adapted command subtitles:
- Functional: "Read your King James and finally understand what your pastor skipped."
- Emotional: "Lead your house again after years of letting it drift."
- Financial: not the lead lever for this audience; use Functional + Emotional instead.
- Functional + Emotional combo for The Biblical Man: "Reclaim the head of your house and stop apologizing for the Word."
- Follow Me register, command subtitle: "Carry the thing you buried for 30 years into the light."

## Brand application
- Adam writes the subtitle before the title on every PDF, Vault page, and product. The title (lowercase, staccato) can stay mysterious. The subtitle carries the promise.
- Run the spreadsheet for real. Three columns. 27-minute timer. No editing during. For this audience the Financial column is weak, so over-weight Functional and Emotional and lean on the combo.
- Command form fits the sword voice already. "Stop letting your wife lead the theology of the house." "Read the Book before the screen." Those are command subtitles and confrontation hooks at the same time.
- Specificity is the same rule the discernment audience demands everywhere: name the chapter, the number, the exact behavior. "Improve your faith" loses. "Read Revelation the way it was written to be read" wins.
- 3-to-5-second test is the product-page equivalent of the scroll-stop. If a tired blue-collar man cannot grasp the promise fast, the page is dead.
- Never false-promise. A discernment reader hunts the seam and never forgives a lie. The subtitle must pay off exactly what the book delivers, same honesty gate as a hook.
