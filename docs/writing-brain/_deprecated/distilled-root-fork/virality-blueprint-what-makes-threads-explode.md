# Virality Blueprint — What Makes Threads Explode

## Core thesis
Threads explode when the first tweet hooks a near-universal human want, quantifies everything with hard numbers, and proves the payoff is real. Then the body keeps people on the post longer (videos, maps, images, story) so the algorithm reads high dwell time and pushes the thread to everyone.

## Key frameworks / rules
- **Open with a number.** Numbers read like all-caps words because the glyphs are physically larger than lowercase letters. They grab the eye mid-scroll. One thread packed four numbers into the first tweet. Quantify everything you can.
- **Universal-want framing.** Pick a topic everyone on the planet cares about, not just your niche. The Ritz thread was sold to hotel owners but framed around what every human wants: to feel cared for, to save time, to avoid bureaucracy. Tie your niche topic to a feeling all readers share.
- **Counterintuitive big statement, second tweet.** State the belief that flips what the reader assumes. "Luxury wasn't about opulence." "The British Empire was in some ways a force for good." "I used to think the Crusades were evil." The flip is the engine of the whole thread.
- **The "but" twist in the first tweet.** If you can land a "but" in tweet one, you create a twist the reader has to resolve. "Everyone talks about legendary writers like Hemingway... but there's a British author who redefined the game forever."
- **Input bias (effort signaling).** People pay more attention to things that took obvious hard work. "I binge watched 100 hours of his interviews" beats "I watched his interviews." A homemade chart, a hand-built map, an "obsession" backstory all signal effort and earn the read.
- **Risk and the unthinkable.** Humans are risk-averse, so they fixate on risk. "They did the unthinkable." "I thought this was a huge risk." Frame a bold or dangerous move and everyone wants to know how it played out.
- **Story sequence: philosophy, then concrete examples, then lessons.** The Ritz thread ran underlying philosophy, then four specific stories (forgotten laptop, the $600 gamble, honeymoon rescue, wedding ring vanished), then three extracted lessons. People do not connect with the abstract. They connect with specific, concrete examples of what actually happened.
- **Power words.** Single vivid words do heavy lifting: vanished, botched, jarring, dystopian, unshakable, almighty, rendered, ascent, envy, bloodthirsty. Reach for the cool word over the flat one.
- **The 5% to 10% read-through rule.** Conversion ratio = views on tweet two divided by views on tweet one. 5% is solid. 10% is the ceiling, the "10 out of 10." One thread hit 6.4M views on only a 5% read-through because the hook sold hard and videos held people. Know your number and push it up.
- **Vertical image at the start, horizontal in the body.** A vertical image at the very start of the thread takes up more of the scroller's screen, so it stops the scroll. Square is the fallback, never horizontal at the top. Inside the body, use horizontal images so people keep scrolling instead of stopping. Square is acceptable in body too.
- **Arrows and red circles on images.** Drawn arrows and red circles pull the eye to the one detail you want seen and stop a scroll-past. Culture Critic and the J.K. Rowling chart both used a red arrow or circle to direct attention.
- **The unreadable chart trick.** A complex chart nobody will actually read still works. It signals "there is a system here, and I will give it to you." People are lazy and will not dissect it, but the implied promise of a system earns the read.

## Hook or thread patterns
- **"After [X years / X clients / X hours], here is every [thing] I could come up with."** Coach Kev's fitness thread. The number is credibility and input bias in one line. It promises a massive dump of value, no expansion needed per item, best items first. The teacher calls this a format anyone can use: after 17 years teaching Sunday school, after raising five kids, after reading the Book through X times.
  - KJV-discernment version: "After 17 years teaching Sunday school, here is every hard truth about raising sons in the faith I could come up with."
- **Open on an emotionally triggering claim that detractors will quote-tweet.** Coach Kev opened with stop drinking alcohol plus a stat. The angry quote-tweets shared the thread to their own audiences for free. 14.4M views, 6.6K reposts. Pick the one line your opponents cannot scroll past.
  - KJV-discernment version: "Your 'personal relationship with Jesus' is not in the Book. You inherited a slogan, not a doctrine."
- **Outsider-looking-in list.** "15 American oddities I still can't wrap my head around." A European on US culture. An outsider on a topic everyone already has feelings about. 63M views. The "I still can't wrap my head around it" shows you are still stunned, not just reporting.
  - KJV-discernment version: "15 things modern churches do that the early church would not recognize."
- **"Why does [universal thing] [bad outcome]?" expert teardown.** "Why do modern movies suck?" Builds authority by diagnosing, names famous faces in the image (humans connect with faces), invites a visceral reaction.
  - KJV-discernment version: "Why do most men in the pew die spiritually dead and never know it?"
- **Mystery object zoom.** Culture Critic: "This 600-year-old painting is one of the most mysterious in history. That mirror in the back is just three inches wide." Vertical image, zoom, arrow. Lean into mystery because people need to close the loop.
- **"Whatever you think, you're wrong."** Tells the reader they hold the wrong answer and you have the right one. Forces the read to resolve the gap. Culture Critic used it twice ("Whatever you think, you're wrong"; "Wrong.").
  - KJV-discernment version: "You think you know what that verse means. You don't. Read who it was written to."
- **Hidden empire / "nobody talks about them."** Dickie Bush on Ferrero. A brand everyone has seen plus a mystery (why does no one talk about them) plus a quantified empire ($17.7 billion turnover in 2023). Two attention grabs at once: familiarity and secrecy.
- **"I used to think X. But [learning] made me rethink this entirely."** Illegitimate scholar on the Crusades. Stand where the reader stands now, then flip. 7.7M views.
  - KJV-discernment version: "I used to think the King James was just an old translation. Then I learned what they cut from the modern ones."

## Brand application
- Adam already opens hard. The missing lever is the number in the first line. Put a quantified credential or stat up top: "17 years." "Five sons." "Read it through 40 times." Numbers are bigger glyphs and they stop the scroll.
- Run the "after X, here is every Y" format as a flagship thread. It is the most copy-ready format in the source and it sits on top of his real authority (17 years Sunday school, 24-year marriage, five kids). Best, most emotionally charged item first.
- Use the angry-quote-tweet engine deliberately. One named, defensible KJV line that opponents cannot leave alone gets shared to their audiences for free. This is the same mechanic already in PART 2 (named confrontation maxes replies and quotes), now with a concrete proof point: 14.4M views off one triggering opener.
- Map-maxing and image dwell. For Biblical Man, maps of the spread of early Christianity, charts of what modern translations cut, side-by-side KJV vs modern verse images. Hand-built visuals signal input bias and hold dwell time. Use red arrows and circles to point at the exact word.
- For Follow Me, the "I used to think X, but" flip fits the 2 AM scar-tissue register exactly. Confession is the flip. "I used to think I was a good father. Then my son stopped answering my calls."
- Keep the honesty gate. Every quantified claim must be exact (PART 1). A discernment reader hunts the seam. Never inflate a number for the hook.
