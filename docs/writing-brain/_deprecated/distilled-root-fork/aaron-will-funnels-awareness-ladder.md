# Aaron Will — Funnels, the Awareness Ladder, and Killing the Engagement Addiction

## Source
- File: `transcripts/AARON_WILL.txt`
- Teacher: Aaron Will (285k followers on X, building since ~2020), interviewed by Dino (Art of Purpose).
- Topic: why to stop chasing the algorithm, how a personal brand is one big funnel, the five stages of awareness, and a 90-day plan to build from a small account.
- Type: transcript.

## Core thesis
Followers and likes are vanity. Revenue comes from a funnel: content and a newsletter feed people up the awareness ladder until they ask to buy. Build the email list first, post value content that proves you can solve the big problem without giving the whole fix away, and be seen everywhere instead of obsessing over one viral post.

## Key frameworks / rules

- **The personal brand is one big funnel with two doors.** Every entry point is either content or the newsletter. Both exist to move a stranger toward a call, a DM, or a sale. Owning the email list matters because you do not own your X page.

- **Newsletter first, before any offer.** With 1k to 5k followers and no funnel, the first build is the newsletter, not a product. Content feeds the list, the list sends nurture emails, the emails move people up awareness, then the offer lands.

- **Five stages of awareness (Eugene Schwartz, Breakthrough Advertising).** Unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, product-aware, ready (most-aware). Most readers arriving from content sit at level two or three. Content's job is to walk them one level higher, not to sell on first contact. Match the content to where the reader actually is.

- **Nurture before you pitch.** Most people send hard-sell emails the second someone grabs the lead magnet. Wrong order. Educate first with solution emails, walk them to product-aware, only then send the emotional sales emails. The sale gets easy because the reader is already ready.

- **Nano problems (Aaron's named framework).** Every big problem is made of many small problems. "I'm overweight" breaks into: not enough steps, eating too much, an illness, scared to go to the gym. Pick 3 to 5 big problems your audience has, then break each into as many nano problems as possible. Each nano problem is one piece of content. This is how you write 20+ value posts on one theme without repeating "you're broke, do this" until people tune out.

- **Solve, but stop at the surface (the value ceiling).** Give real solutions to the nano problems but never the full step-by-step that lets them do it alone. Too much depth means they fix it themselves and never pay you. "Hold the apple in front of them on the fishing rod" so they reach out for the rest. Surface-level real solutions, not the whole machine.

- **Two content types only.** Value content (nano-problem solutions, positions you as the authority) and personal content (your opinions and takes, builds the man behind the brand). Stay off politics, gender, elections. One wrong-take quote tweet from the wrong person can sink an account. Keep personal takes near your niche (he talks gym and relationships alongside funnels).

- **Inbound beats cold.** When your content builds enough authority, people DM you saying "this tweet helped, what do you do?" or come from referrals ("a guy I follow said you're the one for landing pages"). The content's real job is not the like count, it is becoming the go-to name so that when someone asks "who's the guy for X," your name is the answer. Better to have people walk through your door than to chase them.

- **Disconnect from the algorithm.** The fundamentals never change: post content people enjoy, leave comments, build relationships. The algorithm always changes; do not panic, do not cry over impressions, just keep posting and quietly adjust when something stops working. Aaron's first algorithm change scared him; Dino told him "just keep going," he persisted, and the panic was the only real damage.

- **The engagement-addiction trap.** Posting one platitude a day to soak up all your engagement on a single tweet is a slave loop. You wake, post, check, get depressed if it is under 10k likes, and you build no real brand. People follow you "for nothing," get a dopamine spike, and forget you. Aaron rebuilt from scratch (took ~3 years) to trade pure engagement for actual authority and revenue. No regret.

- **Comment like you are texting an old friend.** Follow people whose content you genuinely enjoy (not just your target audience). Scroll, form your opinion in your head, reply with the first real thing that comes out. Do not overthink "adding value." Realness stands out next to LinkedIn-flex comments and AI-generated comments.

- **Comments are also raw material.** Leaving comments and following people who inspire you feeds your own ideas. "I saw that Aaron Will thing, I could write something like that for Substack."

- **Volume / digital real estate rule.** More content, more comments, more DMs, more newsletters, more products, more service. Take up as much digital real estate with your name as possible. The biggest beginner mistake is not understanding you must be seen everywhere. Two or three tweets a day limits you: the algorithm shows a post to ~10% of your followers first, then expands only if early engagement is strong. Pump out a high quantity of high-quality posts. Do not spam 50 tweets an hour, but do not starve the system either.

## Hook or thread patterns

The source is conversation, not a hook list, but two repeatable email/content structures land:

- **Case study as embedded how-to.** Not "I helped this guy make an extra 10k a month." Instead: "This person struggled with X, Y, Z. We did A, B, C. Here's the result." The reader thinks "I'm struggling with that, maybe I'll try A," goes away, fails alone, and comes back to hire you. Brand-adapted: "A deacon I know couldn't get his teenage son to open a Bible. We stopped the family devotional. We did three things instead. Six weeks later the boy was reading on his own. Here's what changed."

- **Solve one problem, expose a bigger one (Dino's addition, the gold).** The lead magnet solves the surface problem and reveals a larger problem only your offer fixes. Dino's "Tweetfluence" taught networking, then ended with "the real way is to have a crew, and the best community is Masterclass." Brand-adapted: a free guide that teaches a man to read one chapter a day, ending with "but you can't disciple your house alone, and most churches won't teach you how. That's what the Vault is for."

- **The "number four" tell.** Give two or three solutions, then "but number four is the one that actually works, here's exactly how." Proves you can solve the big problem while still holding the full fix back.

## Brand application
- **Build the Follow Me / Biblical Man list before the next product.** Adam already has the list and the offer ladder. The unused lever here is the nurture-before-pitch sequence: stop routing warm readers straight to a buy, run them through solution emails first.
- **Use nano problems to kill the "I already said that" feeling.** "Spiritually dead men" is the big problem. Break it: doesn't pray with his wife, screen before Bible, can't name the last book he finished, lets his wife lead the theology, tithes to a building but won't kneel at home, scared to lead family worship and look stupid. Each nano problem is a tweet, a Note, an email, a thread. Twenty pieces, no repetition.
- **Hold the apple on the rod.** Adam's free posts should name the wound and the direction, never the full 30-day repair. The full repair is the Vault. Surface truth in public, the machine in the product.
- **Inbound is already the model.** Readers DM "this saved my marriage." Treat those DMs as the funnel's true output, not the like count. The post's job is to make Adam the name when a wife asks "who do I send my husband to read."
- **Stop grieving impressions.** Same lesson Adam's own brain already preaches as PART 2's "net reach." Keep posting from the wound, adjust quietly, never panic over an algorithm change.
