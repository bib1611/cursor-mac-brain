# Tweeting in Reverse

## Source
- File: `transcripts/Tweeting_in_Reverse.txt`
- Teacher: AOP (Masterclass 24/7, CPP cohort, Lesson 7)
- Topic: emotion-first content creation. Pick the reaction before you write the line.
- Type: transcript

## Core thesis
Viral tweets are not born from templates. They are born from an idea that makes people feel, and the feeling decides the reaction. Write backwards: choose the emotion you want to activate first, then build the post to land in that emotional quadrant. A post that makes the reader feel nothing gets nothing.

## Key frameworks / rules

### The Emotion Quadrant (the axis AOP tweets from every time)
Two axes: positive vs negative effect, high vs low activation. Each quadrant pulls a different button on the page. Pick the quadrant before you write.
- **High positive activation (happy, elated, agree):** gets LIKES. Reader nods, feels good, taps like. Use to be liked, not to sell hard.
- **High negative activation (anger, disgust, upset):** gets QUOTE TWEETS and COMMENTS. The reader feels it inside and must resolve it, so he clicks a button. Strongest driver of replies and quotes.
- **Deactivation / calm (serene, peaceful):** gets LIKES and BOOKMARKS. The "curveball" quadrant. Reader feels calm and saves it. Rarely sells on its own.
- **Surprise / alert:** gets RETWEETS and shares.
- A bad tweet sits in none of these. It is a flat statement aimed at the audience. No feeling, no transfer, no attention.

### Tweet in reverse (the method)
- Do not start from the template, the format, or "is it pretty." That is the beginner move (Mickey Mouse casting a spell he does not understand).
- Start from one question: "What is this tweet making people feel?" Build the line to hit the quadrant you chose.
- Templates are a starting point only. The reaction is what goes viral, not the format.

### Rotate quadrants (do not get tuned out)
- Never be the anger guy every day. Readers tune out a one-note account, and the emotion stops landing.
- "You cannot be a pitcher who throws only fastballs. You need a curveball." Mix anger, surprise, deactivation, happy across the week.
- If you are always angry or always surprised, you burn out the surprise and the anger. The high-negative quadrant loses its power if it is the only one you use.

### The Conduit / Access Point (how to get the reader onto the axis)
- Naming a specific brand, celebrity, addiction, or trending fight is the conduit that drops the reader onto the emotional axis, because he identifies a piece of himself with it.
- "McDonald's sells 6.5 million burgers a day. All of them suck." goes viral. "Fast food sells 20 million burgers a day" bombs. Same fact. The named brand is the conduit; the generic version touches no one.
- The real thread can be about something neutral (how to cook a burger). The named conduit in line one is what gets everyone onto the axis so the neutral body gets read.
- Use addictions, celebrities, TV shows, named brands, and live trending fights as the on-ramp.

### Read the comments of viral posts
- The comment section shows you exactly which emotions a post activated. Study winners by reading replies, not just counts.

### The Refrigerator Magnet effect
- When a post is valuable enough that a reader wants to keep it, he retweets it as a way to bookmark or save it for later (or to show his wife). Most people on X are consumers, not creators; the retweet is their save button. Pack a post with keepable value and you earn retweets as storage.

### Edutainment over information
- Making people laugh is a superpower. Humor gets elation (likes) and pulls in people outside your tribe, even opponents. "Some atheists enjoy my stuff because they think it's funny. That means you are winning."
- Rule from a mentor: "The internet is one giant shit test. Whenever you get angry, you lose." You will feel anger; never show it on the timeline.

### Bragging with no ego (flip the win onto the reader)
- You must share wins so the audience trusts you enough to buy. But never make it me-me-me on the axis.
- Spin every brag into something for them: "I did this. Here is how it helps you." This lands on high-positive (happy) AND alert at once.
- Same rule on confessional "bad things happened to me" threads: they are powerful, but anchor them to the lesson the reader gets, not your catharsis.

### Poll follow-up extends the life of a viral post
- After a post hits, reply to it with a poll that taps the same feeling. It re-opens the conversation and stacks more engagement on the winner. ("Who do you trust more with the future of America?" pulled ~1,000 votes.)

### Roots
- The axis traces to Dr. John B. Watson (Johns Hopkins), who conditioned a child to fear furry white rabbits, was pushed out, went to Madison Avenue, and brought testimonials into early advertising to pace how readers feel. The lesson: it is not the thing, it is how you feel about the thing.
- Ben Settle confirmed in a call that he writes emails the same way: not from a template but from "how do I want people to feel today."

## Hook or thread patterns (with example wording)

### Contrast pattern (puts the reader on the axis with two named images)
- Original: "At Kamala Harris rallies, they twerk. At Trump rallies, they perform Ave Maria." Paired with the two contrasting images. ~500k views. Follow-up question: "Do you see what is at stake in America?" to convert the feeling into quotes.
- KJV-discernment brand adaptation: "At the megachurch, they ran the fog machine. At the old country church, they read the King James out loud. One filled the seats. One filled the men." Pair with two images. Self-reply with: "Which one raised your father?"

### Named-conduit pattern (drop the brand in line one)
- Original: "McDonald's sells 6.5 million burgers a day. All of them suck. You deserve better." Then the neutral how-to body.
- Brand adaptation: "The NIV has sold 450 million copies. Every one of them softened a verse God meant to cut. You deserve the blade He actually wrote." Then the body on what got changed.

### Polarizing-fact pattern (mention the addictive / identity thing up front)
- Original: the fitness hook that led with alcohol so it split readers into elated vs disgusted. ~40 to 50k earned, 100k bookmarks, ~33k retweets.
- Brand adaptation: "Every piece of fatherhood advice I have after raising five: kill the screen, kill the porn, kill the comfort." The named vices split the room and force a reaction.

### Like-only nod pattern (high positive, for reach not replies)
- Original: "Are you normal? Or are you an extremist?" with a relatable image. Heavy likes, few comments, because readers just nod "that's me."
- Brand adaptation: "There are two kinds of men in church now. The ones who clap. And the ones who tremble." Built for the agreeing nod and the like.

### The win-flipped-to-reader pattern (sales-safe, high positive + alert)
- Original: "We sold over 1 million" reframed into "this is for you," with an auto-DM to the lead magnet on engagement. Drew tips and ~1,000 followers.
- Brand adaptation: "We just shipped the 10,000th copy of the manual. Not my win. Yours. Every one went to a man who decided to lead his house again."

## Brand application
- Before any tweet, name the quadrant out loud. Sword posts for The Biblical Man should usually aim high-negative activation (anger at a dead translation, a soft pulpit, a passive father) so they earn quotes and replies, the algorithm's top two signals. Scar-tissue posts for Follow Me sit in deactivation/calm and high-positive, the 2 AM confession the reader bookmarks.
- Rotate. Do not be the angry discernment guy seven days a week or the men tune out and the sword goes dull. Plan the week across quadrants: a confrontation, a calm confession, a surprise, a laugh.
- Use the conduit on purpose. Name the NIV, the named megachurch pastor, the fog machine, the smartphone, the streaming app. Named enemies drop dead men onto the axis. Generic "modern Christianity" touches no one.
- Read the comments on your own winners to learn which wound you actually hit, then reload it from a fresh quadrant.
- Flip every win onto the reader. "We shipped X" becomes "your brothers decided to lead." Keeps the sale clean per the selling-without-selling-out rule, and stacks happy plus alert.
- Stay off anger ON the page even when you feel it. The discernment fight makes men hot; the post stays a blade, not a tantrum.
