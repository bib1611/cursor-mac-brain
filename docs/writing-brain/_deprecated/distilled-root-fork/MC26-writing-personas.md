## Core thesis
Write for everyone and you write for no one. A reader persona is a detailed semi-fictional portrait of your one ideal reader built from real people, so every sentence is aimed at a named human with real fears, lies, and hopes. Same truth, audience-shaped method, the way Paul reasoned from Scripture to Jews and quoted poets to Greeks.

## Key frameworks / rules

**The Four-Part Persona (named, ordered):**
1. External Reality. Demographics and circumstances. Age range stated tight ("35-50" not "adults"), gender, location, education, profession, income, marital status, kids and ages, season of life, time and money constraints, recent major life changes, daily schedule.
2. Internal Reality. Psychographics and values. What they believe about themselves and the world, what they value most, what worldview drives decisions, which authorities they trust, analytical vs intuitive, data vs stories, skeptical vs trusting, where they get information, who else they follow.
3. The Problem. Three layers stacked: the Surface Problem (the obvious pain, what made them look for a fix, what they already tried that failed), the Deeper Problem (the real issue under it, the fear driving it, the lie they believe that keeps it alive, the emotional cost), and the Stakes (what happens if it stays unsolved, what they lose now, the future they fear). Plus their Current Narrative, the excuses and justifications they tell themselves.
4. The Aspiration. Practical goal (measurable outcome, the skill they want), Identity goal (who they want to become, how they want to feel and be seen), the Vision (their ideal day in the future), and the Why Behind the Why (the deeper longing, the legacy).

**The build process (timed steps):**
- Step 1, 20 minutes. Do not invent the persona. List 5-10 real people who need the book, find their shared struggles and aspirations, pick 2-3 who best fit, build a composite from them.
- Step 2, 5 minutes. Name the persona and attach a face/image so you write to a person.
- Step 3, 30-45 minutes. Write a 1-2 page profile across five sections: Who They Are, What They Value, What They're Struggling With, What They're Striving For, How Your Book Serves Them.
- Step 4, ongoing. Print it. Read it before every writing session. Test against four questions while drafting (below).

**The four test questions (run while writing):** Would [Name] understand this? Would [Name] care about this? Is this example relevant to [Name]'s life? Does this match [Name]'s values and communication preferences?

**Five named mistakes to avoid:**
1. Too broad. "Anyone who wants to be healthier" is dead copy.
2. Confusing the persona with yourself. They are usually one step behind where you are now.
3. Multiple personas for one book. One book equals one primary persona. Trying to serve several dilutes all of them.
4. Making the persona too perfect. Real people are contradictory: they want to lose weight and love dessert. Keep the contradiction.
5. Ignoring objections and doubts. Build the reasons they will resist your message into the persona so you answer them before they raise them.

**Writing-with-persona controls (apply at the line level):**
- Vocabulary and tone. Use their language, not yours. Match their technical level. Examples from their world.
- Story selection. Use stories from their life stage. Drop examples that alienate or confuse them.
- Depth and detail. Give exactly enough to act, no more. Do not oversimplify the sophisticated or drown the beginner.
- Structure and format. Match how they want to consume it: summary vs deep dive, steps vs principles, many examples vs straight instruction.

## Hook or thread patterns
This source teaches no new hook patterns. The craft move is upstream of the hook: aim every hook at the named persona instead of at "men."

Adapted application of the contradiction rule for The Biblical Man, naming the persona (call him Dale, 52, drives a route, deacon on paper):
- Surface problem to write to: "Dale opens X before he opens the Book."
- Deeper lie to name in copy: "He tells himself a praying wife covers the house."
- Stakes line for a hook: "His sons are watching which one he reaches for first."

The persona contradiction makes the Mirror Test hook land harder because it is built on a real split, not a cartoon. "You tithe to a building. You won't pray with your wife." works because Dale is both things at once.

- The Biblical Man: name him. A 52-year-old route driver, 24 years married, deacon on the rolls, KJV in the truck door pocket, opens his phone before the Book, tells himself his wife's prayers cover the household. Surface problem, screen addiction. Deeper lie, "a praying wife is enough cover." Stake, his sons learn which one he reaches for first. Write the sword posts at that man.
