                  Unlocking the X Algorithm

Most people think the X algorithm is mysterious, biased, or manually controlled. It is much
simpler and much more mechanical than people imagine.

Earlier, X released its recommendation code publicly, not as a marketing gesture, but to
make it easier for people to understand how the system works. When you read that code,
one thing becomes obvious quickly.

Every time you open the For You feed, X runs a fast pipeline whose only job is to answer one
question: which posts are you most likely to engage with right now?

Not which posts are best, not which posts are true, and not which posts matter. Just which
ones you are most likely to interact with.

The process starts with you. Before picking any posts, X pulls your recent behavior. It looks
at what you liked, what you replied to, what you watched, what you ignored, who you follow,
and who you muted. This becomes a live behavioral profile that represents your current
interests and habits. The system uses a moving snapshot of how you have behaved in the
very recent past.




Then the system pulls posts from two main sources.
   1. The first is in-network content, which simply means recent posts from accounts you
      already follow. This layer is familiar and predictable, and it gives the feed a sense of
      continuity.

   2. The second source is out-of-network content, which comes from the entire
      platform. Here, X uses a machine learning retrieval system that finds posts engaged
      with by people who behave similarly to you.

Both you and every post are turned into mathematical vectors, and the system retrieves the
closest matches by similarity.

At this stage, X may have thousands of candidate posts. Most of them never make it to
scoring. Duplicates are removed, old posts are removed, your own posts are removed,
blocked authors are removed, muted keywords are removed, and posts you have already
seen are removed. By the time ranking begins, the feed has already been heavily cleaned
and narrowed down.
Now comes the core of the system. A single large transformer model scores every
remaining post. It does not predict whether a post is relevant in some abstract sense. It
predicts specific actions. It estimates the probability that you will like the post, reply to it,
repost it, click it, watch the video, dwell on it, follow the author, mute them, block them, or
report them. Each of these predicted actions is multiplied by a weight. Positive actions
push the post up, while negative actions push it down. All of these weighted predictions are
added together into one final number, and that number determines where the post appears
in your feed.

One detail matters more than most people realize. Each post is scored in isolation. Posts
are not compared to each other. They are compared only to you. This means your feed is
not ranked by what is popular or trending. It is ranked by what the model thinks you
personally are most likely to react to.

Two people can open the same app at the same time and see completely different worlds
because the ranking is individualized at the scoring level.

Another design choice explains much about how modern feeds behave. X no longer relies
on hand-written relevance rules. There are no humans deciding to boost politics, penalize
links, or prefer long posts. Almost all relevance is learned from engagement patterns. If a
type of content reliably produces clicks, replies, or dwell time, the model learns to promote
it. If a type of content leads to blocks, mutes, or reports, the model learns to suppress it.

The system does not optimize for truth, wisdom, nor social good. It optimizes for expected
engagement. Over time, it learns which topics keep you scrolling, which emotions hold
your attention, which conflicts you engage with, and which styles you ignore. The result is
not a neutral feed but a personalized reality, shaped quietly by your own past behavior.

That is the X algorithm in simple terms: a machine that studies your past actions, predicts
your future reactions, and builds your feed to maximize the chance that you interact again.
