The Art and Science of Book Subtitles: A Practical Guide

How to Write 99 Book Subtitles in 27 Minutes

Even if you have no idea what your book title should be

The Foundation: Understanding Why People Buy Books

Before you write a single subtitle, understand this fundamental truth: people don't buy
books randomly. They buy books for one (or more) of three specific reasons:

1. Functional

Help me do something or solve a problem

   •   Examples: Learn a skill, implement a system, fix an issue

   •   Reader question: "What will I be able to do after reading this?"

2. Emotional

Help me feel something or become someone

   •   Examples: Find peace, gain confidence, experience transformation

   •   Reader question: "How will I feel different after reading this?"

3. Financial

Help me save or make money

   •   Examples: Increase income, reduce expenses, build wealth

   •   Reader question: "What's the ROI of my time and money?"

Exercise: Think about the last three books you bought. Which reason(s) drove each
purchase? You'll likely find at least one (and often multiple) of these motivations at work.
The Critical Distinction: Title vs. Subtitle

Most Authors Get This Backwards

Common mistake: Trying to pack all the value into the title

The truth: Your title and subtitle have completely different jobs

Title's Job

Create curiosity and intrigue. Make people want to know more. Act as a hook.

   •   Think of it like a newspaper headline

   •   It can be mysterious, clever, or provocative

   •   It doesn't have to explain everything

   •   Examples: "Atomic Habits," "Thinking, Fast and Slow," "The Obstacle Is the Way"

Subtitle's Job

Explain the value proposition. Answer "Why should I care?" Close the deal.

   •   This is where you get specific

   •   This is where you promise transformation

   •   This is where you speak to the reader's needs

   •   Examples:

              o   "An Easy & Proven Way to Build Good Habits & Break Bad Ones"

              o   "The Surprising Science of Meetings"

              o   "The Timeless Art of Turning Trials into Triumph"



The Framework: Writing 99 Subtitles

Why 99?

Because your first idea is rarely your best idea. By forcing yourself to generate volume, you:

   •   Break through obvious ideas to find gems

   •   Explore multiple angles
   •   Discover unexpected combinations

   •   Give yourself real options to choose from

The Process

Step 1: Create Your Spreadsheet

Open a spreadsheet with three columns headed:

   •   Functional

   •   Emotional

   •   Financial

Step 2: Answer Key Questions for Each Column

For each reason (Functional, Emotional, Financial), brainstorm answers to:

   •   What do I want my book to do for people?

   •   What will they learn?

   •   What will they be able to do?

   •   How will this make their life easier/better/richer?

   •   What problem does this solve?

   •   What transformation does this enable?

Step 3: Write in Second Person Commands

Frame your answers as direct, active commands to the reader. This creates immediacy and
clarity.

Examples:

   •   "Lose 30 pounds in 90 days"

   •   "Build a business that runs without you"

   •   "Prevent chronic disease through simple daily habits"

   •   "Double your productivity in half the time"

   •   "Find peace in the midst of chaos"

   •   "Master the art of persuasive writing"
Why commands work: They paint a clear picture of the outcome. The reader can
immediately imagine themselves achieving that result.

Step 4: Mix and Match

Once you have 30+ options per column, start combining elements:

   •   Functional + Emotional: "Master public speaking and conquer your fear"

   •   Functional + Financial: "Build mobile apps and earn $10K/month"

   •   Emotional + Financial: "Find fulfilling work that pays what you're worth"

   •   All three: "Transform your body, reclaim your confidence, and save thousands on
       healthcare"

Step 5: Test and Refine

Read your top 10-15 subtitles to others and ask:

   •   "What do you think this book is about?"

   •   "Would you want to read this?"

   •   "What do you expect to get from it?"

Their answers will reveal whether your subtitle is clear, compelling, and accurate.



Best Practices and Additional Principles

Clarity Over Cleverness

Your subtitle is not the place for wordplay or mystery. Be direct. Be specific. If someone
reads your subtitle and doesn't immediately understand what the book offers, revise it.

Specificity Sells

Compare:

   •   Weak: "Improve your health"

   •   Strong: "Reverse type 2 diabetes through intermittent fasting"

The more specific you can be (while remaining accurate), the more your subtitle will
resonate with your target reader.
Use Power Words Strategically

Certain words carry inherent value and urgency:

   •   Time-based: "In 30 days," "Step-by-step," "Quick," "Simple"

   •   Transformation: "Transform," "Master," "Discover," "Unlock"

   •   Results: "Proven," "Practical," "Complete," "Essential"

Length Considerations

   •   Subtitles can be longer than titles (10-15 words is common)

   •   But don't ramble—every word should earn its place

   •   Test readability: Can someone grasp it in 3-5 seconds?

Avoid These Common Pitfalls

   •   Vague promises: "Change your life" (How? In what way?)

   •   Insider jargon: Terms your target reader won't understand

   •   Multiple ideas: One clear promise beats three muddled ones

   •   False promises: Don't oversell what the book delivers



A Note on Classical Rhetoric

The ancient rhetoricians understood something Joshua Lisec has rediscovered: effective
communication requires understanding human motivation. Aristotle identified three
modes of persuasion—logos (logic/reason), pathos (emotion), and ethos
(character/credibility)—that map remarkably well to our functional, emotional, and
financial appeals.

Your subtitle must speak to the whole person: their rational need for solutions (functional),
their emotional desire for transformation (emotional), and their practical concerns about
resources (financial). This isn't manipulation; it's honest communication that respects the
full humanity of your reader.
Your Action Steps

   1. Don't write your title first. Start with the subtitle.

   2. Open your spreadsheet today. Create those three columns.

   3. Set a timer for 27 minutes. Write without editing or judging.

   4. Aim for 99 options. Quantity leads to quality.

   5. Let it sit overnight. Fresh eyes reveal winners.

   6. Test your top 5. Get feedback from your target readers.

   7. Choose the subtitle that most clearly communicates value. Then, and only then,
      worry about the title.



Final Thought

Your book represents hours, months, or years of your life. It contains valuable insights that
could genuinely help people. Don't let a weak subtitle keep it from reaching the readers
who need it most.

A great subtitle is an act of service—it helps the right readers find the right book at the right
time.

Now go write 99 options. Your readers are waiting.
