Creating Reader Personas: Writing for Real
People, Not Abstractions
A Practical Lesson for Authors Who Want to Connect with Their
Readers


Why Personas Matter: The Principle of Particularity
The Problem with Writing to "Everyone"

When you try to write for everyone, you end up writing for no one. Your message becomes
diluted, generic, and forgettable. As the saying goes, "If you're speaking to everyone, you're
speaking to no one."

The ancient wisdom: Even Aristotle understood this. In his Rhetoric, he emphasized that
effective communication requires understanding your specific audience—their age, education,
values, circumstances, and concerns. You cannot persuade someone whose mind you do not
understand.

The practical reality: Try this exercise—write a single paragraph explaining "why you should
eat healthy" to:

   1. A 25-year-old athlete
   2. A 55-year-old executive with prediabetes
   3. A busy mother of three young children

Notice how the same core message must be communicated completely differently based on who
you're addressing? That's why personas matter.

Writing as an Act of Service

From a biblical perspective, knowing your reader is an act of love and stewardship. To serve
someone well, you must understand their needs, fears, hopes, and circumstances. Paul wrote
differently to the Corinthians than to Philemon—not because truth changed, but because
different people needed truth applied to their specific situations.

Your reader persona is not a marketing gimmick. It's a tool that helps you faithfully steward your
message by delivering it in the way your specific reader needs to receive it.
What Is a Reader Persona?
A reader persona is a detailed, semi-fictional representation of your ideal reader based on real
data, reasonable assumptions, and strategic thinking about who will benefit most from your
book.

What it includes:

   •   Demographics (age, gender, location, income, education)
   •   Psychographics (values, beliefs, attitudes, interests)
   •   Current situation (life stage, challenges, goals)
   •   Pain points (problems they face, frustrations they feel)
   •   Aspirations (who they want to become, what they want to achieve)
   •   Objections (why they might resist your message)
   •   Information habits (where they get information, what they trust)

What it is NOT:

   •   A sterile demographic report
   •   A limiting factor that excludes other readers
   •   An excuse to write only what people want to hear rather than what they need to hear




The Four-Part Framework for Building Your Persona
Part 1: The External Reality (Demographics & Circumstances)

These are the observable facts about your reader's life.

Key questions to answer:

Basic Demographics:

   •   Age range (be specific: "35-50" not "adults")
   •   Gender (if relevant to your topic)
   •   Location (urban/suburban/rural, region, country)
   •   Education level
   •   Career/profession
   •   Income range
   •   Marital status
   •   Children (ages if relevant)

Current Life Circumstances:
   •   What season of life are they in?
   •   What are their time constraints?
   •   What are their financial constraints?
   •   What major life changes are they facing or have recently faced?
   •   What is their typical daily schedule like?

Example: "Sarah is a 38-year-old marketing director living in suburban Chicago. She has a
master's degree, earns $95K annually, and has two children ages 6 and 9. She's married to a
software engineer who travels frequently for work. Her days are packed from 6 AM to 9 PM with
work deadlines, school pickups, activities, and household management."



Part 2: The Internal Reality (Psychographics & Values)

These are the invisible factors that shape how your reader thinks and decides.

Key questions to answer:

Values & Beliefs:

   •   What do they believe about themselves?
   •   What do they believe about the world?
   •   What do they value most? (family, achievement, security, freedom, faith, etc.)
   •   What worldview shapes their decisions?
   •   What authorities do they trust?

Personality & Preferences:

   •   Are they analytical or intuitive?
   •   Do they prefer data or stories?
   •   Are they risk-takers or security-seekers?
   •   Are they skeptical or trusting?
   •   Do they like step-by-step instructions or big-picture vision?

Information Consumption:

   •   Where do they get their information? (books, podcasts, social media, friends)
   •   How do they prefer to learn? (reading, watching, doing)
   •   How much time do they have for reading?
   •   What other authors/influencers do they follow?

Example: "Sarah values family above career but feels guilty about not 'having it all.' She's a
practical problem-solver who trusts data and expert opinion. She listens to podcasts during her
commute, scrolls Instagram before bed, and reads 1-2 books per month. She follows productivity
experts and parenting influencers. She's skeptical of anything that sounds too easy or 'woo-
woo.'"



Part 3: The Problem (Pain Points & Frustrations)

This is where you identify the specific struggles that your book addresses.

Key questions to answer:

The Surface Problem:

   •   What immediate, obvious problem are they facing?
   •   What prompted them to search for a solution?
   •   What have they already tried that didn't work?

The Deeper Problem:

   •   What's the real issue beneath the surface problem?
   •   What fear drives this problem?
   •   What lie do they believe that perpetuates this problem?
   •   What's the emotional cost of this problem?

The Stakes:

   •   What happens if this problem isn't solved?
   •   What are they losing right now?
   •   What future are they afraid of?

Their Current Narrative:

   •   How do they explain their problem to themselves?
   •   What excuses or justifications do they use?
   •   What makes them feel hopeless about solving it?

Example: "Sarah feels constantly overwhelmed and behind. Surface problem: She can't keep up
with everything. Deeper problem: She believes she's failing as both a mother and a professional,
and she's exhausted trying to prove she can do both perfectly. She's tried productivity apps, meal
planning, and hiring cleaners, but nothing addresses the core issue—her perfectionism and
inability to set boundaries. She fears that if she doesn't solve this, she'll burn out, damage her
relationships, or miss her children's childhood. She tells herself, 'Other women manage this—I
just need to try harder.'"
Part 4: The Aspiration (Goals & Desired Future)

This is where you understand what transformation your reader is seeking.

Key questions to answer:

The Practical Goal:

   •   What specific outcome do they want?
   •   What would success look like in measurable terms?
   •   What ability or skill do they want to gain?

The Identity Goal:

   •   Who do they want to become?
   •   How do they want to feel about themselves?
   •   How do they want others to see them?

The Vision:

   •   What does their ideal future look like?
   •   What does a typical day look like in that future?
   •   What becomes possible that isn't possible now?

The Why Behind the Why:

   •   Why does this goal truly matter to them?
   •   What deeper value or longing does it fulfill?
   •   What legacy or impact do they want to have?

Example: "Sarah wants to feel calm and present instead of frantic and scattered. Specifically,
she wants to end her workday by 6 PM without guilt, be fully present during family dinners, and
sleep well without replaying her day's failures. She wants to become the kind of mother her kids
remember as engaged and joyful, not stressed and distracted. She wants to be seen (and to see
herself) as someone who has her life together without sacrificing her values. Ultimately, she
wants to steward her limited time on earth well—to invest in what truly matters while it matters."




Creating Your Persona: The Complete Exercise
Step 1: Start with Real People (20 minutes)

Don't invent your persona from thin air. Base it on real people you know or have observed
who would benefit from your book.
Exercise:

   1.   List 5-10 real people who need what your book offers
   2.   What do they have in common?
   3.   What are their shared struggles?
   4.   What are their shared aspirations?
   5.   Choose 2-3 who best represent your ideal reader
   6.   Use them as the foundation for your composite persona

Step 2: Name Your Persona (5 minutes)

Give your persona a name and find or create an image that represents them. This makes them
feel real and helps you write to a person, not an abstraction.

Why this matters: When you sit down to write, you'll think "Will Sarah understand this?"
instead of "Will readers understand this?"

Step 3: Write the Persona Profile (30-45 minutes)

Create a 1-2 page document that includes:

Section 1: Who They Are

   •    Name, age, occupation, family situation
   •    Key demographic details
   •    Brief day-in-the-life description

Section 2: What They Value

   •    Core values and beliefs
   •    Worldview and trusted authorities
   •    Learning preferences and information sources

Section 3: What They're Struggling With

   •    The surface problem
   •    The deeper problem
   •    Failed solutions they've tried
   •    The lies they believe
   •    The emotional and practical cost

Section 4: What They're Striving For

   •    Practical goals
   •    Identity transformation
   •    Ideal future vision
   •   The deeper "why"

Section 5: How Your Book Serves Them

   •   What specific help does your book offer?
   •   What transformation does it enable?
   •   Why is your approach uniquely suited to them?

Step 4: Test Your Persona (Ongoing)

As you write, regularly ask:

   •   Would [Persona Name] understand this?
   •   Would [Persona Name] care about this?
   •   Is this example relevant to [Persona Name]'s life?
   •   Does this match [Persona Name]'s values and communication preferences?




Common Mistakes to Avoid
Mistake #1: Being Too Broad

Wrong: "My book is for anyone who wants to be healthier" Right: "My book is for busy
professional women in their 40s who want to prevent chronic disease without extreme diets or
spending hours at the gym"

Mistake #2: Confusing Your Persona with Yourself

Your ideal reader might share some of your characteristics, but they're not you. They might be
one step behind where you are now, or facing challenges you've already overcome.

Mistake #3: Creating Multiple Personas for One Book

One book = one primary persona. Yes, others will read it, but trying to serve multiple personas in
one book creates confusion and dilution.

Mistake #4: Making Your Persona Too Perfect

Real people are contradictory. They want to lose weight but also love dessert. They value family
but also ambition. Don't create an unrealistically consistent persona.

Mistake #5: Ignoring Objections and Doubts
Your persona should include reasons they might resist your message. This helps you address
objections proactively and write more persuasively.




Advanced Application: Writing with Your Persona
Vocabulary and Tone

Ask: How does [Persona Name] talk?

   •   Use their language, not yours
   •   Match their level of technical expertise
   •   Respect their time and intelligence
   •   Avoid jargon they won't understand
   •   Use examples from their world

Story Selection

Ask: What stories will resonate with [Persona Name]?

   •   Use examples from their life stage and situation
   •   Reference cultural touchstones they'll recognize
   •   Avoid examples that alienate or confuse them

Depth and Detail

Ask: How much does [Persona Name] need to know?

   •   Don't oversimplify if they're sophisticated
   •   Don't overwhelm if they're beginners
   •   Give them exactly enough to act, no more

Structure and Format

Ask: How does [Persona Name] want to consume this?

   •   Quick summaries or deep dives?
   •   Step-by-step or principles-based?
   •   Lots of examples or straightforward instruction?
The Classical and Biblical Foundation
Aristotle on Audience

In Rhetoric, Aristotle taught that effective communication requires understanding three things
about your audience:

   1. Their existing beliefs and knowledge (what do they already think?)
   2. Their emotional state (what do they feel?)
   3. Their character and values (what do they care about?)

A persona addresses all three. It helps you meet your reader where they are—not where you wish
they were.

Paul's Example

Consider how Paul adapted his message:

   •   To Jews, he reasoned from Scripture
   •   To Greeks, he quoted their poets and engaged their philosophy
   •   To different churches, he addressed their specific circumstances and challenges

Paul's message was unchanging, but his method was flexible. He knew his audience. Your
persona helps you do the same.

The Heart of the Matter

Creating a persona is not manipulation—it's empathy. It's asking, "Who is my neighbor, and how
can I serve them well?" It's recognizing that truth must be communicated in ways that specific
people can receive it, understand it, and apply it.

C.S. Lewis was a master of this. He wrote differently for children (Narnia), intellectuals (Mere
Christianity), and grieving sufferers (A Grief Observed). Same core convictions, different
approaches—because he understood his audience.




Your Assignment
Part 1: Create Your Primary Reader Persona (60-90 minutes)

Using the framework above, create a complete persona profile for your book's ideal reader.
Include all four parts:

   1. External Reality
   2. Internal Reality
   3. The Problem
   4. The Aspiration

Part 2: Test It (15 minutes)

Read your persona to someone who fits the description. Ask:

   •   Does this feel accurate?
   •   What's missing?
   •   What resonates most?
   •   What would you add?

Part 3: Use It (Ongoing)

Print your persona and keep it visible while you write. Begin each writing session by reading it.
Ask yourself regularly: "Am I serving this person well with what I'm writing?"




Final Thought: Writing as Incarnation
The Christian doctrine of incarnation teaches us something profound about communication: God
didn't send us a message from heaven. He became one of us, spoke our language, walked in our
shoes, understood our struggles.

When you create a detailed persona and write specifically to them, you're practicing a similar
principle. You're incarnating your message into the specific life, language, and circumstances of
real people.

This is harder than writing generically. But it's also more faithful, more effective, and ultimately
more loving.

Your reader is not a demographic. They're a person made in the image of God, with real fears
and real hopes, who needs what you have to offer.

Know them. Serve them well.
