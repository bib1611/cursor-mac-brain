# Growth Strategies — Building a Substack From Day One

## Source
- File: `transcripts/BUILDING_A_SUBSTACK_FROM_DAY_ONE_.txt`
- Teacher: Dino (Art of Purpose / Creation 24-7 on X), with The Culturist as guest (~265,000 subscribers)
- Topic: Setting up and launching a Substack live from zero, plus first-1,000-subscriber growth
- Type: transcript (live masterclass session)

## Core thesis
Substack is not another social feed. It is the first media platform built only to create a paid subscription relationship between writer and reader, sitting on an ocean of people already logged in with credit cards used to paying for newsletters. The Notes feed is "2020 Twitter" again, an early-mover discovery engine, and following on Notes hands you an email subscriber by default. Set it up fast, promote it brutally, and treat it as a publication to build, not a list to spam.

## Key frameworks / rules

- **Notes follow = free email subscriber.** When someone follows you on Notes (the Twitter clone inside Substack), they are added as a subscriber to your publication by default. Social follow and email opt-in are the same action. This is why Notes is the discovery engine.
- **Notes is "2020 Twitter."** First movers who put high-quality, effort-posted long-form into Notes will ride the growth wave before the platform gets crowded. The Culturist: Notes is now "by far the biggest source" of new subscribers, more than X, Instagram, and TikTok combined.
- **Your Notes profile must mirror your publication.** A reader scrolling Notes who lands on your profile should know exactly what your paid publication delivers. The profile page is a billboard for the publication, nothing more.
- **The launch post is not the welcome page.** Your first published post should be a one-page explainer emailed to the whole list: what the publication is, how often you send, what free vs paid gets, the cadence. Everyone joining Substack should send this big launch explainer post.
- **Segment the homepage into buckets / custom tabs.** Build the front page into named sections (podcast, book club, free newsletter, paid deep-dives) and add genre tabs across the top. Custom tabs can even link to an external site (your store, your other offers). A bucketed page reads like a real resource, not a feed. All content is evergreen, so readers scroll the archive and find everything.
- **Deep, unfinished back-catalog kills churn (stated indirectly, reinforced from the value angle).** Loading old recordings, interviews, and archive content gives paid readers a reason to stay.
- **Annual beats monthly, always push it.** "Would you rather make nine bucks right now or ninety?" Yearly subscribers are the bread and butter and the people you upsell to high-ticket. Monthlies "kick tires," see the bill, stick around 4-5 months, then bail. Annual locks them in for a year.
- **People quit from boredom, not lack of value.** "It's never because you didn't give them enough value. It's because they got bored." Retention is an engagement problem, not a value problem.
- **Discount the annual hard to make it enticing.** Don't price annual as ~10 months of monthly (too small a gap). Give roughly 3 months free as the standing rule, 6 months free for major promos, even 9 months free for the biggest push. The Culturist gives 3 months free standard.
- **Price to the genre and the outcome, not the average.** Generic art/history Substacks run ~$5/mo. Anything that can make the reader money (or transform them) charges comfortably above average; $10/mo is "the floor." The Culturist runs $8/mo.
- **Founding tier = golden ticket bundle.** Set the founding membership price equal to your high-ticket community/course price (example floated: $500), and have it unlock everything on Substack PLUS the key to your higher product (a free link to the masterclass/community). You sell the high-ticket through Substack's founding tier.
- **Grandfather pricing.** You can change price anytime; existing subscribers keep their old rate. So a launch-day discount ($5 today, double tomorrow) is safe.
- **Substack fee is ~10%** (called "about the same as Gumroad," a little less). No approval friction; paid tiers go live instantly, unlike X's subscriber-approval process.
- **Migration play: import every list you own.** Export from Gumroad, Mailchimp, beehiiv, ConvertKit and import to Substack. Only import people who opted in (a real list), or send them a list and invite them rather than force-subscribe (gray area legally). New signups on the old platform get manually re-imported every week or month. First import may take up to 12-24 hours to approve; later imports clear in ~10 minutes.
- **Don't kill the old email address, repurpose it by register.** Three channels, three jobs: old Gumroad/list = pure hard-sell, no value, big arrows, "buy this." Free Substack = light value + storytelling + hard CTA at the end. Paid Substack = value, value, value, soft mention at the end. Warm the old list before migrating ("last email from this address; future ones come from [name]@substack.com"), then phase the old one out over ~6 months.
- **Free email rating is built in and free.** Substack scores every subscriber by open/engagement and auto-prompts your most-engaged free readers to upgrade ("become paid, here's 10% off"). ConvertKit/Mailchimp charge ~$1,000/mo to run engagement scoring on 150k contacts; Substack does it for free. You still own the list and can export anytime.
- **Lead-magnet bundle in the bio.** Bio/CTA should be a short second-person caption selling the join, and can bundle a freebie: "join to get my free PDF on XYZ."
- **Set up fast, fix later.** Don't stall on logos, Canva, perfect bio. Do the big things first: fill the blanks, set pricing, send the launch email, get recommendations. "I'm gonna make a hundred mistakes" is the right posture on day one.

## Growth engine: recommendations + leaderboard

- **The recommendation network is the core growth loop.** When you subscribe to a publication you're prompted to recommend it. When someone subscribes to you, Substack shows them up to three publications you recommend (random pick from your list). Recommendations are reciprocal currency: "go paid and I'll recommend you back."
- **Write a sentence for each recommendation.** No limit on how many you recommend, but recommendations with a written sentence get prioritized over bare ones. Always write the one-line reason.
- **Going viral inside Substack.** A single banger article can go viral in the Substack app and pull ~1,000 new subs off one post. The ecosystem amplifies, not just your own list.
- **Study the leaderboard before you write.** Go to substack.com → Explore → your category (Business, Finance, etc.). Two lists: **Rising** = most annual recurring revenue added in the last 24 hours. **Top / Best Sellers** = overall ARR ranking. Heavy overlap between them. The ranking is revenue-based, not subscriber-count (a publication with 159,000 subscribers ranked #91 because rank tracks ARR).
- **Purple checkmark = 10,000+ paid subscribers.** Use it to spot who is actually printing money and reverse-engineer them.
- **Target the category leader.** Pick the best account in your niche, study their content, then build a more exciting / differently-angled version. (Their example: target Justin Welsh, "kind of dry, no offense," beat him on energy and a purpose angle.)
- **Day-one game plan:** import every owned list → email all of them today announcing the Substack → announce in every community/Discord/group chat you're in → set up recommendations and trade them with peers → study category leaders on the leaderboard.

## Hook / reply / launch patterns (brand-adapted)

- **The launch explainer post** (every new Substack sends this, emailed to the whole list):
  - "Here is what The Biblical Man is. Here is how often I send. Here is what you get free. Here is what opens up when you go paid."
  - Brand version intro: "I taught Sunday school 17 years and could not defend one verse under fire. This publication is where I hand you the blade. Free: a hard word, most weeks. Paid: the verses your modern translation gutted, and how to answer for them."
- **The migration warm-up email** (sent to an old list before/while moving it):
  - "We're moving the list. This is one of the last emails from this address. Going forward it comes from Dead Hidden on Substack. Same fight, sharper sword. If you opened these, follow me over."
- **The recommendation sentence** (written for every reciprocal recommendation, prioritized over bare ones):
  - "Subscribe to him. He will make you a better Christian. You will learn the faith and become a wiser, harder-to-fool man."
- **The reciprocal-recommendation ask** (run during a launch or Space):
  - "Go paid and I'll recommend you back. Paid only. I'm setting the bar higher."
- **The founding-tier pitch** (golden ticket):
  - "Founding members get the full archive, every paid drop, and the key to the Vault. One door, everything behind it."

## Brand application (Adam's voice)

The lesson under all of this: Substack is land before the rush. Get on it now. Stop polishing the logo.

For The Biblical Man and Follow Me, the moves that matter:

- Treat Notes like X in 2020. Effort-post the discernment hooks there daily. Every follow is an email on the list you own. That is free.
- Send the launch explainer this week. One page. What you are, the cadence, free vs paid, the founding door.
- Build the homepage into tabs: the free word, the paid drops, the archive, the Vault. A reader should know in three seconds what they get free and what opens when they pay.
- Push annual, not monthly. Monthlies see the bill and bail. Give three months free and lock them for a year. Remember the real reason men quit: boredom, not thin value. Keep the back-catalog deep and the next drop coming.
- Make the founding tier the golden ticket. Founding price = Vault price. One door, everything behind it.
- Trade recommendations with three to five discernment-adjacent writers. Write the sentence every time. Bare recommendations get buried.
- Import every list you own. Warm them, move them, phase out the old address over six months.
- Study the Rising and Best Sellers leaderboard in Faith/Religion before you write. Rank is revenue, not headcount. Find the purple checkmarks and reverse-engineer them.
