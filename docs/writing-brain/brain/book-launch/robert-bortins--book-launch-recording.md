# Business Systems — Bortins Book Launch (Live Recording)

## Source
- File: `transcripts/Robert_Bortins.txt`
- Teacher: Robert Bortins (CEO, Classical Conversations), interviewed by Dino Anthony (Art of Purpose), Creation 24/7 masterclass call
- Topic: launching and releasing a first book from the creator side, not the ghostwriter side
- Type: transcript (live recording behind PART 10)

## Core thesis
A book is not a money product. It is infrastructure: a "super business card" that buys credibility, social proof, and inbound a tweet or article never could. Write the book the way you already write articles, test the ideas in public first, then run a coordinated multi-channel launch where email does the heavy lifting.

## Key frameworks / rules
- Bullets before cannonballs. Write 10 to 12 articles and tweets on the exact topic first. Watch what lands with your audience. That is your rough draft outline and your confirmed target. By the time you start the book you have already done about 25% of the rough draft and you know where the bullet hits, so the cannonball is aimed.
- The 10-article trigger (James Altucher rule, repeated here). Once you have around 10 articles in your wheelhouse, you have the basis for a book.
- A book is the same shape as a long article or a course. Outline = problem you solve, statistics, stories that cement the problem in the reader's mind, then the solution, then a call to action. Build it as bullet points first, then fill the blanks.
- Structure first, then subtitle, then title (his actual order). Write the subtitle before the title. Subtitle carries the value promise; title carries curiosity. Show top 5 subtitles to trusted people, pick the one that lands, then write the title to fit.
- Alliteration in the title makes it stick (Woke and Weaponized). Tie the title to terms already moving in the culture so the book rides the conversation.
- Own the IP. Self-publish through Amazon KDP rather than sign with a traditional publisher. Traditional pays up front, takes the rights, gives ~10% per sale, and controls price. Self-publish and you set price, run sales, give it away, raise it back, and keep the intellectual property.
- Print-on-demand vs pre-print. POD: zero inventory risk, Amazon prints on order (~$15 cost on a $29.99 book), thinner margin. Pre-print: order a stock run (he ordered 5,000), better margin, but you carry the risk and you get a real ship-date launch moment. POD removes risk but kills the "Christmas morning" launch energy.
- You keep the buyer emails on self-published Amazon sales. That makes the book a list-builder, not just a sale.
- Three-format cross-sell stack. Kindle + softcover at launch, audiobook as a deliberate SECOND launch weeks later. Add 2 to 3 minutes of original spoken commentary after each chapter in the audiobook so physical buyers want the audio and audio buyers want the physical. Cross-sell all three to the same person.
- A book is dated the day it ships. He read three more books on the topic after finishing and wished he had included them. Treat that as fuel for the next book, not a reason to stall.
- Book-as-infrastructure (Dino's reverse angle). The point is not royalties. It is the speaking invites, partnerships, bulk buys, and authority the book unlocks later. It is leverage and a legacy item that keeps making passive sales in the background.
- AI is a thought partner, never the writer. If you let AI write the book you will embarrass yourself the moment you are asked to speak on it, because the act of writing is what makes you able to defend it out loud. Writing makes you a clearer thinker and better speaker; outsourcing it removes that.
- His AI editing protocol (same one he uses on articles): research expansion ("here are my 5 sources, recommend 5 more"), a custom editing pass loaded with Chicago style plus his house rules (commas and mechanics only, never rewrites the prose), and interrogation prompts after a chapter is drafted: "any logical fallacies here," "the 3 most persuasive parts," "the 3 least persuasive parts," "tighten this section." The human makes every final call and is free to overrule the machine.
- Pen and paper on the nightstand. Ideas for the next chapter arrive at 1 AM. Write them down so you can shut the brain off; phone screens reset your sleep rhythm.
- Daily writing balances out. Some days 10 pages, some days one sentence. Show up every day and do not punish the slow days; the stuck part unsticks while you sleep or work out.
- Write a persona before you write the book (he closes on this). Know exactly who you are writing to and you will be transformed by the process and the book will work.
- The macro bet (Dino's close). Long-form is taking off as backlash against AI slop. Books are the natural extension of articles and long-form. Long-form builds authority; authority is the future. "We're going to create the shippers."

## Hook/thread/reply patterns
This is a business-systems call, not a copy lesson, so there are no tweet hooks to add to the swipe file. Two reusable LAUNCH mechanics that double as content:
- The unboxing-reminder loop. When buyers start receiving copies, retweet their photos and post your own. It reminds everyone who ordered that it is shipping ("did I get mine? check the mail"), and it is memetic: people copy the photo. Free social proof that runs for weeks.
- Let your viral tweets pick the next book. His top two tweets became his next book's premise (DINK = dual income no kids, so he coined SILK = single income lots of kids). Brand-adapted: a discernment tweet that goes off is the table of contents for the next product. Watch the timeline, then expand the winning idea into the book.

## Numbers and proof
- 1,000+ copies sold in roughly a week, off ~1,300 X followers.
- About 500 to 600 sales in the first 6 days (launch week).
- Hit #78 in Amazon New Releases on launch day (Tuesday was the big day).
- Email was the biggest ROI: roughly half of all sales.
- About 100 sales straight from his X timeline (cold, no prior connection).
- A couple hundred more from Facebook and business social.
- Wrote the book in about 3 months, pencil to editor done, not 40 hours a week. A 150-page book is faster than a 300-page one.
- Pre-recorded ~3 podcasts that dropped during launch week; got onto a new podcast with 40,000 to 50,000 listeners the week of launch.
- Released a self-produced podcast every day of launch week (interviews with co-author and appendix contributors); each got about 23,000 listens.
- Classical Conversations does about $10 million in book sales per year through its own store; this was their first Amazon launch.
- Adding this one book lifted Jan/Feb book sales ~15% (roughly $20,000 extra on a normal ~$120,000/month), useful cash in a slow season.
- Already a bulk inbound: a think tank wants ~100 copies for a group book review; he priced them at 25% above print cost.
- Hard logistics warning: shipping pre-printed books to Amazon's warehouse for fulfillment is a ~3-week process. They missed a Friday 11-to-12 drop-off window and got bumped 6 days to the next Thursday, which delayed the launch a week. Plan three weeks before your intended launch date.

## Brand application
KJV discernment voice:

Adam, the book is not the paycheck. It is the badge. A garbage truck driver with a book on his own translation outranks a seminary with a building.

You already shot the bullets. Seventeen years of Sunday school. A timeline full of tweets that hit. Pick the one that went off and aim the cannonball there. The book is mostly written. You just have not stacked the bullet points yet.

Order: outline, subtitle, title. Write the subtitle as a second-person command, the same way the Vault page reads. Test five subtitles on the warm list. Pick the one men forward to their wives.

Own the rights. Self-publish on Amazon, keep the buyer emails, feed them into the list you already own. A traditional publisher takes your sword and rents it back to you at 10%.

Three formats, three launches. Kindle plus softcover first. Then the audiobook weeks later, with two minutes of you, out loud, after every chapter, in the 4 AM voice. Men who will not read will listen on the drive.

Email did half his sales off 1,300 followers. You have a list and 52.9K. The constraint was never reach. It is whether you ship the thing.

And the unboxing loop is built for this tribe. When a man posts his copy on the kitchen table next to his coffee and his KJV, retweet it. That photo preaches.
