# Book Subtitles: 99 in 27 Minutes

## Source
- File: MC26 The Art and Science of Book Subtitles.txt
- Teacher: Joshua Lisec (@JoshuaLisec). Guide compiled by Robert Bortins (@TheRobertBShow).
- Topic: How to write 99 book subtitles in 27 minutes, even with no title yet.

## Core thesis
People buy a book for one of three reasons: functional, emotional, financial. The title's only job is curiosity; the subtitle's job is to close the deal by naming the value. Write the subtitle first, generate volume, then choose.

## Key frameworks / rules
- The three buying reasons:
  - Functional: help me do something or solve a problem. Reader asks "What will I be able to do after reading this?"
  - Emotional: help me feel something or become someone. Reader asks "How will I feel different?"
  - Financial: help me save or make money. Reader asks "What's the ROI of my time and money?"
- Title vs subtitle split (most authors get this backwards):
  - Title = hook. Curiosity and intrigue, like a newspaper headline. Can be mysterious, clever, provocative. Does not explain everything. ("Atomic Habits," "Thinking, Fast and Slow," "The Obstacle Is the Way")
  - Subtitle = value proposition. Specific. Promises transformation. Answers "Why should I care?" ("An Easy & Proven Way to Build Good Habits & Break Bad Ones," "The Timeless Art of Turning Trials into Triumph")
- The 99 process:
  1. Spreadsheet with three columns: Functional, Emotional, Financial.
  2. For each column answer: What do I want the book to do? What will they learn? What will they be able to do? How does this make life easier/better/richer? What problem does it solve? What transformation does it enable?
  3. Write every answer as a second-person command. Commands paint the outcome so the reader pictures the win. ("Lose 30 pounds in 90 days," "Build a business that runs without you," "Find peace in the midst of chaos.")
  4. Once you have 30+ per column, mix and match across columns.
  5. Test top 10-15 on real people. Ask: "What do you think this book is about? Would you read this? What do you expect to get?"
- Best practices:
  - Clarity over cleverness. The subtitle is not the place for wordplay. If a reader does not instantly get it, rewrite.
  - Specificity sells. "Reverse type 2 diabetes through intermittent fasting" beats "Improve your health."
  - Power words: time-based ("In 30 days," "Step-by-step," "Quick," "Simple"); transformation ("Transform," "Master," "Discover," "Unlock"); results ("Proven," "Practical," "Complete," "Essential").
  - Length: subtitles can run longer than titles, 10-15 words is common, but every word earns its place. Graspable in 3-5 seconds.
  - Pitfalls to avoid: vague promises ("Change your life"), insider jargon, multiple ideas in one subtitle, false promises.
- Classical mapping: functional = logos, emotional = pathos, financial = ethos. Speak to the whole person.

## Hook / offer patterns
- Mix-and-match combinations (the engine of the 99):
  - Functional + Emotional: "Master public speaking and conquer your fear"
  - Functional + Financial: "Build mobile apps and earn $10K/month"
  - Emotional + Financial: "Find fulfilling work that pays what you're worth"
  - All three: "Transform your body, reclaim your confidence, and save thousands on healthcare"
- KJV-discernment brand-adapted (The Biblical Man):
  - Functional: "Test every spirit and walk in plain Bible discernment"
  - Emotional: "Stop fearing deception and stand unshaken on the Word"
  - Title plus subtitle: title "Familiar Spirits" / subtitle "How to Spot the Counterfeit, Try the Spirits, and Hold Fast the King James Bible"
- Scar-tissue brand-adapted (Follow Me):
  - Emotional: "Lay down the mask and follow Him through the wreckage"
  - Functional + Emotional: "Confess the thing you hide and walk free"
- Apply the order rule to product pages and offer headlines too: name the value plainly (subtitle job) before getting clever with the name (title job).

## Numbers and proof
- 99 subtitles in 27 minutes. Set a timer for 27 minutes and write without editing or judging.
- Aim for 99 options. First idea is rarely the best; quantity leads to quality.
- 30+ options per column before mixing.
- Test the top 5 (action steps) / read top 10-15 to others (process step).
- Subtitle length 10-15 words common; grasp in 3-5 seconds.
- Action sequence: do not write the title first, fill the three columns, 27-minute timer, 99 options, let it sit overnight, test top 5, then choose the title.

## Brand application (Adam's voice)
- Write the promise before the poetry. The clever title can come last. First answer: what does the reader walk away able to do, feel, or keep.
- Three columns work for any Dead Hidden or Biblical Man product, not just books. Substack post titles, lead magnets, the Vault offer, paid upgrade copy.
- Commands fit the voice. Short. Direct. Second person. "Try the spirits." "Burn the idol." "Read the King James and believe it."
- Specificity is discernment in copy form. Name the exact counterfeit, the exact wound, the exact verse. Vague is the slop tell.
- Honest communication, not manipulation. The subtitle is an act of service: the right reader finds the right book at the right time.
