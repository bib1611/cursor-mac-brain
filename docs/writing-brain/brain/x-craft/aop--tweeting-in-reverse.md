# Tweeting in Reverse — The Emotion Quadrant

## Source
- File: `transcripts/Tweeting_in_Reverse.txt`
- Teacher: AOP (host; Lesson 7 of a cohort course, references CPP students and a prior Lesson 6 from "Al and Jaguya")
- Topic: How to engineer viral tweets by choosing the emotion first, before the template. The "emotion quadrant" and "tweeting in reverse."
- Type: transcript

## Core thesis
Viral tweets are not built from templates or memes. They are built from an idea that makes people feel a specific emotion, because feeling drives the reaction (like, comment, quote tweet, bookmark, retweet). "Tweeting in reverse" means you decide the target emotion first, then build the tweet backward to land it. A tweet that makes the reader feel nothing produces nothing.

## Key frameworks / rules
- **Tweet in reverse.** Do not start from a template or "is it formatted right." Start from one question: "What emotion is this tweet going to convey?" Build the tweet to hit that emotion. The template is a starting point only; the reaction is what makes a post work.
- **The emotion quadrant (Watson-style axis).** Two axes: positive vs negative emotion, and high activation vs low activation (deactivation). Borrowed from Dr. John B. Watson (Johns Hopkins, the conditioned-fear "white rabbit" experiment; later took testimonials to Madison Avenue advertising). Each quadrant pulls a different reaction:
  - **Happiness / high positive** -> likes. Pleasant, agreeable ideas. The reader nods, agrees, gives a like.
  - **Anger, upset, unpleasant / high negative** -> quote tweets and comments. The reader has to resolve the felt emotion, so they fire back. Heaviest comment driver.
  - **Surprise / alert** -> retweets. Also pairs with the high-positive "look at this" share.
  - **Deactivation / calm, serene, low activation** -> likes plus bookmarks. The reader feels calm and good, saves it.
- **A bad tweet hits no quadrant.** It is "just a statement," talking AT the audience, with nothing felt. No feeling means no transfer, no attention, no reaction.
- **Rotate quadrants on purpose.** Do not be the anger/disgust guy every day, or people tune you out. Be the surprise guy, the deactivation guy, the happy guy, on rotation. "You can't be a pitcher and throw only fastballs. You got to have a curveball."
- **Deactivation alone does not sell.** A pure aesthetic/calm page is fine if it matches your purpose, but it will rarely get anyone to buy. If you want people to hand you money, you have to make them feel. Choose this trade consciously.
- **The conduit / access point.** Viral tweets get the reader onto the emotional axis fast by naming something the reader identifies a piece of themselves with: a celebrity, a TV show, an addiction, a brand, a trending event. The named thing is the conduit onto the axis. Generic phrasing has no conduit and bombs.
- **Specific named brand beats generic category.** "McDonald's sells 6.5 million burgers per day" goes viral; "fast food restaurants sell 20 million burgers a day" does not. The brand is the conduit; the category is not. People have a visceral reaction to the brand, not the abstraction.
- **Read the comments of viral tweets** to see exactly which emotion got activated. The comment section is the proof of which quadrant fired.
- **Refrigerator-magnet effect.** When something is so valuable people want to keep it, they retweet to bookmark it for themselves (most people on X are consumers, not creators). High-value how-to content gets retweeted as a save, not just as a share.
- **Strong/polarizing language widens the spread.** "All of them suck. You deserve better." Polarized activation hits both sides: some elated and agreeing, some angry and disgusted. Both react.
- **Contrast as an axis driver.** Put two opposed images or ideas side by side (twerk vs Ave Maria, left vs right). Contrast forces a reaction from everyone and gets them onto the axis.
- **Ride the trend while it is hot.** Build the contrast/celebrity tweet while the event is top of mind (within days). The same meme posted cold, after the moment passes, will not perform. Keep the template ready, drop in the photo everyone is already talking about.
- **Bragging with no ego: spin the W into "you."** You need to share wins so the audience buys, but never make it "me, me, me" on the axis. Reframe the win as a gift: "I did this. This is for you." Makes the reader feel alert plus happy (high positive).
- **Be careful with pure trauma-dump threads.** "All these bad things happened to me" threads can be powerful, but keep the focus on the audience and the lesson they take, not on you.
- **The internet is a giant shit test. Whenever you get angry, you lose.** You will feel upset, but never show it on the timeline. Making people laugh is a superpower; humor pulls atheists and outsiders into your stuff and that means you are winning. Edutainment makes more money than instruction.

## Hook / thread / offer patterns (with example wording)
- **Move the strongest emotional item into the hook.** Original list line buried at #1 in a thread becomes the hook itself. Example given: the fitness thread opened by naming alcohol (an addiction = conduit) instead of the flat "Every piece of fitness advice I could come up with after six years in the gym," which would have bombed.
  - KJV-discernment adaptation: lead the thread with the most charged sin or idol by name, not a tidy summary. "The first thing I cut to walk with God again: the bottle." Then the thread.
- **Visceral-brand hook + bookmark CTA.** "[Brand] sells [exact number] [units] per day. All of them suck. You deserve better. You can make [better outcome] by doing these [N] things. Bookmark this for your next [occasion]." The brand is the conduit; the body is the real lesson (how to cook a burger), the brand just gets people on the axis.
  - KJV-discernment adaptation: "Your church sells comfort by the gallon. Most of it is poison. You can build a faith that survives the fire by doing these four things. Bookmark this before Sunday."
- **Side-by-side contrast image + stakes question.** Two opposed photos, then a question that taps the feeling: "Do you see what's at stake in [thing]?" Drives alert/tense + quote tweets. Example: "At [X] they twerk. At [Y] they perform Ave Maria." Half a million views.
  - KJV-discernment adaptation: "One altar calls it self-expression. One altar calls it the fear of the Lord. Do you see what's at stake for your sons?"
- **Follow a viral tweet with a poll** to extend the conversation. Example: "Who do you trust more with the future of [thing]?" (~1,000 votes in the example.)
- **Nod-of-recognition hook (likes engine).** Make the reader feel "that's me." Gets likes, not comments. Example given: "Are you normal? Or are you an extremist?" with a relatable image. People shake their head yes and like it.
  - KJV-discernment adaptation: "Are you a churchgoer? Or are you a disciple?" + image. Built for agreement-likes.
- **Win-as-gift offer.** Share a result, then flip it to the reader. Example: a "we sold over 1 million" tweet wired so a like fires an auto-DM to a lead magnet. The framing stays on "you," which keeps it high-positive instead of self-promotional. (Reported a $30 tip and a follower bump off this style.)
- **Guarantee-DM CTA on a thread.** Example tail used: "P.S. If there was a 100% chance you'd lose 30 pounds by Christmas, would you take it? DM me '90 days' and I'll guarantee it." Reported ~1,000 DMs.

## Numbers and proof
- Fitness/alcohol thread: "made like 40, $50,000," 33,000 retweets, 100,000 bookmarks, ~1,000 DMs from the guarantee CTA, ~10,000 followers gained. Called "one of the most epic tweets of all time."
- Twerk vs Ave Maria contrast tweet: ~500,000 views (half a million), posted within four days of the trending moment.
- Follow-up poll: ~1,000 votes.
- PewDiePie / royals "biggest decision is who you marry" contrast meme: 1.6 million views.
- "Are you normal or are you an extremist?" tweet: a lot of likes, few comments (likes-quadrant proof).
- Humor/meme tweet: 1.7 million views, ~1,000 comments, ~1,000 followers gained.
- Calm/serene tweet (deactivation example): ~2,400 likes "yesterday."
- AOP's claim of method, not luck: "everything I've learned from four years of teaching and 20 years of studying psychology," denies bots, retweet trades, and engagement groups.
- Ben Settle parallel: writes emails by asking "how do I want people to feel today?" first, template second.

## Brand application (Adam's voice)
- Before any tweet, Note, or email, name the target emotion first. One word. Then write backward to land it. This is the Watson axis applied to KJV discernment.
- Map the offer ladder to the quadrant: anger/conviction tweets drive the quote-tweet fights that grow reach; nod-of-recognition tweets ("that's me") bank likes; surprise/contrast tweets earn shares; calm scripture posts earn bookmarks and saves. Rotate. Do not be the rage-bait prophet seven days a week, or the timeline tunes you out.
- The conduit for this audience is named idols and named compromises: the megachurch, the watered translation, the bottle, the phone, the fatherless home, the trending cultural event. Name the specific thing the reader carries a piece of themselves in. Generic "the church today" has no conduit.
- Conviction without contempt. The internet is a shit test; visible anger loses. Land the hard truth, then make them feel the way back. Confession and the occasional clean laugh pull in the lost, and that is winning.
- Wins are gifts. When sharing a result (subs, a transformed reader, a launch), flip it to "this is the path open to you," never "look at me." Keeps it high-positive and sells without the stink of ego.
- Use the calm scripture post as the curveball after a run of high-negative conviction posts. The serene KJV line lands harder because it breaks the rhythm.
