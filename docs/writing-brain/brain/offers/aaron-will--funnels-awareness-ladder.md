# Aaron Will — Funnels, Nano Problems, and the Awareness Ladder

## Source
- File: `transcripts/AARON_WILL.txt`
- Teacher: Aaron Will (~285K followers on X), interviewed by Dino (Art of Purpose), Creation 24/7 / Masterclass 24/7
- Topic: stop chasing the algorithm, build funnels off content and newsletter, break problems into nano problems, move readers up the five-stage awareness ladder
- Type: transcript (spaces/call recording, ~1 hour)

## Core thesis
Likes are not the business. Authority and owned distribution are. Build a personal brand whose only job is to move a reader from "I have a problem" up the awareness ladder to "this person sells the solution, take my money." You do that with value content, a newsletter, and an email sequence that educates before it sells. Aaron's revenue did not drop when his engagement got cut 60 to 70 percent, because the money was never in the engagement.

## Key frameworks / rules

- **The two entrance points.** Every funnel inside a personal brand has exactly two doors: content and newsletter. Both are value. Their one job is to educate a reader and walk him to the next level of awareness. There can be many funnels inside one brand, all ending at the same place: get on a call, get in the DMs, or close in the email.
- **The five stages of awareness (Eugene Schwartz, Breakthrough Advertising).** Unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, offer-aware (Aaron calls it "most aware" / product-aware in the chain), ready. Most readers arriving from content sit at level two or three (problem-aware / solution-aware). The whole engine is just: figure out where a reader is, then write the next rung. (Dino names the source on the recording: "this is copywriting, legendary, Eugene Schwartz, Breakthrough Advertising.")
- **The job of content is the bottom rungs.** Aaron only works the unaware, problem-aware, and solution-aware levels with public content. Content names a problem and shows a solution, but never the full how. It places you as the authority in the niche so that when someone asks "who's the go-to guy for landing pages," your name is the answer.
- **The job of the newsletter is depth.** Same value as content, but more in depth. The newsletter exists to capture the email (you own it, you do not own your X page) and then run the nurture sequence.
- **The job of the email sequence is to make them ready before you sell.** Lead magnet first, then educational emails that solve common problems, then you walk them problem-aware to solution-aware to offer-aware. Only when they hit "ready" do the emotional / sales emails go out. Most people sign up a reader and immediately blast sales emails. Not wrong, just inefficient. Get everyone as ready as possible first because then the close is easy.
- **Nano problems (Aaron's own framework, coined ~2 years ago).** Every big problem is built from a stack of smaller problems. "I'm overweight" is not one problem; it is not enough steps, eating too much, an illness, fear of the gym, no time. Take your 3 to 5 big audience problems, then break each into as many nano problems as you can. Each nano problem is one piece of value content. This is how you produce endlessly without repeating "here's how to lose weight" until people get bored. Twenty nano problems under "he's broke" gives you twenty non-repeating posts.
- **Solve the problem, but not all the way (the apple-on-the-fishing-rod rule).** Give real, useful, surface-level solutions. Do NOT go so deep the reader can fully fix it himself, because then there's no reason to pay you. Hang the apple in front of him close enough to want it, far enough that he can't reach it without you. The leak: creators who post so much in-depth value they teach the audience to not need them, then attract a crowd of broke freeloaders.
- **Solve one problem, expose a bigger one (Dino's add-on).** Best lead magnets solve a problem and at the end introduce a new, bigger problem whose solution is your offer. Dino's "Tweetfluence" taught networking, then revealed the real fix is having a crew, which is the community. Solve, then expose the next gap.
- **The "number four" value pattern (Dino).** Give two or three full solutions, then say the best one is number four and show it. Or list solutions and gate the strongest behind the offer. Proves you can solve the big problem (believability) without handing the whole thing away.
- **Inbound over outbound.** Aaron almost never cold-pitches. Clients come from emails, DMs, and referrals because the content built the authority. "It's much better to have people coming through your door than you going out to them." He only does cold for big (three-month) deals.
- **Stop obsessing over the algorithm.** The algorithm always changes; nobody can stop it. The fundamentals never change: post content people enjoy, leave comments, build relationships. When something stops working, do not cry, research what changed (ask AI), adjust, keep going. Becoming a slave to the numbers is the trap. Aaron's first algorithm change scared him; Dino told him to just keep posting; persisting through it is what taught him the numbers were never the point.
- **Authority beats reach.** The revenue test: Aaron's engagement got cut 60 to 70 percent and revenue did not drop. Dino's account got throttled ~70 percent and he made more money, because the throttle forced him to learn to sell, DM, and close instead of leaning on raw engagement.

## The first 90 days (no funnel, ~1,000 followers)

1. **Mindset first.** It's a long process, not an overnight. Do not chase money in week one. The first couple of months are about getting a foot in the door and taking up digital real estate.
2. **Pick the target audience from your skill.** Take what you can do (build websites, train people in the gym) and decide exactly who you help. Web designer to agencies. Fitness coach to busy CEOs and founders who think they need the gym 24/7.
3. **Find their problems.** Pick 3 to 5 things that audience struggles with that you can fix (conversion rates suck, weak copy, broken structure / no time, wrong beliefs).
4. **Set the offer simply.** "I help [audience] [result] with [what you do]."
5. **Break problems into nano problems** for the content engine.
6. **Set the content split: value + personal.** Value content = nano problems solved at surface level. Personal content = opinions and takes (gym, relationships, your own life). Stay away from politics, gender, and touchy subjects; one wrong quote-tweet from the wrong person gets you piled on for nothing.
7. **Engagement, kept simple.** Follow people whose content you genuinely enjoy, not just your target audience. Scroll occasionally, form an opinion in your head, reply. The trick: pretend you've known this person forever and you're texting a friend. Don't overthink comments.
8. **Then just post and comment every single day** for as long as you can, building the foundation. Once the foundation is set, introduce offers, then new funnels.

## Volume / "be seen everywhere"
- The biggest beginner mistake: not understanding you need to be seen everywhere. Two or three tweets a day and "nobody sees me" is no mystery: the algorithm first shows a post to ~10 percent of your followers, then expands only if early engagement is good. Few posts = self-throttling.
- "Increase the volume of everything you do. More content, more comments, more DMs, more newsletters, more products, more service. The aim is to take up as much digital real estate as possible. You should be everywhere at all times. Imprint your name." (Aaron's tweet, quoted on the call.)
- Caveat: pump out a high quality and quantity, don't spray 50 junk tweets an hour for the sake of it.

## Email patterns that convert (highest reply / conversion)

- **Below-the-surface breakdowns.** Take a real reader problem ("my conversion rate is set, how do I fix it") and write a genuine step-by-step that solves it at surface level, deliberately not deep enough for them to fully execute alone. Step one, step two, breaking the problem down. They try, hit the wall, come back.
- **Case studies as inside-secrets, not brags.** Not "I helped this guy make an extra 10K a month." Instead: this person struggled with X, Y, Z; we did A, B, C; here's the result. The reader thinks "I'm struggling with that too, maybe I'll try A," tries it, can't pull it off, reaches out.
- **Personal / story emails convert the lowest tier.** Dino notes his personal "here's what I did" emails convert mostly to his cheapest offer (his $8/month paid Substack), the warm "I like this guy" crowd, not the serious buyers.
- The goal of all of it: a DM that opens "I've been loving your content" or "this tweet really helped me, what do you do" — inbound, pre-sold by the sequence.

## Substack as a second entrance point (Dino's pitch to Aaron)
- An all-inclusive, smaller but active social site. Import your existing emails, email your list from there, and publish those emails as articles at the same time. Email + blog + a notes feed (Notes) that picks up new subscribers.
- The one-click subscribe button next to your name in Notes collects emails instantly — like an X with a built-in subscribe-and-capture button. Nearly eliminates the need for a lead magnet or auto-DMs to collect emails.
- You can run a paid newsletter as a low rung of the funnel (e.g. ~$5).
- Reuse: copy-paste existing X articles straight onto Substack.

## Brand application (Adam's voice)
You already own the front of this funnel. 52.9K on X. ~50K subscribers across the brands. The leak is the middle and the back.

Pick the awareness rung before you write. A man who's never doubted his Bible is unaware. A man who feels something is off with his ESV is problem-aware. A man who's heard "the modern versions cut verses" is solution-aware. The man asking "which Bible do I buy for my son" is ready. Write the next rung, not the rung he's already on.

Nano problems. "Modern Bibles are corrupt" is not one post. It's fifty. Acts 8:37 missing. 1 John 5:7 gutted. The footnote that says "earliest manuscripts lack." The pastor who skips it. The study Bible note that doubts the verse. Each garbled verse is one post. You will never run dry and you will never repeat.

Solve, but hang the apple. Show him the verse his Bible deleted. Don't hand him the whole manuscript argument for free. The full case is the Plain Bible Manual, the Vault. Surface in the post, depth in the offer.

The Biblical Man content names the problem in his Bible. Follow Me content is the scar tissue — your own. Keep the split: discernment teaching and personal wound. Stay off the third-rail politics that gets you piled on for nothing.

Newsletter does the nurture. Most readers get a free PDF and then a sales blast. Wrong order. Educate them up the ladder first — "here's the verse, here's why it's gone, here's what they told you, here's who told you" — then the offer email lands on a man who's already ready.

Substack is already your engine. The Notes subscribe button is the one-click capture Dino is describing. You're living the pitch. Run the paid tier as the low rung, the Vault as the high rung.

And stop reading the like count like Scripture. Engagement is not the harvest. Authority is. The man who DMs "which Bible do I buy" never showed up in your impressions.
