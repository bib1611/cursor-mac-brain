# Harrison Schenck: The Five-Part Offer Framework (build, price, validate before you build)

## Source
- File: `transcripts/OFFERS_LESSON__for_Masterclass_24_7.txt`
- Teacher: Harrison Schenck (Savior's Sons), partner with AOP (Art of Purpose); teaches client offer-building inside their coaching/consulting program and the CPP students
- Topic: how to build and price an offer for a service, product, coaching, or consulting package sold online, and how to validate it before doing any of the build work
- Type: transcript (presentation)

## Core thesis
An offer is the outcome your prospect gets in exchange for their money. Nothing more. Define that outcome with five fill-in-the-blank parts, then go sell it before you build anything. The five parts are not just structure; each one is a price lever. Get them right and you cannot go wrong.

## Key frameworks / rules

**Definition first.** An offer is the outcome your prospect gets in exchange for their money. It is what happens to or for the prospect after they pay you. The more specific the outcome, the better off you are. Stop being mysterious about it.

**The five-part offer.** Every offer is exactly five components, no more, no less. Fill in the blank for each one and you have a pitchable offer in minutes:
1. The big outcome
2. The length of time / time commitment
3. The medium of delivery
4. The guarantee
5. The price

**Part 1, the big outcome (the most important part).** It must do two things at once: be specific and measurable, and solve an acute pain.
- Specific and measurable: "I'm gonna help you lose 30 pounds," not "I'm gonna make you better." There is zero doubt at the end whether it happened. It is not open for interpretation.
- Acute pain: a problem the prospect thinks about 24/7, one that really hurts, not a dull or infrequent annoyance.
- The rule: the more specific and measurable the outcome, AND the more acute the pain, the more you can charge.

**Specificity equals tangibility.** When people pay, they need to know what they are getting in return. If they cannot picture the outcome clearly, they will not buy, and they certainly will not pay a lot. With a non-physical product there is nothing to hold or sit in (unlike a Tesla you can test-drive), so the outcome has to be made tangible through specificity, or the buyer feels like they are throwing money away.

**Acute-pain logic.** If the pain is acute enough, people will pay almost anything to fix it. An infrequent or dull pain commands almost nothing. (Nobody pays $10,000 to cure occasional hiccups; a man who has tried every doctor for daily knee pain will pay anything for the answer.) The more you want to charge, the more acute the pain must be.

**Part 2, length of time / time commitment.** Two questions: how long until they get the outcome (six weeks, six months), and how much of their own time it costs them along the way (no time, a couple hours a week, a couple hours a day). This is a price lever: people will invest a lot of time OR a lot of money, not both. The 30-pounds-in-a-year-of-brutal-workouts version sells for a few hundred dollars. The "take this pill, wake up 30 pounds lighter" version is worth billions. Less of their time = you can charge more.

**Part 3, medium of delivery.** What does the prospect's life look like during the program? Text-only, weekly calls, a PDF you hand off and never speak again, full done-for-you? The rule: the more you do for them and the less they lift a finger, the more you can charge.
- Caveat / common mistake: do not assume adding calls makes the offer more valuable. Calls cost the busy client time, which bleeds into Part 2 and forces a lower price. Take the time off them and put it on you, but do not create more work for them. Busy buyers want the result, not more meetings with you.

**Part 4, the guarantee (his personal favorite, the one most people get wrong).** Humans have loss aversion and skip guarantees because they fear getting taken advantage of. Yes, a few people will exploit it, but far fewer than you fear, and the extra sales a strong guarantee converts more than cover the loss. Most people are honest. Ignore the tough-guy "no refunds" gurus.
- The spectrum: strongest end is a satisfaction-based guarantee with a financial incentive ("day 1 or day 90, for any reason at all, no reason needed, I refund every penny plus $500"). Weakest end is "all sales final, buyer beware," which is only acceptable on low-price items. He always leans toward the strong end with higher-ticket coaching; close rates go way up.
- Make it memorable: on the sales call, talk about the guarantee a lot, give it a funny name, and put a little performance around it (his fitness guarantee was the "Ironclad Chad Dad Guarantee," with his partner playfully correcting the name). People remember it.
- Why it works: reverse the risk. Take it off the prospect and put it on you. People will pay more when they feel secure doing so.

**Part 5, price (dictated by the other four).** Price flows from outcome size, pain acuteness, time cost, delivery, and guarantee. Starting rules:
- "Charge what you think is fair, plus a thousand dollars." We undervalue ourselves as a species (imposter syndrome), so we underprice.
- Beginner coaching ballpark: a few thousand dollars for a few months. Make it worth your time.
- Higher if the pain is specific and acute. Lower if it costs them a lot of time. Higher if the guarantee is great.
- You will not nail the number by thinking. You find it by validating, testing, and seeing what sells.

**The master rule: validate BEFORE you build.** Do not build the course, the website, the 100-email sequence, the PDFs, the video modules, or buy software before you have sold the offer at least once. The whole point of the five-step framework is to give you something simple to pitch fast. Pitch it; if it sells, then figure out how to deliver. Spending three months building a product around an unvalidated offer is the exact failure mode this prevents.

## Do's and don'ts (the closing checklist)
- Do not solve ambiguous problems ("make you better/healthier/stronger"). Solve concrete measurable ones ("add 100 lbs to your bench," "get you to 12% body fat," "make you $10,000").
- Do not solve minor problems (a morning routine, 20 minutes back in your day). Big acute problems = big pricing ("quit porn forever," "save your marriage").
- Do not build the program before the offer is validated.
- Do not sign clients you do not believe can succeed. Qualify and judge them as hard as they judge you. A bad-fit failure makes you look like a fool and risks refunds and reputation.
- Do not sell what you cannot deliver. If unsure, lean on guarantees, or work free / discounted / on affiliate terms until you have a track record, then raise prices.
- Do not be afraid to refund. Do not run high-ticket offers without enough cash to refund any client on the spot. (He refunded an $8,000/month ghostwriting client who became a bad fit; peace of mind beat the money.)
- Do not neglect your winners. Winner clients should take more of your time than losers. Most coaches get this backwards because of sunk-cost bias; one rock-star client drives more referral business than ten losers.
- Do not make it complicated. A prospect should understand all five elements in two sentences or less. If you are giving long-winded explanations, you have not finished simplifying.
- Do your high-ticket offer FIRST, not last. Starting with a $30 ebook is more work, sells for less, still needs validation plus copy plus a platform plus affiliates, and you must sell a hundred of them to match one high-ticket client. Validate the high-ticket offer, take what you learn from real clients, then package that into the course or ebook to scale.
- Never stop upskilling. What you can charge is tied to the size of the problems you can solve, which is tied to your skill set. Add skills (he later learned ads, websites), solve bigger problems, charge more.

## Hook/thread/offer patterns (KJV-discernment brand-adapted where natural)

Adam does not sell coaching, but the five-part skeleton maps cleanly onto a Vault upgrade, a PDF product, a paid Substack tier, or a fundraiser ask. The lever logic (specific + acute + low time + strong guarantee = higher price and higher conversion) is the durable part.

Five-part offer applied to a Dead Hidden / Biblical Man digital product:
1. Big outcome (specific, measurable, acute): "In 30 days you will read the King James with your sons and not flinch when they ask the hard questions." Not "grow spiritually." Measurable: you sit down, you open it, you lead. Acute: a father who knows he is failing here thinks about it every night.
2. Time: "It is built to read in one sitting. One evening. Not a 12-week curriculum you will abandon by week two." Low time cost = higher willingness to pay.
3. Medium of delivery: "A PDF that tells you exactly what to do. No course login. No homework you have to invent." Done-for-the-reader thinking, not "go figure it out."
4. Guarantee: name it and reverse the risk. "The Plain Bible Guarantee. Read it. If it does not put the Book back in your hands, reply to any email and I refund every cent. No reason needed." Strong end of the spectrum, memorable name.
5. Price: charge what feels fair, then nudge up. Do not underprice the work because of false humility.

Hook patterns that carry the same specific/acute logic into Adam's voice (staccato, KJV-discernment):
- "Most men cannot name the sin that runs their house. You will. By Friday."
- "I won't make you a better father. I'll make your kids run to the door when you come home."
- "You don't need another sermon. You need the exact words to say when your boy asks why God let it happen."
- "Ambiguous repentance is no repentance. Name the thing. Today."

The same blade cuts at counterfeit teachers: a vague promise ("level up your walk") is the offer of a hireling. Scripture deals in measurable fruit. "By their fruits ye shall know them" (Matthew 7:20, KJV). Specific outcome, measurable fruit, acute pain. The discernment frame and the offer frame are the same frame.

## Numbers and proof
- The framework has exactly five parts.
- Validate by selling at least once before building.
- Pricing rule of thumb: "charge what you think is fair, plus a thousand dollars."
- Beginner coaching ballpark: a few thousand dollars for a few months.
- Strongest guarantee example: refund every penny "plus five hundred dollars," any reason, day 1 through day 90.
- Validated offer examples he and his clients have actually sold:
  - Dating coach: 10 dates with 10 great women, 120 days, around-the-clock text plus calls, full refund guarantee. Price $3,000.
  - Fitness coach: lose 30 pounds, 150 days (five months), around-the-clock text plus calls, done-for-you meal plan, full refund within 150 days. Price $4,000.
  - Better-dad ebook: kids run into your arms, six-month self-run program, ebook only (reader does all the work), refund guarantee. Price $20 (weak measurability, weak pain framing, long time, no done-for-you = low price, and it shows).
  - Business coach: "I will 3x your current monthly online income," 120 days, one-on-one chat plus calls, guarantee = coach you until you hit it plus 30-day any-reason refund. Price $10,000 (the most expensive; literal measurable ROI, acute pain, strong guarantee).
- Ebook math against high-ticket: you may sell a $30 ebook instead of a $3,000 client, meaning you must sell a hundred ebooks to match one client, and selling a hundred of anything is hard.
- AOP/Harrison consulting pitch: they help clients build and validate an offer in "10 percent of the time," compressing roughly two years of solo work into a few months.

## Brand application (Adam's voice)
- Adam's revenue is writing, not coaching, so this is a product/offer-design lens, not a pivot. Use it for Vault upgrades, PDF launches, paid-tier pitches, and fundraiser asks.
- Kill vague product promises. "Deepen your walk" is the ebook that sells for $20. Name a specific, measurable, acute outcome in the buyer's own pain.
- Price lever discipline: lower the reader's time cost (one-sitting read, done-for-them clarity), strengthen the guarantee, and you earn the right to charge more without apology.
- Reverse the risk publicly. A named, generous refund guarantee on a digital product converts more than the rare abuser costs. Refunds and public discounts still require Adam's explicit approval (Support-Commerce rule), so frame the guarantee, get the green light, then ship it.
- Validate before building: pitch a product in a Note or email and watch the response before producing the full PDF or course. One sold copy beats three weeks of building the wrong thing.
- High-ticket first principle, Adam-flavored: the premium Vault / cohort / signed product earns more per buyer than chasing a hundred $9 sales. Build and validate the premium thing, then atomize down into cheaper assets.
- The discernment crossover is the on-brand payload: counterfeit teachers sell ambiguous outcomes; Scripture deals in measurable fruit. Use the offer logic to expose hirelings and to make Adam's own offers honest at the same time.
