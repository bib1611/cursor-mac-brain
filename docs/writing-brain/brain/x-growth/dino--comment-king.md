# Comment King — Advanced Reply and Comment Strategy (Dino)

## Source
- File: `transcripts/HowtoBeACommentKing.txt`
- Teacher: Dino (Art of Purpose), Creation 24/7 / Masterclass 24/7
- Topic: turning comments and replies into a growth, connection, and content engine
- Type: transcript (masterclass module, ~1 hour)

## Core thesis
Comments are not throwaway. A great comment is a standalone post placed on someone else's distribution, fast, before the crowd. Stop spraying 1,000 soulless AI replies. Build a curated engagement machine, recycle your best old content as comments, and train your own audience to keep talking to you. Every comment should aim to pull three replies back.

## Key frameworks / rules

- **Kill the For You page for engagement.** It is not quick, not people you have a relationship with, not people you want one with. Nothing strategic happens there. Do not hunt comments from it.
- **Curate the platform first (notification muting).** On the phone only: Settings > Notifications > filter menu. Mute notifications from people you do not follow, people who do not follow you, new accounts, no-default-profile accounts. Kill text and email notifications. Removes "99% of the notifications I receive." A clean notification feed becomes a clean engagement queue. Bigger account = mute harder.
- **Use XPro lists, not the feed.** Build a VIP list of high-quality accounts you want to build connections with. XPro shows you their latest moves first, so you can reply fast and reply only where it counts. Fall-through order when the VIP list is quiet: VIP list, then cleaned notifications (e.g. "new post" alert from a big account), then your Following feed chronologically. For You is the last resort.
- **Subscribe to the big accounts you plan to engage, and turn on their notifications.** Highest-ROI activity on X. Subscribers get shown first in comment sections, and big creators (Dino, and per Dino, Musk) prioritize replies from their subscribers. The point is not the subscriber-only content. The point is comment-section priority and access.
- **Speed window.** Comment as fast as possible. Inside ~10 minutes, definitely inside ~30, unless the post is going mega-viral. Early high-quality comments get seen by the most people.
- **The 3-reply goal.** Every comment you leave should aim to pull three replies back from that comment. Dino's claim: a comment that draws more than three replies can itself get monetized. Whether or not the monetization holds, the rule forces conversation-starting comments over agreement.
- **The standalone-comment rule.** Every comment, especially early on, should be good enough to be its own post, or at least a writing prompt. Not "fire emoji," not "let's go." If it cannot stand alone, do not leave it. Dino's best comments are literally old posts he reused.
- **The self-reply test (Il Scholar's rule).** Before you post a comment, ask: could I reply to my own comment with something of value? If not, neither can anyone else, and you have left an "extended like." A bare agreement is a thumbs-up with extra words.
- **Recycle your own validated content as comments.** Keep every old post and meme in a searchable archive (Apple Notes searches text and images). When a post or meme on the timeline triggers a memory, search a keyword, pull the old validated piece, and drop it as a comment before the crowd arrives. Dino's Thomas Sowell quote from three years prior, reused as a comment, got a Musk repost and thousands of followers.
- **Voice-to-text for reply volume.** Dictate replies to move faster through a comment section. Speed is the whole game.
- **Comment volume target.** Not 1,000 a day. Roughly 20 to 30 really good ones. Quality and speed over spray. (Matches the brain's existing "no spray" flag.)

## Highlighting / training your own comment section

- **Do not reply to every comment.** Replying to all of them can lower your own tweet-cred score. Highlight 5 to 10.
- **Who to highlight, in order:** your subscribers first; bigger blue-check / high-tweet-cred accounts; people being disagreeable (great for engagement and pulls readers deeper into the thread); and "fawning" accounts who post your own meme or praise you (free social proof). Musk-tier replies get highlighted on sight as social proof.
- **Training by behaviorism.** A like or a reply back trains a reader to engage again. Behaviorism tenet: you do not need big rewards, just small ones, frequently. A like costs five seconds and nothing. Ignore your comment section and you teach your fans they are not important, and they stop coming back. Most accounts throw scheduled tweets and never reply to their own fans. That is the leak.
- **Steer both sides into a fight.** Pull a disagreeable comment up, answer it with a challenge ("make it make sense"), and let supporters and critics battle it out in your replies. A post that generates conversation in its own comment section is exactly what the algorithm rewards.
- **Leave gold deep in a shallow post.** Sometimes post something superficial, then drop a deep, real take buried in your own comments. The shallow post pulls the click, the buried gold rewards the dweller and feeds the thread.
- **Reward quote-tweeters.** When a bigger account quote-tweets you, reply on the quote itself (sometimes retweet it) to condition them to keep doing it.

## Comments-to-content loop

- **Browse to engage AND to harvest ideas at the same time.** While replying, bookmark reusable material into a categorized bank: affiliate-sale posts, account-definition tweets, great threads, great memes, base posts, auto-DM campaigns. (Matches the brain's existing bookmark-bank rule, extends the categories.)
- **Turn winning comments into posts, and old posts into comments.** The loop runs both directions. A comment that performed is a post waiting to be written. An old post that fits the moment is a comment waiting to be reused.

## Hook / reply patterns (brand-adapted)

- **Conversation-starting reply, not agreement.** Weak: "Amen." "Big win for the faith." Strong reply that pulls three back: agree, then take it one step further with a specific critique or a "what they should do instead." Brand: under a porn-to-faith testimony post, "Repentance is good. But a testimony without the practice is still a performance. Where is the fruit?" That invites the next reply.
- **The reuse-an-old-post reply.** When a timeline post echoes something you already wrote, drop your validated line as the comment. Brand: someone posts about a softened modern translation; you reply with your old standalone, "Your NIV deleted Acts 8:37. They told you the originals were lost. They lied."
- **The disagreeable-bait highlight.** Post a likable line as bait, then pull the sharpest objector up and challenge it. Brand bait: "It's always the steadiest men I know who read the King James out loud." Atheists and modern-version defenders argue, you steer.
- **The "name it, I'll show it" reply prompt.** Brand: "Drop the verse your pastor skipped. I'll tell you why he skipped it." Pulls replies, each one feeds a future post.

## Numbers and proof
- Goal: every comment pulls "three people to reply." Comments drawing more than three replies "get monetized," per Dino.
- Reply speed window: under ~10 minutes, ~30 max, unless mega-viral.
- Comment volume: ~20 to 30 strong comments a day, not 1,000.
- Notification muting removes "99% of the notifications that I receive."
- Subscribed: Dino subscribes to ~10 accounts, has over 200 subscribers himself. $2 to sub to Musk in his example, $4 to sub to Dino.
- Musk has liked his posts "eight or nine times," replied a few times, retweeted him; each touch brings "thousands and thousands" of followers. Called "the highest ROI activity you can do on X."
- Reused 3-year-old Thomas Sowell post as a comment, got Musk repost, "thousands of followers."
- One viral post had 10,000 comments, another 12,000; subscribers shown at the top.
- A subscriber comment example: "100,000 impressions," and "you can get to your five million impressions quickly doing this."
- Highlight 5 to 10 commenters, not all (replying to all can lower tweet-cred).
- Live demo bait post drew ~54 comments in ~83 minutes off ~15 minutes of active reply work, then "piece out after 15 minutes."

## Brand application (Adam's voice)
You already write comments that could stand alone. Most men do not. They drop "Amen" and wonder why nobody follows.

Build the machine.

Make a VIP list. Discernment accounts. KJV men. Pastors worth arguing with. Reply to them first, fast, before the crowd.

Mute the noise. Kill notifications from drive-by accounts. Your notification feed is a hunting ground, not a junk drawer.

Keep every old post in Apple Notes. When a man posts about his dead translation, search "Acts 8:37" and drop the line you already wrote. Validated content. Reused on his distribution.

Never leave an extended like. Before you hit reply, ask: could I answer my own comment? If not, kill it.

Train your own readers. A like back costs you five seconds. Ignore them and you teach them they do not matter. Highlight the subscribers, the big accounts, the man swinging at you. Let the modern-version crowd fight your tribe in your replies. That is the conversation the machine pays for.

Twenty good comments. Not a thousand dead ones.
