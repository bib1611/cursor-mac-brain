# Decoding the X Algorithm (distilled)

## Source
- File: `transcripts/Decoding the X Algorithm.txt`
- Teacher: not named (Masterclass 24/7 presenter, references Thomas Travers and "Tiwa Savage"-style peers in the room)
- Topic: how the X feed ranks posts, what drives reach, first-hour mechanics, network-building for compounding visibility
- Type: transcript

## Core thesis
X is run by Grok, which scores every post in isolation by the predicted chance a viewer takes a deep action on it. Likes barely count. Replies, reply chains, reposts, bookmarks, profile clicks, and dwell time carry the weight, and the first 30 to 60 minutes with your own followers decide whether Grok pushes the post out of network where virality actually happens.

## Key frameworks / rules

- **Grok is the gatekeeper, and it must classify your topic.** If Grok cannot pin down what the post is about, it cannot decide who to show it to, and reach collapses. Name the subject plainly. "Top 10 history lessons that will change your life" is a weak vague signal. "Haudenosaunee Dichi turned banking wealth into public memory" is a strong specific signal Grok can route. Specificity is the single most important lever in this version of the algorithm.

- **Original beats borrowed.** If other accounts are already saying the same thing, Grok rates your post lower. Use original examples, original context, original images and video. Recycled phrasing gets buried.

- **The follower-validation gate (the new early mechanic).** On posting, the post is shown first to a small slice of your followers, stated as five to 15 percent. If those followers interact, Grok reads it as positive and widens distribution, eventually out of network. If they ignore it, Grok kills it as "not interesting."

- **Non-follower interaction early can HURT you.** Early on, if non-followers interact but do not follow, that can create a negative score. This is an anti-bought-engagement guard against purchased retweets. Counterintuitive but stated plainly: followers validate, non-followers scale later.

- **Interactor reputation is weighted.** Every account has a rating. A subject-matter expert or influencer interacting gives near full marks. A 1-to-2-follower account interacting gives reduced marks and can even draw a penalty if that account has been penalized before. Bot followers actively kill reach because they never interact. "More bots is going to kill your reach."

- **The reply-chain multiplier.** A back-and-forth chain (you reply, author replies to your reply, others chime in) can score up to 75x. This is the strongest single mechanic named in the talk. Write posts and replies engineered to pull a reply back, which starts a chain.

- **Out-of-network penalty scaling.** When out-of-network people interact, the interaction can be discounted at 0.7x, 0.8x, or 0.9x depending on distance from your network and the interactor's rating.

- **The self-competition / cannibalization rule.** Posting 20 to 30 times a day makes your own posts compete against each other, because Grok wants to show varied authors. Your reach gets split across your own posts so nothing takes off. Rule of thumb given: one post every three to four hours.

- **Six-hour news-cycle ceiling.** Even a great post can die if posted into a hot news cycle or against other strong posts at the same minute. Not all good posts go viral. Timing and competition decide survival as much as quality.

- **Filtration kills before ranking.** Posts get cut early for duplicates, already-seen, blocked authors, muted keywords, and especially paywalled links (a link to content behind a pay gate is suppressed). Reach failures are often a filter failure, not a quality failure.

- **The quote-as-duplicate effect.** When someone quotes your post, your post now lives in two places. Impressions still credit you (X monetizes on impressions, so pay is unaffected), but interactions can pool on the quote instead of your original. High impressions with low interactions usually means someone quoted you and is absorbing the engagement.

- **Emojis are dead weight to the machine.** Grok ignores emojis entirely. No value added, no ranking benefit. (Cited as repeated publicly by "Yewa Nikita / Nikita.")

- **Premium for early follower visibility.** X Premium Plus gives roughly 4x follower visibility in the early window. Without it, no early-visibility boost. Worth holding a paid account just for the first-hour push.

- **Reposts vs replies confusion to avoid.** The presenter mis-speaks once ("don't give too much emphasis to replies") but the table he is reading shows reposts, replies, bookmarks, profile clicks, and read-time as the very favorable signals and likes as low (0.5x to 1x, can be under 1x). Treat replies, reposts, bookmarks, profile clicks, and dwell as the targets. Likes are the floor.

## Hook or thread patterns

This source is mechanics, not hook copy. The one transferable copy rule is the **specificity-for-classification rule**: every post should let Grok name the exact subject in one read.

- Vague (suppressed): "Here are the lessons that will change your faith."
- Specific (routed): brand-adapted, "Bishop Ussher dated creation to 4004 BC, and your study Bible quietly deleted it."
- The dwell-and-reply engineering question to ask before posting, verbatim from the talk: "Would I reply to this if I saw it from somebody else? Or would I repost it? Or would I just like it?" If the honest answer is "just like it," rewrite.

- **The cross-niche relevance bridge (a topic-expansion move).** Take your core subject and attach a second audience's angle without leaving your box, so more interest-communities can be classified into the post. Example given: a finance writer asking "what was the impact of the Medici banking empire on civilization" pulls finance and cultural accounts. Brand-adapted: take a KJV discernment point and bolt on a masculinity or fatherhood angle, "the same modern Bibles that soften hell also soften what a man owes his house," to draw both the discernment crowd and the men's crowd into one classifiable post.

## Brand application

- Before any tweet, run the one-read test: can Grok name the exact subject? If the line is "the church is compromised," it is vague and dies. "Your ESV deletes Acts 8:37" is specific and routes. Name the translation, the verse, the chapter, the word. Discernment is a specificity niche by nature, so this algorithm favors how Adam already thinks.
- Treat the first hour as the whole game. Post when his 52.9K followers are most awake (pull the active-hours data from analytics), then sit in the replies for 60 minutes answering every reply with a real sentence that earns a reply back. The reply chain is the 75x lever and Adam's confrontational style is built to provoke chains.
- Cap volume. Adam's instinct under ADHD pressure is to flood the timeline. One strong post every three to four hours beats ten that cannibalize each other.
- Build the discernment pod on purpose. Five to ten KJV / masculinity / Reformed-adjacent accounts who reliably reply to each other in the first hour. The presenter's whole closing point is that today's small friends become tomorrow's big accounts, and every share later compounds. This is a year-long deposit, not a hack.
- Kill emojis in posts. They do nothing for reach and they soften the voice anyway.
- Keep the Substack link out of the body. A paywalled link in the body gets the post filtered. Link in the self-reply, which Adam already does.
- For Follow Me (the darker lane), the dwell mechanic rewards paced one-idea-per-line confession. Long, slow, staccato scar-tissue posts hold read-time, which the table scores as very favorable.
