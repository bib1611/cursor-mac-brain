# MC26 — Writing Personas

## Source
- File: `MC26 Writing Personas (1).txt`
- Teacher: Robert Bortins (X: @TheRobertBShow)
- Topic: Building reader personas so you write to one real person, not an abstraction.

## Core thesis
Write to everyone and you write to no one. A persona is a detailed, semi-fictional portrait of one ideal reader, built from real people, that lets you apply unchanging truth in the exact way one specific person can receive it. Specificity is the strength, not the limit.

## Key frameworks / rules

### The Four-Part Persona Framework
1. **External Reality** — demographics and circumstances. Be specific ("35-50," not "adults"): age, gender, location, education, profession, income, marital status, children's ages, season of life, time and money constraints, recent major life changes, daily schedule.
2. **Internal Reality** — psychographics and values. What they believe about themselves and the world, what they value most, worldview, trusted authorities, analytical vs. intuitive, data vs. stories, risk-taker vs. security-seeker, skeptical vs. trusting, where they get information, who else they follow.
3. **The Problem** — three layers:
   - Surface problem (the obvious one, what made them go looking).
   - Deeper problem (the real issue, the fear under it, the lie they believe that keeps it alive, the emotional cost).
   - The stakes (what happens if it stays unsolved, what they lose now, the future they fear).
   - Their current narrative (how they explain the problem to themselves, the excuses, why they feel hopeless).
4. **The Aspiration** — practical goal (measurable outcome), identity goal (who they want to become, how they want to be seen), the vision (ideal future, typical day in it), and the why behind the why (deeper longing, legacy).

### Build process
- **Step 1 (20 min):** Start with real people. List 5-10 who need your work, find shared struggles and aspirations, pick 2-3 who best fit, build a composite. Never invent from thin air.
- **Step 2 (5 min):** Name the persona and attach a face. Then you ask "Will Sarah get this?" not "Will readers get this?"
- **Step 3 (30-45 min):** Write a 1-2 page profile in five sections: Who they are, What they value, What they're struggling with, What they're striving for, How your work serves them.
- **Step 4 (ongoing):** Test as you write. Would [Name] understand this? Care about this? Is this example relevant to their life? Does it match their values?

### Five mistakes to avoid
1. Too broad ("anyone who wants to be healthier").
2. Confusing the persona with yourself. They are usually one step behind you, or facing what you already beat.
3. Multiple personas per work. One book = one primary persona. Others read it; you do not write to them.
4. Too perfect. Real people contradict themselves (want to lose weight, love dessert). Keep the contradiction.
5. Ignoring objections. Build in the reasons they resist so you can answer them before they raise them.

### Writing with the persona (advanced)
- **Vocabulary/tone:** use their language, match their expertise level, examples from their world, cut jargon they will not know.
- **Story selection:** pick stories from their life stage and cultural touchstones; drop examples that alienate.
- **Depth:** give exactly enough to act, no more. Do not oversimplify the sophisticated or bury the beginner.
- **Format:** match how they consume (summary vs. deep dive, step-by-step vs. principle, many examples vs. plain instruction).

## Hook / thread / offer patterns
- **Specificity hook:** lead with the exact reader, not the broad claim. Not "for men who want to grow." Instead: "For the 41-year-old who teaches Sunday School, secretly doubts he's saved, and has never told his wife." Naming the hidden struggle out loud is the hook.
- **Surface-then-deeper move (thread / post structure):** open on the surface problem the reader admits to, then name the deeper problem and the lie underneath. The reader feels seen at the line they did not expect you to know. This is the scar-tissue mechanic for Follow Me.
- **Objection-baked-in reply:** when answering a reader, voice their doubt first ("You're thinking this is just discipline talk"), then break the lie. Pre-empting resistance reads as honesty, not selling.
- **Offer framing:** frame the product as the bridge from current narrative to the aspiration. Section 5 of the profile ("How your work serves them") is the offer copy: what specific help, what transformation, why your approach fits this exact person.
- **One-reader test for KJV-discernment work:** before publishing, ask whether the named persona (e.g., the man being deceived by familiar spirits but who would never use that phrase) would recognize his own life in the first three lines.

## Numbers and proof
- "If you're speaking to everyone, you're speaking to no one."
- Be specific: write "35-50," not "adults."
- Persona profile is **1-2 pages**, five sections.
- Time blocks: real-people pass 20 min, naming 5 min, profile 30-45 min, full assignment 60-90 min, reader test 15 min.
- Base the composite on **5-10 real people**, then choose **2-3** who best represent the ideal reader.
- One work = **one** primary persona.

## Brand application (Adam's voice)
- Paul wrote differently to the Corinthians than to Philemon. Same truth. Different wound. Write the same way. KJV does not change; the man you hand it to does.
- The Biblical Man persona is not "Christian men." It is one man, named, with a specific secret. Discernment content lands when it diagnoses a struggle he has never said out loud.
- Follow Me runs on scar tissue. The persona's deeper problem and the lie they believe is your raw material. Write from the wound the reader hides, not the topic.
- Classical and biblical backing for the method: Aristotle's Rhetoric (know their beliefs, their feelings, their character); Paul reasoned from Scripture to Jews and quoted poets to Greeks; C.S. Lewis wrote Narnia for children, Mere Christianity for intellectuals, A Grief Observed for the grieving. Same convictions, fitted method.
- Frame: persona work is incarnation, not manipulation. God did not send a memo; He became one of us, spoke our language, walked in our shoes. Writing to one real reader is the same move on a small scale.
- Keep the persona printed and visible. Begin each writing session by reading it. Ask: am I serving this person well with what I am writing.
